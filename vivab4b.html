250 High-Yield Viva Questions: Protein Metabolism & Pathologies

Q1. Are amino acids stored in the body for future use like fats and carbohydrates?
A: No. Unlike fats and carbohydrates, no protein exists whose sole function is to maintain a supply of amino acids for future use. Any amino acids in excess of the biosynthetic needs of the cell are rapidly degraded.
💡 Mnemonic: No AA ATM (No amino acid storage machine).
Q2. What are the three sources that supply the amino acid pool?
A: 1) Amino acids provided by the continuous degradation of body proteins, 2) amino acids derived from dietary protein, and 3) the de novo synthesis of nonessential amino acids from simple intermediates of metabolism.
💡 Mnemonic: The Pool is fed by Diet, Degradation, and De novo synthesis.
Q3. What are the three routes that deplete the amino acid pool?
A: 1) Synthesis of new body protein, 2) consumption as precursors to synthesize essential nitrogen-containing small molecules (like purines, pyrimidines, heme), and 3) conversion of carbon skeletons to glucose, glycogen, fatty acids, ketone bodies, or oxidation to CO2 and water.
Q4. What exactly defines a state of "nitrogen balance"?
A: In healthy, well-fed individuals, the input of amino acids to the pool is exactly balanced by the output. The total amount of amino acids in the pool remains constant, meaning the individual is in a steady state known as nitrogen balance.
💡 Mnemonic: Nitrogen Balance = Input matches Output.
Q5. What is the approximate size of the amino acid pool compared to total body protein?
A: The free amino acid pool is very small, comprising only about 90 to 100 grams of amino acids, compared to the massive amount of protein in the body (about 12 kilograms in a typical 70-kg man).
Q6. Define the concept of "protein turnover".
A: Protein turnover is the simultaneous, constant synthesis and degradation of body proteins, which permits the removal of abnormal or unneeded proteins. In healthy adults, 300 to 400 grams of body protein are hydrolyzed and resynthesized every single day.
💡 Mnemonic: Turnover = Out with the old, in with the new.
Q7. What are the two major, distinct enzyme systems responsible for degrading damaged or unneeded proteins in the cell?
A: 1) The ATP-dependent ubiquitin-proteasome system located in the cytosol, and 2) the ATP-independent degradative enzyme system found within the lysosomes (acid hydrolases).
💡 Mnemonic: Proteasome uses power (ATP), Lysosome uses acid.
Q8. What specific types of proteins are targeted by the proteasome versus the lysosome?
A: Proteasomes primarily degrade endogenous proteins (proteins that were synthesized internally within the cell). Lysosomal enzymes primarily degrade extracellular proteins, such as plasma proteins taken into the cell by endocytosis, and cell-surface membrane proteins.
💡 Mnemonic: Endo goes to Proteasome; Exo goes to Lysosome.
Q9. Describe the exact biochemical mechanism of protein ubiquitination.
A: Proteins selected for degradation are covalently tagged with ubiquitin, a small globular protein. Ubiquitination occurs through the linkage of the alpha-carboxyl group of the C-terminal glycine of ubiquitin to the epsilon-amino group of a lysine residue on the target protein via a three-step, ATP-dependent process.
Q10. How does the proteasome physically process the ubiquitin-tagged target proteins?
A: The large, barrel-shaped macromolecular proteasome complex recognizes the polyubiquitin chain, unfolds the target protein, deubiquitinates it (recycling the ubiquitin), and cuts the protein into peptide fragments, which are then further degraded to free amino acids in the cytosol.
Q11. How does the specific N-terminal amino acid radically influence a protein's intracellular half-life?
A: The half-life is highly dependent on the N-terminal residue. For example, proteins with serine at the N-terminus are long-lived (half-life over 20 hours). In contrast, proteins with aspartate at the N-terminus are rapidly degraded (half-life of only 3 minutes).
Q12. What are PEST sequences and what is their clinical/biochemical significance?
A: PEST sequences are regions in a protein rich in Proline, Glutamate, Serine, and Threonine. Proteins containing PEST sequences are rapidly degraded by cellular systems and therefore exhibit very short intracellular half-lives.
💡 Mnemonic: PEST proteins get exterminated quickly!
Q13. How much protein is typically consumed daily in the American diet?
A: Most nitrogen in the diet is consumed as protein, typically amounting to 70 to 100 grams per day.
Q14. Which three specific organs produce the proteolytic enzymes responsible for degrading large dietary proteins into absorbable units?
A: The stomach, the pancreas, and the small intestine.
Q15. What are the two precise functions of hydrochloric acid (HCl) secreted by gastric parietal cells?
A: Stomach acid (pH 2 to 3) is too dilute to directly hydrolyze peptide bonds. Instead, it functions to 1) kill ingested bacteria, and 2) denature dietary proteins, unfolding them to make them more susceptible to subsequent hydrolysis by proteases.
💡 Mnemonic: Acid Denatures and Destroys (bacteria).
Q16. How is the proenzyme pepsinogen activated to functional pepsin in the stomach?
A: Pepsinogen is secreted by the chief cells as an inactive zymogen containing extra amino acids that prevent catalytic activity. It is activated to pepsin either by the acidic environment (HCl) or autocatalytically by other pepsin molecules that have already been activated.
Q17. What two polypeptide hormones mediate the release and activation of pancreatic zymogens into the small intestine?
A: Cholecystokinin and secretin, which are hormones of the digestive tract.
💡 Mnemonic: CCK and Secretin Secrete the pancreatic juice.
Q18. Explain the critical master role of enteropeptidase in intestinal protein digestion.
A: Enteropeptidase (formerly enterokinase), synthesized by and present on the luminal surface of intestinal mucosal cells, converts the pancreatic zymogen trypsinogen to active trypsin by removing a hexapeptide. Trypsin then acts as the common activator for all other pancreatic zymogens in a massive proteolytic cascade.
💡 Mnemonic: Trypsin Triggers The rest.
Q19. What is the precise cleavage specificity of the pancreatic enzyme trypsin?
A: Trypsin is a highly specific endopeptidase that cleaves peptide bonds exclusively when the carbonyl group of the peptide bond is contributed by the basic amino acids arginine or lysine.
Q20. What clinical signs present in individuals with a severe deficiency in pancreatic secretion (e.g., chronic pancreatitis, surgical removal, or cystic fibrosis)?
A: Digestion and absorption of fat and protein become incomplete, resulting in the abnormal appearance of lipids (steatorrhea) and undigested protein in the feces.
💡 Mnemonic: Steatorrhea = Stool full of fat and protein.
Q21. What is the exact biochemical and immunological trigger for Celiac disease (celiac sprue)?
A: It is a severe disease of malabsorption resulting from immune-mediated damage to the small intestine in response to the ingestion of gluten (specifically the protein gliadin produced from gluten) found in wheat, barley, and rye.
Q22. What is the specific function of intestinal aminopeptidase on the luminal surface?
A: It is an exopeptidase that repeatedly cleaves the N-terminal residue from oligopeptides to produce even smaller peptides and free amino acids ready for cellular absorption.
Q23. Detail the specific transport systems used by enterocytes to absorb the varied products of protein digestion.
A: Free amino acids are taken up by a Na+-linked secondary transport system on the apical membrane. Di- and tri-peptides, however, are taken up by a H+-linked transport system, and are subsequently hydrolyzed completely to free amino acids in the cytosol before being released into the portal system by facilitated diffusion.
💡 Mnemonic: Na+ pulls free AAs; H+ hauls the peptides.
Q24. Does the liver directly metabolize every single amino acid absorbed into the portal vein?
A: No. Branched-chain amino acids (BCAAs: leucine, isoleucine, valine) are not metabolized by the liver; instead, they are sent from the liver primarily to peripheral tissues like muscle via the systemic circulation.
💡 Mnemonic: BCAAs Bypass the liver for the Biceps.
Q25. What drives the active transport systems required to move amino acids from extracellular fluid (low concentration) into cells (high concentration)?
A: The transport systems are active and are driven directly or indirectly by the hydrolysis of ATP to maintain the steep concentration gradient.
Q26. What specific amino acid transport system is genetically defective in Cystinuria?
A: The common carrier system in the proximal tubule of the kidney and the small intestine responsible for the uptake of cystine and the dibasic amino acids ornithine, arginine, and lysine (represented as "COAL").
💡 Mnemonic: COAL (Cystine, Ornithine, Arginine, Lysine).
Q27. What are the clinical consequences, frequency, and basic treatments for Cystinuria?
A: Occurring in 1 in 7,000 individuals (most common genetic error of AA transport), the inability to reabsorb cystine leads to its massive accumulation and precipitation in the urinary tract, forming kidney stones (calculi) that can cause blockages. Oral hydration is a critical part of treatment.
Q28. A defect in the transport of tryptophan and other neutral amino acids results in what rare disorder?
A: Hartnup disorder, which presents clinically with pellagra-like dermatologic and severe neurologic symptoms due to a lack of tryptophan to make niacin.
Q29. What is the obligatory, protective first step in the catabolism of most amino acids to produce energy?
A: The removal of the alpha-amino group, which safely unlocks the carbon skeleton for oxidative breakdown while preventing toxic ammonia accumulation.
💡 Mnemonic: Transamination Takes off the nitrogen safely.
Q30. Describe the general biochemical reaction of transamination.
A: It is the transfer of an alpha-amino group from an amino acid directly to alpha-ketoglutarate, producing a new alpha-keto acid (derived from the original amino acid) and converting the alpha-ketoglutarate into glutamate.
Q31. Which two amino acids are the strict exceptions that NEVER participate in transamination at any point in their catabolism?
A: Lysine and threonine. They lose their alpha-amino groups strictly by direct deamination.
💡 Mnemonic: Losers and Thinkers don't Transaminate.
Q32. What specific transamination reaction is catalyzed by Alanine Aminotransferase (ALT)?
A: ALT catalyzes the reversible transfer of the amino group of alanine to alpha-ketoglutarate, resulting in the formation of pyruvate (the carbon skeleton of alanine) and glutamate. It acts as a collector of nitrogen from alanine.
💡 Mnemonic: ALanine makes pyruvATe via ALT.
Q33. How does Aspartate Aminotransferase (AST) deviate from the general rule of transaminases funnelling to glutamate?
A: During amino acid catabolism, AST uniquely transfers amino groups FROM glutamate TO oxaloacetate, forming aspartate. This aspartate is then directly used as a crucial source of nitrogen in the urea cycle.
💡 Mnemonic: ASpartate is Synthesized by AST.
Q34. Detail the precise mechanism of action for the coenzyme Pyridoxal Phosphate (PLP) in aminotransferase enzymes.
A: PLP (a derivative of vitamin B6) is covalently linked to the epsilon-amino group of a specific lysine at the active site. The enzyme transfers the amino group from the amino acid to the pyridoxal part to generate pyridoxamine phosphate. The pyridoxamine form then reacts with an alpha-keto acid to form a new amino acid, regenerating the original aldehyde form of PLP.
Q35. Why is the equilibrium constant of most transamination reactions near one (~1)?
A: This allows the reactions to function completely reversibly in both amino acid degradation (removing amino groups after a protein meal) and biosynthesis (adding amino groups to carbon skeletons when diet is inadequate).
Q36. What is the clinical diagnostic value of finding highly elevated ALT and AST in the blood plasma?
A: Aminotransferases are normally intracellular enzymes. Highly elevated plasma levels indicate severe physical trauma or a disease process causing cell lysis and necrosis (e.g., severe viral hepatitis, toxic injury, prolonged circulatory collapse, or myocardial infarction).
Q37. Between AST and ALT, which is more specific for liver disease and which is more sensitive?
A: ALT is more specific for liver disease, but AST is more sensitive because the liver contains much larger absolute amounts of AST.
💡 Mnemonic: L for Liver (ALT), S for Sensitive (AST).
Q38. What is the exact role of Glutamate Dehydrogenase in amino acid catabolism?
A: In contrast to transamination which only transfers groups, Glutamate Dehydrogenase catalyzes the rapid oxidative deamination of glutamate, resulting in the actual liberation of the amino group as free, toxic ammonia (NH3) and regenerating alpha-ketoglutarate.
💡 Mnemonic: GDH Gets rid of ammonia.
Q39. What is unique about the specific coenzyme requirements for Glutamate Dehydrogenase?
A: It is highly unusual because it can use EITHER NAD+ (used primarily for oxidative deamination, simultaneously losing ammonia and oxidizing the carbon skeleton) OR NADP+ (used primarily for reductive amination to synthesize amino acids).
💡 Mnemonic: NAD+ takes away, NADP+ puts back.
Q40. How is Glutamate Dehydrogenase allosterically regulated by cellular energy levels?
A: Guanosine triphosphate (GTP) is a strong allosteric inhibitor, whereas adenosine diphosphate (ADP) is an activator. Thus, when cell energy levels are low, amino acid degradation by glutamate dehydrogenase is high to facilitate energy production from carbon skeletons.
Q41. D-amino acids are not used in mammalian proteins. How are they metabolized if ingested from plant/microbial sources?
A: They are efficiently metabolized by D-Amino acid oxidase (DAO), an FAD-dependent peroxisomal enzyme in the kidney and liver, which catalyzes oxidative deamination to produce alpha-keto acids, ammonia, and hydrogen peroxide.
Q42. What clinical psychological condition is linked to increased activity of D-Amino acid oxidase (DAO) acting on D-serine?
A: Increased DAO activity degrades D-serine (which normally modulates NMDA-type glutamate receptors) and has been linked to an increased susceptibility to schizophrenia.
Q43. What is the primary mechanism for safely transporting ammonia from most peripheral tissues to the liver?
A: The enzyme glutamine synthetase combines toxic ammonia (NH3) with glutamate to form glutamine, a completely nontoxic transport form of ammonia, which is then transported in the blood to the liver.
💡 Mnemonic: Glutamine is the safe ammonia Taxi.
Q44. In the liver, what specific enzyme acts upon the transported glutamine?
A: Hepatic glutaminase cleaves the glutamine to produce glutamate and free ammonia, the latter immediately entering the urea cycle.
Q45. Describe the glucose-alanine cycle used primarily by muscle for ammonia transport.
A: In muscle, pyruvate (the end product of aerobic glycolysis) is transaminated to form alanine. Alanine is transported by the blood to the liver, where it is converted back to pyruvate by transamination. The liver uses this pyruvate for gluconeogenesis to synthesize glucose, which enters the blood and returns to the muscle.
💡 Mnemonic: Alanine = Muscle's ammonia Uber to the liver.
Q46. What percentage of the nitrogen-containing components of urine is accounted for by urea?
A: Urea accounts for about 90% of the nitrogen-containing components of urine, making it the major disposal form of amino groups.
Q47. What are the exact specific molecular sources of the two nitrogen atoms, the carbon, and the oxygen in a newly synthesized urea molecule?
A: One nitrogen is supplied by free ammonia, and the other nitrogen by the amino acid aspartate. The carbon and oxygen of urea are derived entirely from CO2.
💡 Mnemonic: Ammonia and Aspartate (A & A) give the N.
Q48. Which distinct intracellular compartments are involved in the reactions of the urea cycle?
A: The first two reactions leading to urea synthesis occur in the mitochondrial matrix, whereas the remaining cycle enzymes are located in the cytosol.
💡 Mnemonic: Mitochondria starts it, Cytosol finishes it.
Q49. What is the absolute rate-limiting and committed step of the urea cycle?
A: The formation of carbamoyl phosphate by the enzyme Carbamoyl Phosphate Synthetase I (CPS I), driven by the cleavage of two molecules of ATP.
💡 Mnemonic: CPS I is the Cycle's Controller.
Q50. What is the absolutely essential positive allosteric activator required by Carbamoyl Phosphate Synthetase I?
A: N-acetylglutamate. Without it, CPS I is completely inactive.
💡 Mnemonic: NAG Nags CPS I to work.
Q51. How does Carbamoyl Phosphate Synthetase I fundamentally differ from Carbamoyl Phosphate Synthetase II?
A: CPS I functions in the mitochondria for the urea cycle using free ammonia and requires N-acetylglutamate. CPS II participates in the biosynthesis of pyrimidines in the cytosol, does NOT require N-acetylglutamate, and uses glutamine as the nitrogen source.
Q52. What reaction is catalyzed by Ornithine Transcarbamoylase (OTC)?
A: OTC transfers the carbamoyl portion of carbamoyl phosphate to ornithine. The high-energy phosphate is released as Pi, and the product, citrulline, is transported out of the mitochondria into the cytosol.
💡 Mnemonic: OTC = Ornithine To Citrulline.
Q53. Why are the basic amino acids ornithine and citrulline never found incorporated into human cellular proteins?
A: They are not incorporated into cellular proteins because there are simply no DNA codons for these amino acids in the genetic code.
Q54. Detail the reaction catalyzed by Argininosuccinate Synthetase.
A: It combines citrulline with aspartate (providing the second urea nitrogen) to form argininosuccinate. This reaction is powerfully driven by the cleavage of a third molecule of ATP into adenosine monophosphate (AMP) and pyrophosphate.
💡 Mnemonic: ASS (Argininosuccinate Synthetase) Adds Aspartate.
Q55. What does the cleavage of argininosuccinate by Argininosuccinate Lyase strictly yield?
A: It yields arginine (the immediate precursor of urea) and fumarate.
💡 Mnemonic: ALF (Argininosuccinate Leaves Fumarate & Arginine).
Q56. How does the fumarate produced in the urea cycle act as a crucial link to gluconeogenesis and the TCA cycle?
A: Fumarate is hydrated to malate, providing a link to several pathways. Malate can be transported into the mitochondria, oxidized to oxaloacetate, and used for gluconeogenesis, or transaminated to aspartate to reenter the urea cycle.
Q57. What specific enzyme catalyzes the final release of urea, and in what organ is it exclusively active?
A: Arginase cleaves arginine to ornithine and urea. This occurs almost exclusively in the liver (other tissues can make arginine but cannot cleave it to urea).
💡 Mnemonic: Arginase releases the Urea.
Q58. How do intestinal bacteria dangerously contribute to ammonia levels in patients with kidney failure?
A: In kidney failure, plasma urea levels are elevated, promoting massive diffusion of urea into the gut. Intestinal bacterial urease cleaves this urea into CO2 and NH3. This ammonia is reabsorbed into the blood, causing toxic hyperammonemia.
💡 Mnemonic: Urease Unleashes ammonia in the gut.
Q59. Why is oral neomycin administered to patients suffering from hepatic hyperammonemia?
A: Neomycin acts as an antibiotic that drastically reduces the number of intestinal bacteria responsible for this continuous, toxic NH3 production.
💡 Mnemonic: Neomycin Nukes the urease bugs.
Q60. What is the precise overall stoichiometry of ATP consumption in the synthesis of one urea molecule?
A: Three molecules of ATP are consumed, but four high-energy phosphate bonds are cleaved (since one ATP goes to AMP + PPi). This severe energy drop makes the synthesis of urea totally irreversible.
💡 Mnemonic: 3 ATPs, 4 bonds = Expensive cycle.
Q61. Explain how a protein-rich meal biochemically upregulates the rate of urea synthesis via N-acetylglutamate.
A: A protein meal provides high levels of glutamate (a substrate) and arginine. Arginine is an absolute activator for N-acetylglutamate synthase, rapidly increasing synthesis of N-acetylglutamate. This leads to massive allosteric activation of CPS I, increasing the overall rate of urea synthesis to handle the nitrogen load.
💡 Mnemonic: Arginine Accelerates NAG.
Q62. What does the term "transdeamination" refer to in the context of massive ammonia production?
A: It refers to the linking of two enzymes: aminotransferase (funnelling amino groups to glutamate) and glutamate dehydrogenase (oxidatively deaminating glutamate), effectively stripping ammonia from excess dietary amino acids.
Q63. How do the kidneys specifically use circulating glutamine to maintain the body's acid-base balance?
A: The kidneys generate ammonia from glutamine using renal glutaminase and glutamate dehydrogenase. This ammonia binds protons to become NH4+, which is excreted in the urine, effectively removing acid from the body.
💡 Mnemonic: Kidney Cleaves Glutamine to trap H+.
Q64. Besides amino acids, name three other substantial minor sources of ammonia in the human body.
A: 1) Bacterial action on urea in the intestine, 2) action of amine oxidase on dietary amines and neurotransmitters, and 3) the catabolism of purine and pyrimidine rings.
Q65. Why must normal serum ammonia levels be maintained at very low concentrations (5–35 µmol/L)?
A: Because even slightly elevated circulating concentrations are highly neurotoxic to the central nervous system, leading to a medical emergency.
💡 Mnemonic: Ammonia Attacks the Brain.
Q66. List the severe clinical symptoms of ammonia intoxication.
A: Symptoms include tremors, slurring of speech, somnolence, vomiting, cerebral edema, blurring of vision, and at very high concentrations, irreversible coma and death.
Q67. What is the definition and typical cause of "acquired hyperammonemia" in adults?
A: Hyperammonemia caused by liver disease (e.g., viral hepatitis, alcohol hepatotoxins). Cirrhosis can cause portal blood to shunt directly into systemic circulation via collaterals, completely bypassing hepatic urea conversion and causing severe ammonia spikes.
💡 Mnemonic: Shunt Skips the Urea factory.
Q68. What is the most common congenital genetic defect of the urea cycle overall?
A: Ornithine transcarbamoylase (OTC) deficiency.
💡 Mnemonic: OTC = Often The Culprit.
Q69. How does the genetic inheritance pattern of OTC deficiency radically differ from all other urea cycle defects?
A: OTC deficiency is strictly X-linked, predominantly affecting males (though female carriers may be symptomatic), whereas all other urea cycle disorders follow an autosomal recessive inheritance pattern.
💡 Mnemonic: OTC = Only The Chromosome X.
Q70. Why is the hyperammonemia seen with a genetic arginase deficiency generally less severe than blocks higher in the cycle?
A: Because the accumulated substrate, arginine, already contains two waste nitrogens within its structure, and it can be directly and safely excreted in the urine, clearing the nitrogen load.
💡 Mnemonic: Arginine Already carries the trash.
Q71. Explain the pharmacological mechanism of phenylbutyrate as a life-saving treatment for urea cycle defects.
A: Phenylbutyrate is an oral prodrug rapidly converted to phenylacetate. Phenylacetate condenses directly with glutamine to form phenylacetylglutamine, which is excreted in the urine. Because glutamine contains two nitrogen atoms, this allows massive clearance of nitrogenous waste independent of the broken urea cycle.
💡 Mnemonic: Phenylbutyrate Pushes nitrogen into the urine.
Q72. To what seven specific intermediate products do all amino acid catabolic pathways eventually converge?
A: Oxaloacetate, pyruvate, alpha-ketoglutarate, fumarate, succinyl coenzyme A (CoA), acetyl CoA, and acetoacetate.
💡 Mnemonic: O, P, a-KG, F, S, A, A.
Q73. What precisely defines a "glucogenic" amino acid biochemically?
A: Amino acids whose carbon skeleton catabolism yields pyruvate or one of the intermediates of the citric acid cycle. These specific intermediates are substrates for gluconeogenesis and can give rise to the net formation of glucose in the liver and kidney.
💡 Mnemonic: Glucogenic Generates Glucose.
Q74. What precisely defines a "ketogenic" amino acid biochemically?
A: Amino acids whose catabolism yields either acetoacetate or one of its precursors (acetyl CoA or acetoacetyl CoA). Their carbon skeletons are not substrates for gluconeogenesis and cannot give rise to the net formation of glucose.
💡 Mnemonic: Ketogenic Keeps making Ketones.
Q75. Which amino acids are exclusively ketogenic?
A: Amino acids whose catabolism yields either acetoacetate or one of its precursors (acetyl CoA or acetoacetyl CoA) are termed ketogenic. Leucine and lysine are the only exclusively ketogenic amino acids found in proteins.
💡 Mnemonic: Legends do Keto diet L for Leucine lysine and Keto for ketogenic
Q76. Which four specific amino acids are BOTH glucogenic and ketogenic?
A: Tyrosine, Phenylalanine, Tryptophan, and Isoleucine. Their catabolism splits, yielding both glucose precursors and ketone precursors.
💡 Mnemonic: PIT-T (Phenylalanine, Isoleucine, Tyrosine, Tryptophan).
Q77. What is the exact medical rationale for administering the enzyme asparaginase systemically to treat leukemic patients?
A: Some rapidly dividing leukemic cells are completely unable to synthesize sufficient asparagine to support their massive growth, making it an essential nutrient from the blood. Systemic asparaginase hydrolyzes plasma asparagine to aspartate, starving the cancer cells to death.
💡 Mnemonic: Asparaginase Assassinates leukemia cells.
Q78. Histidine is oxidatively deaminated by the enzyme histidase to form what specific compound?
A: Urocanic acid, which subsequently forms N-formiminoglutamate (FIGlu).
Q79. How is the FIGlu excretion test used diagnostically in the clinic?
A: FIGlu must donate its formimino group to tetrahydrofolate (THF) to proceed. If a patient is deficient in folic acid, FIGlu drastically accumulates and is excreted in high amounts in urine, especially after a large oral histidine load.
💡 Mnemonic: High FIGlu = Folate Failure.
Q80. Alanine loses its amino group by reversible transamination to directly form what major gluconeogenic intermediate?
A: Pyruvate, making alanine the body's most major gluconeogenic amino acid.
💡 Mnemonic: ALanine makes pyruvATe.
Q81. A deficiency in the transaminase that converts glyoxylate to glycine causes what dangerous disease?
A: Primary oxaluria Type 1. It causes a massive overproduction of oxalate which precipitates, leading to severe, destructive kidney damage.
💡 Mnemonic: Oxaluria = Oxalate Overload in kidneys.
Q82. Cysteine undergoes desulfuration to yield pyruvate. What is the highly important metabolic fate of the released sulfate?
A: The sulfate can be used to synthesize 3'-phosphoadenosine-5'-phosphosulfate (PAPS), which is the universal activated sulfur donor for acceptors like glycosaminoglycans.
💡 Mnemonic: PAPS Provides the Sulfate.
Q83. What enzyme initiates the catabolism of phenylalanine, and what are the ultimate products?
A: Tetrahydrobiopterin-requiring phenylalanine hydroxylase converts it to tyrosine. This merged pathway ultimately leads to the formation of fumarate and acetoacetate (making them both glucogenic and ketogenic).
Q84. What are the four amino acids that eventually degrade to form succinyl CoA?
A: Methionine, Valine, Isoleucine, and Threonine.
Q85. Describe the structure and energetic formation of S-adenosylmethionine (SAM).
A: Methionine condenses with adenosine triphosphate (ATP), forming SAM—a high-energy compound unusual in that it contains no phosphate. The formation is driven by the total hydrolysis of all three phosphate bonds in ATP.
💡 Mnemonic: SAM is the Super Active Methylator.
Q86. Why is the transfer of the methyl group from SAM considered essentially irreversible in metabolism?
A: The methyl group attached to the tertiary sulfur in SAM is highly "activated". Transferring it yields S-adenosylhomocysteine (SAH), a simple thioether. The massive resulting loss of free energy makes the transfer essentially irreversible.
Q87. Following methyl donation, SAH is hydrolyzed into what two distinct compounds?
A: Homocysteine and adenosine.
💡 Mnemonic: SAM gives a methyl and becomes SAH (Sad and Hollow).
Q88. What happens to homocysteine if there is a cellular deficiency of methionine?
A: Homocysteine undergoes remethylation to reform methionine. It accepts a methyl group from N5-methyltetrahydrofolate in a strict reaction requiring methylcobalamin, a coenzyme derived from vitamin B12.
💡 Mnemonic: B12 and Folate are the Remethylation Mates.
Q89. If methionine stores are adequate, homocysteine enters the transsulfuration pathway. Detail this reaction.
A: Homocysteine condenses with serine to form cystathionine. Cystathionine is then hydrolyzed to alpha-ketobutyrate and cysteine. This sequence has an absolute requirement for Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine) as a coenzyme.
💡 Mnemonic: B6 Builds Cysteine.
Q90. Why is cysteine considered a nonessential amino acid, yet is totally dependent on an essential one?
A: Because it is synthesized via transsulfuration where the sulfur atom is derived exclusively from the essential amino acid methionine. If dietary methionine is inadequate, cysteine synthesis halts and it becomes an essential amino acid.
Q91. What is the clinical significance of elevated plasma homocysteine levels?
A: Elevations promote oxidative damage, profound inflammation, and endothelial dysfunction, serving as a powerful independent risk factor for occlusive atherosclerotic vascular disease.
💡 Mnemonic: High Homocysteine Hurts the Heart.
Q92. Large elevations of plasma homocysteine, resulting in premature vascular disease and thrombotic deaths before age 30, are typically due to a deficiency in which enzyme?
A: Cystathionine beta-synthase, which causes classic homocystinuria.
💡 Mnemonic: CBS is broken.
Q93. Elevated homocysteine or decreased folic acid in pregnant women is highly associated with what severe fetal outcome?
A: An increased incidence of neural tube defects in the fetus (improper closure, e.g., spina bifida). Periconceptual folate supplementation drastically reduces this risk.
💡 Mnemonic: Folate Fills the neural tube.
Q94. Catabolism of valine, isoleucine, and methionine produces propionyl CoA. By what mechanism does this enter the TCA cycle?
A: Propionyl CoA is converted to methylmalonyl CoA by a biotin-dependent carboxylase. Methylmalonyl CoA is then rearranged by methylmalonyl CoA mutase (strictly Vitamin B12-dependent) into succinyl CoA.
Q95. Name the three essential branched-chain amino acids (BCAAs).
A: Isoleucine, Leucine, and Valine.
💡 Mnemonic: LIV.
Q96. What is uniquely different about the tissue metabolism of BCAAs compared to almost all other amino acids?
A: They are metabolized primarily by the peripheral tissues (particularly muscle) rather than by the liver.
💡 Mnemonic: BCAAs bypass the liver to Build Biceps.
Q97. What single enzyme catalyzes the initial removal of amino groups from all three BCAAs?
A: A single, vitamin B6-requiring enzyme called branched-chain amino acid aminotransferase.
Q98. What multienzyme complex catalyzes the oxidative decarboxylation of the alpha-keto acids derived from BCAAs?
A: The branched-chain alpha-keto acid dehydrogenase complex.
Q99. List the five distinct coenzymes required by the branched-chain alpha-keto acid dehydrogenase complex.
A: Thiamine pyrophosphate (TPP), lipoic acid, FAD, NAD+, and CoA.
💡 Mnemonic: Tender Loving Care For Nancy.
Q100. What rare, potentially lethal autosomal recessive disorder results from a deficiency in branched-chain alpha-keto acid dehydrogenase?
A: Maple Syrup Urine Disease (MSUD).
💡 Mnemonic: BCKDH Blocked in MSUD.
Q101. What are the severe clinical characteristics of Maple Syrup Urine Disease?
A: Massive accumulation of BCAAs and their toxic alpha-keto acids in blood. Symptoms include feeding problems, vomiting, dehydration, severe metabolic acidosis, mental retardation, physical disabilities, and a characteristic maple syrup odor to the urine.
Q102. A patient with body fluids exhibiting a strange "sweaty feet" odor has a deficiency in what specific dehydrogenase?
A: A deficiency in the FAD-linked dehydrogenase specific for isovaleryl CoA (an intermediate of leucine catabolism), causing neurological problems.
💡 Mnemonic: Isovaleryl CoA = Stinky Feet.
Q103. What are the final metabolic end products of BCAA catabolism?
A: Valine yields succinyl CoA (glucogenic). Leucine yields acetoacetate and acetyl CoA (ketogenic). Isoleucine yields acetyl CoA and succinyl CoA (both).
Q104. What does the "one-carbon pool" specifically refer to in metabolism?
A: It refers to single carbon units that exist in various oxidation states (formyl, methenyl, methylene, methyl) attached to carrier compounds like tetrahydrofolate (THF) and SAM, ready to be transferred for biosynthetic reactions.
Q105. Is carbon dioxide (CO2) considered a member of the THF one-carbon pool?
A: No. CO2 is the dehydrated form of carbonic acid and is carried exclusively by the vitamin biotin for carboxylation reactions. It is NOT part of the THF one-carbon pool.
Q106. How is the active form of folic acid, Tetrahydrofolic acid (THF), produced?
A: It is produced from folate by the enzyme dihydrofolate reductase in a two-step reaction requiring two molecules of NADPH.
💡 Mnemonic: THF = The Helpful Folate.
Q107. Folate deficiency causes what hematological presentation and why?
A: It presents as a megaloblastic anemia due to the decreased availability of purines and of the TMP needed for DNA synthesis in dividing erythrocytes.
💡 Mnemonic: Mega = Massive RBCs from failed DNA.
Q108. How are the nonessential amino acids alanine, aspartate, and glutamate directly synthesized?
A: They are synthesized by direct transamination: transferring an amino group to the alpha-keto acids pyruvate, oxaloacetate, and alpha-ketoglutarate, respectively.
Q109. Explain the synthesis of the amide-containing amino acid asparagine.
A: It is formed from aspartate by asparagine synthetase, using glutamine as the specific amide nitrogen donor in a reaction driven by ATP hydrolysis.
💡 Mnemonic: Aspartate + Glutamine = Asparagine.
Q110. Serine is synthesized starting from what intermediate of the glycolytic pathway?
A: 3-phosphoglycerate, which is first oxidized to 3-phosphopyruvate, transaminated to 3-phosphoserine, and finally hydrolyzed to serine.
💡 Mnemonic: 3-PG to Serine.
Q111. What enzyme synthesizes glycine from serine, and what one-carbon acceptor is utilized?
A: Serine hydroxymethyl transferase, which transfers a hydroxymethyl group from serine to THF, generating N5,N10-methylene-THF and glycine.
💡 Mnemonic: Serine Shrinks to Glycine via SHMT.
Q112. Explain the synthesis of Tyrosine and its exact, non-negotiable coenzyme requirement.
A: Tyrosine is formed directly from phenylalanine by phenylalanine hydroxylase. This reaction strictly requires molecular oxygen and the specific coenzyme tetrahydrobiopterin (BH4).
💡 Mnemonic: Phenylalanine Produces Tyrosine.
Q113. During tyrosine synthesis, BH4 is oxidized to dihydrobiopterin (BH2). How is BH4 regenerated for continued use?
A: BH4 is regenerated from BH2 by the NADH-requiring enzyme dihydropteridine reductase.
Q114. What is the most common clinically encountered inborn error of amino acid metabolism?
A: Phenylketonuria (PKU), with a prevalence of 1:15,000, most commonly caused by a genetic deficiency of phenylalanine hydroxylase.
💡 Mnemonic: PAH Prevents PKU.
Q115. Besides a direct mutation in phenylalanine hydroxylase, what else can cause dangerous hyperphenylalaninemia?
A: Deficiencies in any of the several enzymes required to synthesize BH4 from GTP, or a deficiency in dihydropteridine reductase, which regenerates BH4.
💡 Mnemonic: BH4 Backs up PAH.
Q116. Why must variant PKU (BH4 deficiency) be treated with more than just a low-phenylalanine diet?
A: Because BH4 is also required for tyrosine hydroxylase and tryptophan hydroxylase. Lack of BH4 causes fatal deficiencies in neurotransmitters (catecholamines and serotonin), requiring replacement therapy with L-DOPA and 5-hydroxytryptophan.
Q117. In classic PKU, what alternative toxic metabolites accumulate to give urine a characteristic musty/mousey odor?
A: Phenyllactate, phenylacetate, and phenylpyruvate (the "phenylketone" that gives the disease its name).
💡 Mnemonic: Phenylketones Produce the Pungent smell.
Q118. What are the severe central nervous system symptoms of totally untreated PKU?
A: Severe mental retardation (IQ rarely over 50), failure to walk or talk, seizures, hyperactivity, tremor, and microcephaly.
Q119. What causes the characteristic hypopigmentation (fair hair, light skin, blue eyes) universally seen in PKU patients?
A: The extremely high levels of phenylalanine competitively inhibit tyrosinase, the crucial first enzyme required for the formation of the pigment melanin from tyrosine.
💡 Mnemonic: High Phe Halts Tyrosinase.
Q120. Why might an infant with severe PKU show perfectly normal blood phenylalanine levels immediately at birth?
A: Because the mother clears the dangerously increased blood phenylalanine of her affected fetus through the placenta during gestation. Toxic levels rise only after 24-48 hours of dietary protein feeding.
💡 Mnemonic: Placenta Purifies the fetus.
Q121. What happens if an adult with PKU decides to discontinue their phenylalanine-restricted diet?
A: Adult PKU patients show a marked deterioration of IQ scores, attention, and speed of mental processing. Lifelong dietary restriction is highly recommended.
Q122. What are the severe teratogenic effects of "Maternal PKU syndrome" if the mother is not on a restricted diet prior to conception?
A: High maternal blood phenylalanine crosses the placenta, acting as a teratogen, causing profound microcephaly, mental retardation, and congenital heart abnormalities in the fetus.
💡 Mnemonic: Maternal Phe Poisons the Fetus.
Q123. Describe Albinism and its most severe form.
A: Albinism is a group of conditions where a defect in tyrosine metabolism drastically reduces melanin. Complete albinism (tyrosinase-negative oculocutaneous albinism) results from a total deficiency of copper-requiring tyrosinase.
💡 Mnemonic: Albinism = Absent Tyrosinase.
Q124. What are the symptoms of complete albinism aside from a total absence of pigment in hair, skin, and eyes?
A: Severe vision defects, photophobia (sunlight physically hurts their eyes), and a highly increased, life-threatening risk for skin cancer.
Q125. What is the precise enzyme deficiency in classic Homocystinuria, and what is its genetic inheritance pattern?
A: A defect in cystathionine beta-synthase, which converts homocysteine to cystathionine. It is an autosomal recessive illness.
💡 Mnemonic: CBS is broken.
Q126. What are the dire clinical symptoms of homozygous cystathionine beta-synthase deficiency?
A: Ectopia lentis (displacement of the lens of the eye), skeletal abnormalities, osteoporosis, neurological deficits, and a life-threatening tendency to form thrombi (blood clots), which can cause fatal strokes or heart attacks before age 30.
Q127. Alkaptonuria is caused by a deficiency in which specific enzyme?
A: Homogentisic acid oxidase, a key enzyme in the degradative pathway of tyrosine.
💡 Mnemonic: Homogentisic acid Halted.
Q128. Detail the three highly characteristic symptoms of Alkaptonuria.
A: 1) Homogentisic aciduria (the patient's urine contains elevated levels of homogentisic acid, which oxidizes to a completely dark, black pigment upon standing in air), 2) large joint arthritis (severely crippling), and 3) black ochronotic pigmentation of cartilage and collagenous tissue.
💡 Mnemonic: Urine, Cartilage, Arthritis (UCA).
Q129. What is the fundamental, molecular structure of a normal collagen molecule?
A: A long, rigid fibrous structure in which three polypeptides (alpha chains) are tightly wound around one another in a rope-like superhelix or triple helix.
💡 Mnemonic: 3 chains = Triple threat.
Q130. Detail the specific amino acid composition that forces the triple helical conformation of collagen.
A: Collagen is exceptionally rich in proline, whose ring structure causes strict structural "kinks" preventing standard alpha-helices. Glycine is placed exactly in every third position (-Gly-X-Y-) because it is the only amino acid small enough to fit in the restricted inner center of the triple helix.
💡 Mnemonic: Glycine is mini, fits in the skinny.
Q131. What post-translational modification is incredibly crucial for stabilizing the collagen triple helix via interchain hydrogen bonds?
A: The hydroxylation of proline and lysine residues after their incorporation into the polypeptide chain, forming hydroxyproline and hydroxylysine.
💡 Mnemonic: Hydroxyls Hold the Helix.
Q132. Name the specific enzyme, vitamin, and metal ion strictly required for this hydroxylation.
A: Prolyl and lysyl hydroxylases require Ascorbic Acid (Vitamin C) as a reducing agent, Fe2+, and molecular oxygen to function.
💡 Mnemonic: Iron out your C-ollagen with Oxygen.
Q133. What is the severe pathology of Scurvy related directly to collagen?
A: Ascorbic acid deficiency severely impairs prolyl and lysyl hydroxylation. Without interchain H-bonds, a stable triple helix cannot form, and subsequent fibrils cannot be cross-linked, causing fragile capillaries, massive bruising, and poor wound healing.
💡 Mnemonic: Scurvy = C-urvy (Vitamin C).
Q134. After procollagen is secreted into the extracellular matrix, what enzymes convert it to mature tropocollagen?
A: Extracellular N- and C-procollagen peptidases cleave the bulky, nonhelical terminal propeptide extensions, releasing tropocollagen to assemble into fibrils.
💡 Mnemonic: Pro has Propeptides. Tropo is Trimmed.
Q135. Describe the biochemical cross-linking of mature collagen fibrils to form strong fibers.
A: The extracellular copper-containing enzyme lysyl oxidase oxidatively deaminates some lysyl and hydroxylysyl residues to highly reactive aldehydes (allysine). These spontaneously condense to form covalent desmosine-like cross-links.
💡 Mnemonic: Copper Cops Cross-link Collagen.
Q136. What is Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome (EDS)?
A: A highly heterogeneous group of inherited generalized connective tissue disorders caused by defects in fibrillar collagen synthesis or processing enzymes (e.g., lysyl hydroxylase), resulting in stretchy skin, loose joints, and vascular issues.
💡 Mnemonic: EDS = Extra Darn Stretchy.
Q137. Describe the biochemical basis and presentation of Osteogenesis Imperfecta (Brittle Bone Syndrome).
A: Most severe cases involve mutations replacing glycine with bulky amino acids in the alpha-1 or alpha-2 chains of Type I collagen, physically preventing the tight triple helix formation. It causes easily fractured, bending bones, a humped back, and sometimes blue sclerae.
💡 Mnemonic: OI = Oh I broke a bone! (Type I collagen).
Q138. How does the primary structure of elastin contrast biochemically with collagen?
A: Elastin is an insoluble polymer synthesized from a linear precursor, tropoelastin. It is rich in small, nonpolar amino acids (glycine, alanine, valine) and proline, but contains very little hydroxyproline and absolutely NO hydroxylysine.
💡 Mnemonic: Elastin = Elastic but Empty of hydroxylysine.
Q139. What unique cross-link gives elastin its highly rubber-like ability to stretch and recoil?
A: The complex desmosine cross-link, formed by three allysyl side chains and one unaltered lysyl side chain combining together from neighboring polypeptides.
💡 Mnemonic: Desmosine = Dos/Tres/Cuatro lysines together.
Q140. Mutations in the fibrillin-1 protein, which serves as a crucial scaffold for tropoelastin, cause what disease?
A: Marfan syndrome, a severe connective tissue disorder characterized by impaired structural integrity in the skeleton (long limbs), the eye (lens dislocation), and the cardiovascular system (fatal aortic dilation).
💡 Mnemonic: Marfan = Fibrillin-1 (Tall and thin like a 1).
Q141. What is the absolutely critical physiological role of alpha-1-antitrypsin (a1-AT) in the lungs?
A: a1-AT is a powerful protease inhibitor (mostly from the liver) that neutralizes neutrophil elastase, a destructive enzyme released by immune cells, thereby preventing it from destroying the vital elastin in alveolar walls.
💡 Mnemonic: a1-AT = Anti-Alveolar Tear.
Q142. How does a single genetic mutation (GAG to AAG) in a1-AT cause both pulmonary emphysema and liver cirrhosis?
A: The mutated protein improperly polymerizes in the endoplasmic reticulum of hepatocytes. This causes a massive decrease in secretion into blood (destroying the lungs due to unopposed elastase) and toxic accumulation in the liver cells (causing severe scarring/cirrhosis).
💡 Mnemonic: Mutated AT Gets Trapped.
Q143. By what exact biochemical mechanism does smoking drastically increase lung destruction in individuals with a1-AT deficiency?
A: Smoking causes the rapid oxidation and subsequent inactivation of a specific, critical methionine residue required for a1-AT to bind to and inhibit elastase, rendering the inhibitor completely powerless.
💡 Mnemonic: Smoke Smothers the Methionine.
Q144. Detail the structural composition of a fully active heme molecule.
A: Heme consists of one ferrous (Fe2+) iron ion coordinated perfectly in the center of the large tetrapyrrole ring of protoporphyrin IX.
💡 Mnemonic: Heme = Iron in a Porphyrin cage.
Q145. What structural feature uniquely distinguishes Type III porphyrins (the only physiologically active form in humans) from Type I?
A: Type III porphyrins contain an asymmetric substitution of side chains (acetate and propionate are reversed) on ring D of the tetrapyrrole nucleus.
💡 Mnemonic: Asymmetric Type 3 is the MVP.
Q146. Which two specific tissues are the major sites of heme biosynthesis?
A: The liver (for rapidly synthesizing cytochrome P450 proteins), and the erythrocyte-producing cells of the bone marrow (which actually accounts for over 85% of all heme synthesis to make hemoglobin).
💡 Mnemonic: Liver for Cytochromes, Marrow for Heme.
Q147. Why are mature red blood cells completely incapable of synthesizing their own heme?
A: Heme synthesis requires enzymatic reactions in both the cytosol and the mitochondria. Mature red blood cells lack mitochondria entirely.
💡 Mnemonic: No Mitochondria = No Heme synthesis.
Q148. What is the committed, absolute rate-limiting step of porphyrin biosynthesis?
A: The condensation of the amino acid glycine and succinyl CoA to form delta-aminolevulinic acid (ALA), catalyzed by the mitochondrial enzyme ALA synthase (ALAS), which strictly requires pyridoxal phosphate.
💡 Mnemonic: ALAS Acts First.
Q149. Explain the powerful end-product inhibition of ALAS1 in the liver by hemin.
A: When heme production exceeds apoprotein availability, heme oxidizes to hemin (Fe3+). Hemin heavily represses hepatic ALAS1 activity by inhibiting its mRNA synthesis, decreasing mRNA stability, and blocking mitochondrial import of the enzyme.
💡 Mnemonic: Hemin Halts ALAS1.
Q150. Why does the administration of certain drugs (like barbiturates) reliably trigger acute, painful attacks of porphyria?
A: Barbiturates massively induce hepatic Cytochrome P450 synthesis, heavily consuming intracellular heme. The sudden drop in heme relieves the normal repression on ALAS1, causing a massive overproduction and accumulation of toxic porphyrin precursors.
💡 Mnemonic: Drugs Drain Heme (starts ALAS1).
Q151. What enzyme condenses two ALA molecules into porphobilinogen, and what heavy metal severely inhibits it?
A: ALA dehydratase (porphobilinogen synthase), a zinc-containing cytosolic enzyme that is extremely, specifically sensitive to inhibition by lead (replacing the zinc).
💡 Mnemonic: Lead Loots the Zinc.
Q152. What linear tetrapyrrole is formed by the condensation of four porphobilinogens?
A: Hydroxymethylbilane (synthesized by hydroxymethylbilane synthase).
💡 Mnemonic: 4 PBG = 1 HMB.
Q153. What mitochondrial enzyme catalyzes the final step of heme synthesis, inserting Fe2+ into protoporphyrin IX?
A: Ferrochelatase, an enzyme that, like ALA dehydratase, is powerfully inhibited by lead.
💡 Mnemonic: Ferro Fills the ring with Fe.
Q154. What exactly are Porphyrias?
A: Rare, mostly autosomal dominant inherited defects in heme synthesis resulting in the dangerous accumulation and increased excretion of porphyrins or porphyrin precursors.
💡 Mnemonic: Porphyria = Purple Pigment Problems.
Q155. What clinical symptom strictly defines a porphyria where the enzyme defect occurs AFTER the formation of tetrapyrroles?
A: Severe photosensitivity. Colorless porphyrinogens accumulate and oxidize in the skin to colored porphyrins. When exposed to visible light, these form reactive oxygen species causing severe itching, burning, and skin blisters.
💡 Mnemonic: Post-tetrapyrrole = Photosensitive (Sunburns).
Q156. Describe the enzyme defect, frequency, and clinical presentation of Porphyria Cutanea Tarda.
A: A deficiency in uroporphyrinogen decarboxylase. It is the most common porphyria, characterized by chronic liver disease, extreme photosensitivity with blistering skin eruptions, and urine that turns red to brown (tea-colored) in natural light.
💡 Mnemonic: PCT = Photosensitive, Chronic, Tea-colored urine.
Q157. What severely characterizes Acute Intermittent Porphyria (AIP), and what enzyme is deficient?
A: Deficiency in hydroxymethylbilane synthase (porphobilinogen deaminase). It causes massive accumulation of ALA and porphobilinogen, resulting in severe acute abdominal pain and neuropsychiatric disturbances (anxiety to delirium), but crucially, NO photosensitivity.
💡 Mnemonic: Pain and Paranoia, No Photosensitivity.
Q158. How does intravenous hemin and glucose biochemically abort acute porphyria attacks?
A: Hemin provides direct, immediate end-product repression of ALAS1, rapidly halting the dangerous overproduction of the toxic intermediates ALA and porphobilinogen.
💡 Mnemonic: Hemin & Glucose Halt ALAS1.
Q159. What specific cells are responsible for the constant degradation of senescent, 120-day-old red blood cells?
A: The macrophages of the reticuloendothelial system, particularly localized in the liver and spleen.
💡 Mnemonic: Macrophages Munch old RBCs.
Q160. Describe the crucial first step of heme degradation catalyzed by the heme oxygenase system.
A: In the presence of NADPH and O2, it cleaves the porphyrin ring to produce the green pigment biliverdin, simultaneously releasing ferric iron (Fe3+) and the gas carbon monoxide (CO), which acts as a vasodilator.
💡 Mnemonic: Oxygenase Opens the ring.
Q161. Biliverdin is immediately reduced to form what red-orange pigment?
A: Bilirubin (by the enzyme biliverdin reductase).
💡 Mnemonic: Green (Verdin) turns Red (Rubin).
Q162. Because unconjugated bilirubin is extremely poorly soluble in plasma, how is it transported to the liver?
A: It binds tightly but noncovalently to serum albumin, acting as a carrier.
💡 Mnemonic: Albumin acts as Bilirubin's Boat.
Q163. Why is administering certain anionic drugs like sulfonamides to neonates a massive risk for kernicterus?
A: Sulfonamides and salicylates can displace unconjugated bilirubin from albumin, allowing it to easily cross the blood-brain barrier and cause toxic neural encephalopathy (kernicterus) in the basal ganglia.
💡 Mnemonic: Sulfa Steals Albumin, causing Kernicterus.
Q164. Once inside the hepatocyte, what specific protein binds bilirubin to prevent it from diffusing back out into the blood?
A: The intracellular protein ligandin.
💡 Mnemonic: Ligandin Locks it in the liver.
Q165. How does the liver chemically increase the solubility of bilirubin for biliary excretion?
A: By conjugation: the addition of two highly polar molecules of glucuronic acid, catalyzed by microsomal bilirubin glucuronyltransferase, forming bilirubin diglucuronide.
💡 Mnemonic: UDP-GA Glues water-solubility to bilirubin.
Q166. Severe genetic deficiencies of bilirubin glucuronyltransferase result in what fatal or mild syndromes?
A: Crigler-Najjar syndrome types I and II (severe/fatal) and Gilbert syndrome (mild).
💡 Mnemonic: Crigler is Critical, Gilbert is Gentle.
Q167. Active transport of conjugated bilirubin into the bile canaliculi is the rate-limiting step. A genetic defect here causes what syndrome?
A: Dubin-Johnson syndrome.
💡 Mnemonic: Dubin-Johnson = Defective transport into bile (Black liver).
Q168. What do intestinal bacteria hydrolyze and reduce bilirubin diglucuronide into within the gut?
A: Urobilinogen, a completely colorless compound.
💡 Mnemonic: Bacteria Break it to Urobilinogen.
Q169. What specific compound gives human feces its characteristic brown color?
A: Stercobilin (formed when intestinal bacteria oxidize urobilinogen).
💡 Mnemonic: Stercobilin makes Stool brown.
Q170. Detail the enterohepatic urobilinogen cycle and its critical role in normal urine coloration.
A: A portion of gut urobilinogen is reabsorbed into portal blood. The liver resecretes most of it into bile, but a fraction reaches the kidneys, where it is oxidized to yellow urobilin and excreted, giving urine its characteristic yellow color.
💡 Mnemonic: Urobilin makes Urine yellow.
Q171. Define Jaundice (Icterus) medically.
A: Jaundice is not a disease but a symptom: the yellow coloration of skin, nail beds, and sclerae (whites of eyes) caused by the deposition of bilirubin, secondary to highly increased bilirubin levels in the blood.
💡 Mnemonic: Jaundice = Yellowing from Bilirubin.
Q172. Describe the pathophysiology of Hemolytic Jaundice (e.g., from massive sickle cell crisis).
A: Massive lysis of RBCs produces bilirubin much faster than the liver can conjugate it, leading to highly elevated UNCONJUGATED bilirubin in the blood. However, more conjugated bilirubin is also excreted into bile, increasing urinary urobilinogen.
💡 Mnemonic: Hemolysis Hikes Unconjugated Bilirubin.
Q173. What is the presentation of Obstructive Jaundice (extrahepatic cholestasis) regarding stool and urine?
A: A physical bile duct block (tumor/stone) prevents bilirubin from reaching the gut. Stools become pale, clay-colored (no stercobilin). The liver regurgitates CONJUGATED bilirubin into blood, which is filtered into urine, darkening it significantly on standing.
💡 Mnemonic: Obstruction = Pale poop, Dark pee.
Q174. Why do premature neonates so frequently develop physiological Neonatal Jaundice?
A: The activity of the hepatic enzyme bilirubin glucuronyltransferase is naturally very low at birth and takes about 4 weeks to reach adult levels, leading to a dangerous buildup of toxic unconjugated bilirubin.
💡 Mnemonic: Immature Liver = Unconjugated build-up.
Q175. What is the exact chemical mechanism by which blue fluorescent light (phototherapy) treats neonatal jaundice?
A: The blue light energy converts lipid-soluble unconjugated bilirubin into more polar, water-soluble photoisomers. These can be directly excreted into the bile without needing the immature glucuronyltransferase enzyme.
💡 Mnemonic: Blue Light Breaks Bilirubin down.
Q176. In the van den Bergh reaction assay, which form of bilirubin corresponds exactly to "direct-reacting" bilirubin?
A: Conjugated bilirubin, because it is highly water-soluble and reacts rapidly (within one minute) with diazotized sulfanilic acid in aqueous solution.
💡 Mnemonic: Direct = Conjugated (reacts Directly).
Q177. What three biologically active amines collectively make up the "catecholamines"?
A: Dopamine, norepinephrine, and epinephrine.
💡 Mnemonic: DNE (Dopamine, NE, Epi).
Q178. What single amino acid serves as the primary precursor for all catecholamines?
A: Tyrosine.
💡 Mnemonic: Tyrosine is the Trunk of the catecholamine tree.
Q179. What is the absolute rate-limiting step in catecholamine synthesis?
A: The hydroxylation of tyrosine to 3,4-dihydroxyphenylalanine (DOPA) by the enzyme tyrosine hydroxylase, which strictly requires tetrahydrobiopterin (BH4).
💡 Mnemonic: TH (Tyrosine Hydroxylase) Takes the lead.
Q180. DOPA is decarboxylated to form dopamine. What is the required coenzyme for this step?
A: Pyridoxal phosphate (PLP).
💡 Mnemonic: PLP Plucks the CO2.
Q181. What unique cofactors are strictly required by dopamine beta-hydroxylase to convert dopamine to norepinephrine?
A: Ascorbate (Vitamin C) as a reducing agent, and Copper (Cu2+).
💡 Mnemonic: Vit C and Copper Craft Norepi.
Q182. The synthesis of epinephrine from norepinephrine requires what specific methyl donor?
A: S-adenosylmethionine (SAM).
💡 Mnemonic: SAM Sends the Methyl.
Q183. What is the underlying biochemical pathology of Parkinson's disease?
A: A neurodegenerative movement disorder caused by the idiopathic loss of dopamine-producing cells in the brain, leading to severely insufficient dopamine production.
💡 Mnemonic: Parkinson's = Poor Dopamine.
Q184. Why is L-DOPA administered to Parkinson's patients instead of dopamine itself?
A: Dopamine absolutely cannot cross the blood-brain barrier, whereas L-DOPA can, allowing the remaining brain cells to decarboxylate it into active dopamine.
💡 Mnemonic: L-DOPA Leaps the Blood-Brain Barrier.
Q185. What two enzymes are primarily responsible for the inactivation and degradation of catecholamines?
A: Monoamine oxidase (MAO) for oxidative deamination, and Catechol-O-methyltransferase (COMT) for O-methylation using SAM as a donor.
💡 Mnemonic: MAO and COMT Clean up Catecholamines.
Q186. What is the major final metabolic excretory product of epinephrine and norepinephrine found in urine?
A: Vanillylmandelic acid (VMA).
💡 Mnemonic: VMA is the Urine Marker.
Q187. Elevated VMA in the urine is highly indicative of what type of tumor?
A: Pheochromocytoma, a tumor of the adrenal medulla characterized by massive, excessive, uncontrolled production of catecholamines.
💡 Mnemonic: Pheo Produces Piles of Catecholamines.
Q188. How do MAO inhibitors function therapeutically as antidepressants?
A: They irreversibly or reversibly inactivate MAO, permitting neurotransmitter molecules (like serotonin and norepinephrine) to escape degradation, accumulate in the presynaptic neuron, and leak into the synaptic space, prolonging receptor activation.
💡 Mnemonic: MAOIs Maintain Mood (by keeping neurotransmitters around).
Q189. What powerful vasodilator and mediator of allergic and inflammatory reactions is secreted by mast cells?
A: Histamine.
💡 Mnemonic: Histamine from Mast cells.
Q190. Histamine is formed by the direct decarboxylation of what amino acid, requiring what coenzyme?
A: Histidine is decarboxylated; the coenzyme is Pyridoxal Phosphate (PLP).
💡 Mnemonic: Histidine Drops CO2 to become Histamine.
Q191. What is the chemical name for Serotonin?
A: 5-hydroxytryptamine (5-HT).
💡 Mnemonic: 5-HT = Serotonin.
Q192. Describe the exact synthesis pathway of Serotonin.
A: Tryptophan is hydroxylated by a BH4-requiring enzyme (analogous to PAH) to form 5-hydroxytryptophan, which is then decarboxylated (requiring PLP) to form serotonin.
💡 Mnemonic: Tryptophan Translates to Serotonin.
Q193. Where is by far the largest amount of serotonin found in the human body?
A: In the cells of the intestinal mucosa.
💡 Mnemonic: Gut has the Most Serotonin.
Q194. Serotonin is converted into what sleep-regulating hormone in the pineal gland via acetylation and methylation?
A: Melatonin.
💡 Mnemonic: Melatonin Makes you sleepy (from Serotonin).
Q195. What is the critical role of creatine phosphate in skeletal muscle?
A: It is a high-energy compound that provides a small but rapidly mobilized reserve of high-energy phosphates transferred directly to ADP to maintain ATP levels during the first few minutes of intense muscular contraction.
💡 Mnemonic: Creatine = Quick Muscle Energy.
Q196. What three molecular precursors are absolutely required for the de novo synthesis of creatine?
A: Glycine, the guanidino group of Arginine, and a methyl group from SAM.
💡 Mnemonic: G-A-S (Glycine, Arginine, SAM).
Q197. The presence of the MB isozyme of creatine kinase in plasma is used diagnostically to detect what severe event?
A: Myocardial infarction (heart damage).
💡 Mnemonic: CK Charges up the Creatine.
Q198. How is creatinine, the waste product, formed in the body?
A: Creatine and creatine phosphate spontaneously (non-enzymatically) cyclize at a slow, constant rate to form creatinine, which is then excreted in the urine.
💡 Mnemonic: Creatinine = Constant Waste.
Q199. Why is a rise in blood creatinine an extremely sensitive and reliable indicator of kidney malfunction?
A: Because it is produced at a constant rate perfectly proportional to muscle mass and is normally rapidly removed from the blood and excreted by the kidneys. Any rise implies severe glomerular filtration failure.
💡 Mnemonic: High Creatinine = Kidneys are Quitting.
Q200. What pigment is synthesized from tyrosine in the epidermis to protect underlying cells from harmful sunlight?
A: Melanin, produced by pigment-forming cells called melanocytes.
💡 Mnemonic: Melanin Makes Skin Dark.
Q201. Which four amino acids are exclusively classified as BOTH glucogenic and ketogenic?
A: Phenylalanine, Tyrosine, Tryptophan, and Isoleucine.
💡 Mnemonic: PIT-T.
Q202. What specific intermediate connects histidine degradation directly to the one-carbon THF pool?
A: FIGlu (N-formiminoglutamate), which donates its formimino group to Tetrahydrofolate (THF), leaving glutamate.
💡 Mnemonic: Histidine Hides FIGlu.
Q203. Which two nonessential amino acids are formed by direct AMIDATION reactions requiring ATP and glutamine as a donor?
A: Glutamine (from glutamate via glutamine synthetase using free ammonia) and Asparagine (from aspartate via asparagine synthetase using glutamine as the amide donor).
💡 Mnemonic: Aspartate + Glutamine = Asparagine.
Q204. In the vital transsulfuration pathway, homocysteine condenses with what amino acid to form cystathionine?
A: Serine (requires Vitamin B6).
💡 Mnemonic: B6 Builds Cysteine.
Q205. Propionyl CoA is formed from the catabolism of which amino acids, and how does it enter the TCA cycle?
A: Valine, Isoleucine, Methionine, and Threonine. It is carboxylated (biotin) to methylmalonyl CoA, then rearranged by methylmalonyl CoA mutase (Vitamin B12) to succinyl CoA.
Q206. A genetic deficiency in methylmalonyl CoA mutase results in what clinical presentation?
A: Methylmalonic acidemia/aciduria, causing highly elevated blood methylmalonic acid, severe metabolic acidosis, and developmental problems.
💡 Mnemonic: Mutase Misses the Mark (causes acidosis).
Q207. What is the overarching metabolic function of Tetrahydrofolate (THF)?
A: It serves as a carrier of single carbon groups (formyl, methenyl, methylene, methyl) for transfer to specific structures being synthesized (e.g., purines, TMP).
💡 Mnemonic: THF = The Helpful Folate.
Q208. What is the ONLY reaction in human metabolism where THF both carries and DONATES a methyl group?
A: The resynthesis of methionine from homocysteine. This reaction also uniquely requires Vitamin B12 (methylcobalamin) to accept the methyl from N5-methyl-THF and pass it to homocysteine.
💡 Mnemonic: Remethylation Requires B12 and Folate.
Q209. What is the incidence of Cystinuria, making it the most common genetic error of amino acid transport?
A: 1 in 7,000 individuals.
Q210. Why is Tyrosinemia Type I particularly dangerous and what accumulates?
A: Deficiency of fumarylacetoacetate hydrolase causes accumulation of toxic fumarylacetoacetate and succinylacetone, leading to massive liver failure, renal tubular acidosis, and a characteristic cabbage-like odor.
💡 Mnemonic: Cabbage odor = Tyrosinemia.
Q211. What is the accepted medical treatment for Tyrosinemia Type I?
A: Strict dietary restriction of phenylalanine and tyrosine, and substrate restriction using the drug nitisinone.
Q212. What distinguishes Cystinosis from Cystinuria?
A: Cystinosis is a rare, severe defect in the transport of cystine OUT of lysosomes, resulting in cystine crystal formation within the lysosome and massive tissue damage. Cystinuria is merely a renal tubular transport defect causing kidney stones.
Q213. What is the precise role of N-acetylglutamate (NAG) synthase, and what activates it?
A: It synthesizes NAG from glutamate and acetyl-CoA. It is strictly and allosterically activated by arginine, tying urea cycle upregulation directly to a high-protein state.
💡 Mnemonic: Arginine Accelerates NAG.
Q214. What happens to the branched-chain amino acids during periods of starvation or increased metabolic need?
A: They are heavily oxidized in muscle as an important energy source. This puts patients with MSUD at high risk of fatal decompensation during any catabolic state.
Q215. What specific enzyme is lacking in a patient with Alkaptonuria?
A: Homogentisic acid oxidase.
💡 Mnemonic: Homogentisic acid Halted.
Q216. How does the urine of an Alkaptonuria patient present clinically?
A: It contains high levels of homogentisic acid, which oxidizes to a completely dark, black pigment upon standing in air for a few hours.
💡 Mnemonic: Black urine, Black cartilage.
Q217. What is Ochronosis, commonly seen in Alkaptonuria?
A: The dense, black pigmentation deposited in cartilage (e.g., ears, sclera) and collagenous tissue, notably the intervertebral disks, causing severe crippling arthritis usually by age 40.
Q218. In classic PKU, what happens to the intellectual quotient (IQ) of a child if left entirely untreated?
A: The child typically shows symptoms of profound mental retardation by the age of 1 year, and rarely achieves an IQ greater than 50.
Q219. Why must treatment for PKU start absolutely within the first 7 to 10 days of life?
A: Because the excessively high phenylalanine acts as a direct, powerful neurotoxin; waiting longer will result in irreversible neurological damage and mental retardation.
Q220. What specifically gives the urine of a patient with Maple Syrup Urine Disease its characteristic smell?
A: The massive accumulation of branched-chain alpha-keto acids (from leucine, isoleucine, valine) that cannot be oxidatively decarboxylated due to the BCKDH block.
💡 Mnemonic: Maple Syrup Smells Sweet, but makes blood Sour.
Q221. What happens in Cystathioninuria?
A: Accumulation of cystathionine and its metabolites due to a deficiency in cystathionase. It is completely benign with no clinical symptoms.
Q222. What completely characterizes Histidinemia?
A: A genetic deficiency in histidase leading to highly elevated levels of histidine in the blood and urine.
💡 Mnemonic: Histidase Halted.
Q223. What protein connects the actin cytoskeleton to the extracellular matrix in muscle cells?
A: The massive protein dystrophin, which acts as a structural bridge at the interior face of the plasma membrane.
💡 Mnemonic: Dystrophin Docks the cytoskeleton.
Q224. Mutations in the gene encoding dystrophin cause what severe myopathies?
A: Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy (severe, functionally incompetent synaptic junctions) and Becker Muscular Dystrophy (milder).
💡 Mnemonic: Duchenne Destroys Muscle.
Q225. What is Titin?
A: The world's largest known protein, whose physiological role is to anchor the ends of myofibrils in the contractile apparatus of cardiac and skeletal muscle.
💡 Mnemonic: Titin is a Titan of a protein.
Q226. Dilated cardiomyopathy can be caused directly by mutations in which specific proteins?
A: Dystrophin, muscle LIM protein, CREB (cyclic AMP response-element binding protein), desmin, and lamin.
💡 Mnemonic: Dilated heart from Defective anchors.
Q227. Mutations in the glycosyltransferases that specifically modify alpha-dystroglycan result in what?
A: Congenital forms of muscular dystrophy, such as limb-girdle muscular dystrophy.
💡 Mnemonic: Glycosylation errors = Girdle weakness.
Q228. Why does lead poisoning cause severe anemia?
A: Lead powerfully inhibits two cytosolic/mitochondrial enzymes of heme synthesis: ALA dehydratase and Ferrochelatase, preventing protoporphyrin ring closure and iron insertion, halting heme production.
💡 Mnemonic: Lead Loots the Heme path.
Q229. If a patient presents with severe abdominal pain and acute psychosis, but NO photosensitivity, which porphyria is it?
A: Acute Intermittent Porphyria (deficiency of hydroxymethylbilane synthase), where early precursors ALA and PBG build up, but tetrapyrrole porphyrinogens do not.
💡 Mnemonic: Pain and Paranoia, No Photosensitivity.
Q230. In Porphyria Cutanea Tarda, what specific porphyrin intermediate dangerously accumulates?
A: Uroporphyrin, which accumulates in the skin and urine due to a block at uroporphyrinogen decarboxylase, causing photosensitivity.
💡 Mnemonic: Uroporphyrin is Under the skin.
Q231. Explain exactly why bilirubin must be conjugated to glucuronic acid.
A: Unconjugated bilirubin is highly lipid-soluble and severely neurotoxic (kernicterus). Glucuronidation adds two polar groups, making it water-soluble, allowing it to be safely secreted into aqueous bile.
💡 Mnemonic: Conjugate to Clear it.
Q232. What is the theorized metabolic function of Bilirubin?
A: Unique to mammals, it appears to function as a powerful antioxidant. It oxidizes to biliverdin, which is then re-reduced by biliverdin reductase, acting as a cyclic radical scavenger.
💡 Mnemonic: Bilirubin Battles oxidation.
Q233. How does massive hemolytic jaundice affect urobilinogen levels in the urine?
A: They are vastly INCREASED. The liver conjugates the massive bilirubin load, dumping more into the gut, forming more urobilinogen, which re-enters the blood and spills into the urine.
💡 Mnemonic: More lysis = More urobilinogen in urine.
Q234. In hepatocellular jaundice, why do stools often become pale and clay-colored?
A: Severe liver damage drastically decreases the conjugation and active secretion of bilirubin into the bile, depriving the gut of the stercobilin pigment that gives feces its brown color.
💡 Mnemonic: No Stercobilin = No color in Stool.
Q235. What is the exact function of the enterohepatic urobilinogen cycle?
A: It recycles a portion of gut urobilinogen back through the portal blood to the liver for re-secretion into bile, ensuring a constant loop of pigment clearance while allowing a small fraction to reach the kidneys to color the urine.
💡 Mnemonic: Reabsorb to Recycle.
Q236. What is the chemical nature of the photoisomers created during phototherapy for neonatal jaundice?
A: They are conformational isomers of unconjugated bilirubin that are sufficiently polar and water-soluble to be excreted directly into the bile without needing the immature glucuronyltransferase enzyme.
💡 Mnemonic: Blue light Bends bilirubin so it can escape.
Q237. How is dopamine transformed into norepinephrine inside the nerve terminal?
A: Dopamine is hydroxylated by dopamine beta-hydroxylase, a reaction that absolutely requires ascorbate (Vitamin C) as a reducing agent and copper as a cofactor.
💡 Mnemonic: Vit C and Cu Craft Norepi.
Q238. Where is epinephrine synthesized primarily, and by what enzyme?
A: In the adrenal medulla, by the enzyme phenylethanolamine-N-methyltransferase (PNMT) using SAM as the methyl donor.
💡 Mnemonic: SAM Supplies the Methyl for Epi.
Q239. What is the ultimate source of ALL carbon and nitrogen atoms in the entire porphyrin ring?
A: The nonessential amino acid Glycine, and Succinyl-CoA from the TCA cycle.
💡 Mnemonic: Glycine & Succinyl CoA Start the chain.
Q240. What is the massive physiological mechanism of action of Epinephrine and Norepinephrine outside the CNS?
A: Released during stress ("fight or flight"), they rapidly increase the degradation of glycogen and triacylglycerol, raise blood pressure, and heavily increase cardiac output.
💡 Mnemonic: Fight or Flight fuels the body.
Q241. What is the profound significance of the 3'-phosphoadenosine-5'-phosphosulfate (PAPS) molecule?
A: Synthesized using sulfate derived directly from cysteine catabolism, it acts as the universal activated sulfur donor for sulfating compounds like glycosaminoglycans and glycosphingolipids.
💡 Mnemonic: PAPS Provides the Sulfate.
Q242. Why is Arginine considered semi-essential or essential in certain states (like trauma or growth)?
A: Though synthesized heavily in the urea cycle, almost all of it is rapidly cleaved by arginase to form urea. Growing children and trauma patients cannot synthesize enough to keep up with protein demands, requiring dietary arginine.
💡 Mnemonic: Kids Need Arginine to Grow.
Q243. How do the kidneys directly and specifically generate ammonia?
A: By using the enzymes renal glutaminase and glutamate dehydrogenase to strip ammonia from circulating glutamine, excreting it as NH4+ to trap protons.
💡 Mnemonic: Kidney Cleaves Glutamine to trap H+.
Q244. A patient has cirrhosis with extensive collateral circulation. Why do they get hyperammonemia?
A: Portal blood rich in ammonia from the gut is shunted directly into the systemic circulation, completely bypassing the hepatic urea cycle where it would normally be cleared.
💡 Mnemonic: Shunt Skips the Urea factory.
Q245. What amino acid provides the vital sulfur atom for the synthesis of SAM?
A: Methionine.
💡 Mnemonic: Methionine Mints the SAM.
Q246. What happens to the enzyme ALAS2 in erythroid cells, and how is it regulated differently from hepatic ALAS1?
A: ALAS2 is located on the X-chromosome and is controlled strictly by the availability of intracellular iron, NOT repressed by hemin like hepatic ALAS1. Loss-of-function causes X-linked sideroblastic anemia.
💡 Mnemonic: ALAS2 = Erythroid, Iron-regulated, X-linked.
Q247. Describe the detailed pathway of Melanin synthesis.
A: Tyrosine is hydroxylated by copper-containing tyrosinase in the melanocytes of the epidermis, forming an intermediate that spontaneously polymerizes into the dark melanin pigment to protect against UV.
💡 Mnemonic: Tyrosinase Tints the skin.
Q248. What are the two distinct end-products of the argininosuccinate lyase reaction in the urea cycle?
A: Arginine (which stays to form urea) and Fumarate (which leaves to link to the TCA cycle).
💡 Mnemonic: ALF (Argininosuccinate -> Arginine + Fumarate).
Q249. Which intermediate of the Urea Cycle is transported FROM the mitochondria INTO the cytosol?
A: Citrulline.
💡 Mnemonic: Citrulline Cruises to the Cytosol.
Q250. Which intermediate of the Urea Cycle is transported FROM the cytosol INTO the mitochondria?
A: Ornithine.
💡 Mnemonic: Ornithine Opens the Mitochondria.

PAST VIVA QUESTIONS

Q: What are the Aromatic amino acids?
A: Phenylalanine, Tyrosine, and Tryptophan.
Q: Maple syrup disease deficiency of enzyme?
A: A partial or complete deficiency in the branched-chain alpha-keto acid dehydrogenase complex, a multienzyme complex that decarboxylates leucine, isoleucine, and valine. It leads to the toxic accumulation of these amino acids and their alpha-keto analogs.
Q: How is Ammonia removed from the body?
A: It is disposed of primarily by the formation of Urea in the liver. Ammonia is safely transported from peripheral tissues to the liver fixed inside Glutamine (via glutamine synthetase) or Alanine (via the glucose-alanine cycle from muscle). In the liver, the urea cycle converts it to urea for excretion by the kidneys.
Q: What are the types of Collagen?
A: Based on their organization: 1. Fibril-forming collagens (Types I, II, III). 2. Network-forming collagens (Types IV, VII). 3. Fibril-associated collagens (Types IX, XII).
Q: What are the major Disorders of collagen (Collagenopathies)?
A: Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome (EDS, mostly Type III mutations causing lethal vascular issues, or Type I causing loose joints), Osteogenesis Imperfecta (Brittle Bone syndrome, Type I mutations causing easily fractured bones), and Scurvy (Vitamin C deficiency disrupting hydroxylation and cross-linking).
Q: Homocystinuria... deficient enzyme and its clinical manifestation?
A: Deficient enzyme: Cystathionine beta-synthase (which converts homocysteine to cystathionine). Clinical manifestations: Ectopia lentis (displacement of the lens of the eye), skeletal abnormalities, a deadly tendency to form thrombi (blood clots), osteoporosis, and neurological deficits.
Q: Name the Ketogenic and Mixed amino acids.
A: Exclusively Ketogenic: Leucine and Lysine. Mixed (Ketogenic and Glucogenic): Phenylalanine, Tyrosine, Tryptophan, and Isoleucine.
Q: Why are ketogenic and glucogenic amino acids called so?
A: Glucogenic amino acids yield pyruvate or TCA cycle intermediates, which can be sent through gluconeogenesis to form net glucose in the liver/kidney. Ketogenic amino acids yield acetoacetate or acetyl CoA/acetoacetyl CoA, which can only form ketone bodies or lipids, and cannot be converted to net glucose.
Q: Discuss the pathology of Tyrosine (Albinism & Alkaptonuria).
A: Albinism is caused by a defect in copper-requiring tyrosinase, resulting in an inability to synthesize melanin pigment, leading to white hair/skin, photophobia, and high skin cancer risk. Alkaptonuria is caused by a deficiency in homogentisic acid oxidase, causing homogentisic acid to accumulate, turning urine black upon standing in air, and causing crippling black ochronotic pigmentation in cartilage and joints.
Q: How is the Urea Cycle regulated?
A: It is strictly regulated at the rate-limiting step catalyzed by Carbamoyl Phosphate Synthetase I (CPS I). CPS I requires N-Acetylglutamate as an essential positive allosteric activator. N-acetylglutamate is synthesized from acetyl-CoA and glutamate; this synthesis is powerfully activated by Arginine, ensuring the cycle ramps up after a protein-rich meal.
Q: Describe Collagen structure and the main Amino Acid in Collagen.
A: Collagen is a long, rigid triple-stranded helix made of three alpha-chains. It has a repeating amino acid sequence of -Gly-X-Y-. It is exceptionally rich in Glycine (found at every third position to fit inside the tight helix core), Proline (forms kinks to shape the helix), and Hydroxyproline (which maximizes interchain hydrogen bonds to stabilize the structure).
