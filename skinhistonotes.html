1. The Skin: Overview

  • The skin and its certain specialized derivatives, called appendages, constitute the integumentary system.
  • The appendages of skin include hair, nails, sebaceous glands, and sweat glands.
  • The skin (also called integument or cutis) forms the external covering of the body.
  • It consists of two layers of completely different types of tissues that are attached to each other over their entire extent:
    1. Epidermis: The superficial layer. It is a stratified squamous keratinized epithelium. Developmentally, derived from the surface ectoderm.
    2. Dermis (or Corium): The deeper layer. Composed of dense, irregular connective tissue. Derived from the mesoderm.

The Hypodermis

  • Under the dermis lies the hypodermis, which consists of loose connective tissue containing variable amounts of adipose tissue.
  • The hypodermis binds the skin loosely to the underlying deep fascia (or underlying muscles where deep fascia is absent).
  • This allows the skin to move over the deep fascia.
  • Generally, the hypodermis is not considered to be a part of the skin.
  • However, some histologists regard it as the third layer of skin because it lodges deeper parts of the appendages like hair follicles and sweat glands.

Classification: Thick vs. Thin Skin

  • Taking into account the thickness of the epidermis, the skin is classified into two types:
    • Thick Skin: Covers palms and soles. The epidermal thickness ranges from 400 to 1400 µm.
    • Thin Skin: Found on the remainder of the body. The epidermal thickness varies from 75 to 150 µm.

2. Epidermis: Keratinocytes

  • The epidermis is a continuously self-replacing stratified squamous keratinized epithelium.
  • It contains four types of cells: Keratinocytes, Melanocytes, Langerhans cells, and Merkel cells.

Keratinocytes (Principal Cells)

  • The most characteristic feature is that their cytoplasm is rich in keratin intermediate filaments (also known as tonofilaments).
  • The protein keratin is synthesized in the rough endoplasmic reticulum.
  • The number of tonofilaments increases as the cells move outwards.
  • Renewal: Keratinocytes undergo constant renewal throughout life by continuous production of new cells by mitotic division in the basal layer (stratum basale).
  • Newly produced keratinocytes constantly move outward into the more superficial layers.
  • As they move away from the basement membrane, they undergo progressive changes in size, shape, and content.
  • Eventually, they transform from cuboidal living cells to dead flat cells which are constantly shed off (desquamated) from the skin surface.
  • Tonofibrils: As cells leave the stratum basale and move outwards, the number of keratin filaments increases and they become aggregated into bundles called tonofibrils.
  • In mature keratinocytes (superficial layers), tonofibrils occupy almost 85% of the cell volume.
  • Lamellar Granules: In the stratum granulosum, keratinocytes begin to form small, oval, membrane-bound granules called lamellar granules.
  • These granules consist of concentric lamellae containing pro-barrier lipids.
  • These lipids are secreted into the intercellular spaces in the stratum corneum.
  • Function: These lipids serve to make the epidermis impermeable to water.

3. Epidermis: Melanocytes

  • These are stellate cells having oval bodies and long dendritic processes.
  • Location: Cell bodies are located among the keratinocytes of the stratum basale.
  • Many long, irregular dendritic processes arise from the cell body.
  • These processes penetrate between keratinocytes of the stratum basale and stratum spinosum to terminate on 5–10 keratinocytes.
  • Attachments: Cell bodies are attached to the basal lamina by hemidesmosomes.
  • Important: No intercellular junctions (desmosomes) exist between melanocytes and neighboring keratinocytes.
  • Identification: Not identifiable in routine H&E sections (need special staining). Electron microscope shows numerous mitochondria, large Golgi, and well-developed RER.

Melanin Synthesis

  • Melanocytes synthesize the brownish-black pigment melanin.
  • Formation occurs within membrane-bound bodies called melanosomes.
  • Melanosomes contain the enzyme tyrosinase.
  • Pathway: Tyrosine → (via Tyrosinase) → DOPA → Melanin.
  • Mature melanosomes (melanin granules) are transferred to the tips of dendritic processes.

Cytocrine Secretion & Function

  • Keratinocytes associated with the melanocyte phagocytose the tips of the dendritic processes.
  • The engulfed melanosomes are transported toward the nucleus of the keratinocyte.
  • They form a cap over the nucleus.
  • Function: This cap absorbs ultraviolet rays from sunlight, preventing damage to the DNA of the nuclei of the nonkeratinized cells.

The Epidermal-Melanin Unit

  • 1 Melanocyte + the associated 5–10 Keratinocytes = Epidermal-Melanin Unit.
  • The average number of units is similar in all people.
  • Skin color differences are due to the speed of production and breakdown of melanosomes.
  • People near the equator produce melanosomes very rapidly for greater UV protection.

Clinical Application: Pigmentation Disorders

  • Albinism: Rare genetic disorder. Melanocytes are present but cannot produce melanin (lack of tyrosinase). Results in pale skin, white hair, vision problems.
  • Vitiligo (Leukoderma): Acquired disorder with white patches. Melanocytes are destroyed. Possible autoimmune cause triggered by environmental factors.
  • Nevus (Mole): Benign cluster of melanocytes. Most develop during childhood/adolescence.
  • Malignant Melanoma: Malignant tumor of melanocytes. Indicated by changes in size, shape, or color of a mole. Must be excised surgically without delay.

4. Other Epidermal Cells

Langerhans Cells

  • Type: Antigen-presenting dendritic cells of the adaptive immune system.
  • Location: Mainly in the stratum spinosum.
  • Identification: Not seen in ordinary H&E. Require special staining (e.g., gold impregnation).
  • Structure: Indented nucleus. Cytoplasm contains Birbeck granules (rod-shaped with expanded ends, resembling tennis rackets).
  • Function:
    • They engulf antigens penetrating the skin.
    • They retract processes and migrate through the dermis to lymphatic vessels.
    • They reach the nearest lymph node to undergo maturation.
    • They present the epitope to B and T lymphocytes to initiate an immune response.

Merkel Cells (Tactile Epithelial Cells)

  • Location: Scattered among cells of the stratum basale.
  • Abundant in highly sensitive areas (fingertips, lips).
  • Structure: Contain dense-cored granules (80–100 nm) with neurosecretory peptides. Also contain keratin filaments.
  • Innervation: The basolateral surface makes a synaptic junction with an expanded terminal of an afferent nerve fiber.
  • Merkel Cell-Neurite Complex: The combination of the cell and the nerve terminal.
  • Function: Mechanoreceptors for light touch. Important for texture and shape perception.

5. Layers of the Epidermis

  • The layers reflect successive stages in the life of a keratinocyte (proliferation, growth, differentiation, death, desquamation).
  • Thin skin has 4 layers; Thick skin has 5 layers.

1. Stratum Basale (Stratum Germinativum)

  • Single layer of basophilic cuboidal or low columnar keratinocytes.
  • Rests on the basement membrane.
  • Junctions:
    • Desmosomes: Bind cells to each other.
    • Hemidesmosomes: Bind cells to the basement membrane.
  • Contents: Stem cells (high mitotic activity), Melanocytes, Merkel cells.
  • EM shows: Small Golgi, few mitochondria, abundant free ribosomes, and tonofilaments.
  • Name: Called Stratum Germinativum because new keratinocytes are produced here by mitosis.

2. Stratum Spinosum (Prickle Cell Layer)

  • Several layers of keratinocytes (polyhedral deep, flattened superficial).
  • "Spines": Numerous fine cytoplasmic processes that meet processes of neighboring cells.
  • Joined by desmosomes (visible as "prickles").
  • Function: Desmosomes strongly bind cells to resist abrasion.
  • Contents: Langerhans cells, tonofibrils (aggregated bundles of filaments), melanin granules.

3. Stratum Granulosum

  • 3 to 5 layers of flattened rhomboid keratinocytes.
  • Keratohyalin Granules: Large, intensely basophilic, irregular, no membrane. Contain filaggrin (promotes tonofilament aggregation).
  • Lamellar Granules (Membrane-coating granules): Small, membrane-bound, lamellar structure. Contain lipids (phospholipids, ceramides).
  • Release contents by exocytosis to form a water-impermeable barrier.
  • Also release proteolytic enzymes to break down desmosomes in upper layers.
  • Cell Death: Nuclei degenerate and organelles are lost via autophagy. Cells die before moving higher.

4. Stratum Lucidum (Clear Layer)

  • Only in Thick Skin (palms/soles).
  • Thin, translucent, refractile, stains poorly.
  • 3-5 layers of flattened, dead, eosinophilic cells.
  • No nuclei/organelles.
  • Cytoplasm contains keratin filaments embedded in eleidin (transformation product of keratohyalin).

5. Stratum Corneum (Horny Layer)

  • Many layers of flattened, highly keratinized dead cells called corneocytes (squames).
  • Devoid of nuclei/organelles.
  • Cytoplasm filled with tonofibrils in an amorphous matrix.
  • Plasmalemma is thickened.
  • Desquamation: Desmosomes in superficial layers are degraded by enzymes (from lamellar granules), causing cells to exfoliate.
  • Thickness of this layer determines overall skin thickness (thick vs thin skin).

Clinical Application

  • Callosities: Abnormally thickened patches of stratum corneum due to excessive friction.
  • Psoriasis: Accelerated turnover (7 days vs 30-40 days). Cells don't mature properly. Stratum granulosum is absent. Nucleated cells reach the surface forming scaly plaques.

6. The Dermis (Corium)

  • Sheet of connective tissue supporting the epidermis.
  • Gives flexibility and strength.
  • Thickness: 0.5 mm (eyelids) to 3 mm (palms/soles).
  • Lodges hair follicles, sweat glands, sebaceous glands.

A. Papillary Layer (Superficial)

  • Lies just beneath the epidermis.
  • Dermal Papillae: Projections protruding into epidermis (interdigitate with epidermal ridges). Creates fingerprints.
  • Composition: Loose connective tissue (fine Type I collagen, reticular fibers, elastic fibers).
  • Anchoring Fibrils: Type VII collagen. Extend from basal lamina to papillary layer. Bind epidermis to dermis.
  • Vascularity: Rich capillary loops (thermoregulation and nutrition).
  • Sensory: Meissner’s corpuscles (tactile) in hairless skin (fingertips, lips). Krause end bulbs.

B. Reticular Layer (Deep)

  • Thicker; Dense irregular connective tissue.
  • Composition: Interlacing bundles of collagen (Type I) and coarse elastic fibers.
  • Ground substance: Dermatan sulfate.
  • Cells: Fibroblasts, macrophages, mast cells, adipocytes.
  • Langer’s Lines: Orientation of collagen bundles. Incisions parallel to these heal better.
  • Sensory: Pacinian corpuscles (pressure/vibration), Ruffini corpuscles (stretch).
  • Smooth Muscle: Found in areolae, penis, scrotum (causes wrinkling).

7. Hypodermis

  • Also called subcutaneous tissue or superficial fascia.
  • Loose connective tissue with variable fat (panniculus adiposus).
  • Fat Distribution:
    • Women: Greater amount, more evenly distributed.
    • General: Distinct in abdomen/buttocks. Thin/absent in eyelids, penis, scrotum, dorsal hand/foot.
  • Structure: Unilocular adipocytes grouped into lobules separated by septa.
  • Functions: Padding, energy store, thermoregulation (insulation), allows skin mobility.
  • Contents: Mammary glands, lower hair follicles, deep sweat glands, Pacinian corpuscles, skeletal muscle (in face/neck/scrotum).

8. Appendages: Hair

  • Thread-like keratinized structures.
  • Derived from invagination of ectodermal epidermis.
  • Absent on: Palms, soles, lips, glans penis, clitoris, labia minora.
  • Thickness varies (18 µm to 180 µm).

Structure of Hair

  • Shaft: Projects above skin.
  • Root: Embedded in skin.
  • Bulb: Expanded deep end.
  • Layers (Cross-section):
    1. Medulla (Center): Shrunken cuboidal cells. Absent in thin hair.
    2. Cortex (Bulk): Fusiform, heavily keratinized cells. Contains melanin.
    3. Cuticle (Surface): Single layer of overlapping flat scales (shingles). Heavily keratinized.

Hair Follicle Structure

  • Internal Epithelial Root Sheath: Derived from epidermis. Covers root. Ends at sebaceous duct.
  • External Epithelial Root Sheath: Continuous with stratum basale/spinosum. Merkel cells present here.
  • Glassy Membrane: Thickened acellular basement membrane.
  • Connective Tissue Sheath: Dense irregular CT from dermis.

Hair Growth Cycle

  • Anagen (Growing): Lasts 2-7 years (scalp). 85% of hairs. Growth: 1.25 cm/month.
  • Catagen (Regression): Lasts 10 days (or 2-3 weeks). Follicle shrinks, detaches from papilla.
  • Telogen (Resting): Lasts 1-4 months. Hair sheds.

Associated Structures

  • Hair Matrix: Germinative cells at base of bulb (homologous to stratum basale). Melanocytes here transfer pigment.
  • Hair Papilla: Vascularized CT indenting the bulb. Provides nutrients.
  • Arrector Pili Muscle: Smooth muscle bundle.
    • Attachments: CT sheath of follicle to papillary dermis.
    • Innervation: Sympathetic.
    • Action: Erection of hair ("goose flesh") to trap warm air; aids sebum secretion.
  • Graying: Due to loss of tyrosinase activity and death of melanocytes (starts ~40 years).

9. Appendages: Nails

  • Hard, translucent, rectangular horny plates.
  • Homologous with stratum corneum.
  • Composition: Hard keratin (high sulfur content). Do not desquamate.

Parts of the Nail

  • Nail Plate:
    • Body: Visible part attached to nail bed. Pink due to underlying blood.
    • Free Edge: Projects distally.
    • Root: Proximal part under nail fold.
  • Nail Bed: Skin under the plate. Consists of Stratum Basale + Spinosum only. Dermis attaches to periosteum (no hypodermis).
  • Nail Matrix: Germinative zone at proximal end. Responsible for growth.
  • Lunula: White crescent. Reflection of partially keratinized matrix cells.
  • Eponychium (Cuticle): Stratum corneum of proximal fold extending over plate.
  • Hyponychium: Thickened stratum corneum under the free edge.
  • Growth: Fingernails ~0.5 mm/week. Toenails slower.

10. Sebaceous Glands

  • Type: Simple branched acinar glands.
  • Secretion Mode: Holocrine (whole cell disintegrates).
  • Location: Dermis. Usually associated with hair follicles (Pilosebaceous unit).
  • Absent in: Palms and soles.
  • Direct opening (no hair): Lips, glans penis, labia minora, eyelids (Meibomian).

Cellular Structure

  • Basal Cells: Peripheral stem cells. Small, cuboidal.
  • Central Cells: Large, polyhedral. Accumulate fat (cholesterol/triglycerides). Nuclei become pyknotic and disappear.
  • Sebum: The oily breakdown product.

Function & Control

  • Function: Lubricates skin/hair, weak antibacterial/antifungal.
  • Control: Androgens (Testosterone in males; Ovarian/Adrenal in females). Active at puberty.

Clinical: Acne Vulgaris

  • Chronic inflammation of obstructed sebaceous glands.
  • Sebum accumulates and is invaded by bacteria.
  • Common at puberty due to androgen surge.

11. Sweat (Sudoriferous) Glands

A. Eccrine Sweat Glands

  • Distribution: Entire body (except glans penis). Most numerous on palms/soles.
  • Type: Simple coiled tubular. Merocrine secretion.
  • Secretory Portion:
    • Clear Cells: Transport water/electrolytes. Basal infoldings.
    • Dark Cells: Secrete glycoproteins (bactericidal). Granules present.
    • Myoepithelial Cells: Contractile. Expel sweat.
  • Duct: Stratified cuboidal (2 layers). Spirals through epidermis. Resorbs Na+/Cl-.
  • Secretion: Watery, hypotonic, acidic (pH 4-6.8). Contains urea, ammonia, uric acid.
  • Innervation: Sympathetic Cholinergic fibers.
  • Functions: Thermoregulation, Excretion, Acid mantle (protection).

B. Apocrine Sweat Glands

  • Location: Axilla, Areola, Perineum, Ear (Ceruminous), Eyelids (Moll).
  • Development: Functional at puberty.
  • Opening: Into hair follicle (not surface).
  • Structure: Large lumen. Simple cuboidal/columnar. Myoepithelial cells present.
  • Secretion Mode: Merocrine (exocytosis). *Note: Named "apocrine" historically due to error, but name persists.*
  • Secretion: Viscous, protein-rich. Odorless initially → Musky odor via bacterial decomposition.
  • Innervation: Sympathetic Adrenergic fibers. Responds to stress/emotion.
