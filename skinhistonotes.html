Integumentary System

Chapter 15 • Detailed Notes

Overview & Structure of Skin

▼
  • The skin (also called integument or cutis) forms the external covering of the body.
  • Consists of two layers of completely different types of tissues attached to each other:
    • Epidermis: Superficial layer, stratified squamous keratinized epithelium. Derived from surface ectoderm.
    • Dermis (Corium): Deeper layer, dense irregular connective tissue. Derived from mesoderm.
  • Hypodermis: Lies under the dermis. Consists of loose connective tissue containing variable amounts of adipose tissue.
    • Binds skin loosely to underlying deep fascia or muscles.
    • Allows skin to move over deep fascia.
    • Not considered part of the skin, but histologists regard it as the third layer.

Types of Skin

  • Thick Skin: Covers palms and soles. Epidermal thickness ranges from 400 to 1400 μm.
  • Thin Skin: Found on remainder of body. Epidermal thickness varies from 75 to 150 μm.

Cells of the Epidermis

▼

The epidermis is a continuously self-replacing stratified squamous keratinized epithelium containing four types of cells:

1. Keratinocytes

  • Cytoplasm is rich in keratin intermediate filaments (also known as tonofilaments).
  • Protein keratin is synthesized in the rough endoplasmic reticulum.
  • Number of tonofilaments increases as cells move outwards.
  • Tonofibrils: Aggregated bundles of keratin filaments found in mature keratinocytes. Occupy almost 85% of the cell volume.
  • Lamellar granules: Small, oval, membrane-bound granules in stratum granulosum.
    • Contain pro-barrier lipids secreted into intercellular spaces.
    • Make the epidermis impermeable to water.

2. Melanocytes

  • Stellate cells having oval bodies and long dendritic processes.
  • Located among keratinocytes of the stratum basale.
  • Synthesize brownish-black pigment melanin.
  • Tyrosinase: Enzyme that converts tyrosine → 3,4-dihydroxyphenylalanine (DOPA) → melanin.
  • Melanosomes: Membrane-bound bodies containing melanin.
  • Transferred from dendritic processes to keratinocytes, forming a cap over the nuclei to protect DNA from UV rays.
  • Attached to basal lamina by hemidesmosomes (but no desmosomes with keratinocytes).
  • Clinical Application:
    • Albinism: Genetic disorder, lack of tyrosinase.
    • Vitiligo: Autoimmune destruction of melanocytes (depigmentation).
    • Mole (Nevus): Benign growth, cluster of melanocytes.
    • Malignant Melanoma: Malignant tumor of melanocytes.

3. Langerhans Cells

  • Antigen-presenting dendritic cells.
  • Located in the stratum spinosum.
  • Not identifiable in H&E sections; require special staining (gold impregnation).
  • Possess several dendritic processes.
  • Cytoplasm contains small number of mitochondria, sparse RER, but many lysosomes and vesicles.
  • Constitute an important component of the adaptive immune system.
  • Engulf antigen and migrate to secondary lymphoid organ to present to T lymphocytes.

4. Merkel Cells

  • Tactile epithelial cells.
  • Found in stratum basale (among cells of stratum basale).
  • Function as mechanoreceptors concerned with sensation of light touch.
  • Sensory nerve endings play an important role in perception of texture and shape.
  • Merkel cell-neurite complex: Combination of expanded terminal of afferent nerve fiber and Merkel cell.
  • Cytoplasm contains neurosecretory peptides (dense-cored granules).

Layers of the Epidermis

▼

1. Stratum Basale

  • Also called stratum germinativum.
  • Single layer of basophilic cuboidal or columnar keratinocytes resting on basement membrane.
  • Desmosomes: Bind cells to each other (lateral/outer surfaces).
  • Hemidesmosomes: Bind cells to basal lamina.
  • Contains Merkel cells and cell bodies of melanocytes.

2. Stratum Spinosum

  • Several layers of polyhedral keratinocytes.
  • Cells have "spines" (cytoplasmic processes) joined by desmosomes.
  • Stratum germinativum: Note that mitotic division occurs in this layer too, so some histologists include it in the term "stratum germinativum".
  • Contains Langerhans cells and dendritic processes of melanocytes.

3. Stratum Granulosum

  • 3 to 5 layers of flattened rhomboid keratinocytes.
  • Keratohyalin granules: Intensely basophilic large granules. Irregular, not bounded by membrane. Consist of filaggrin (promotes aggregation of tonofilaments).
  • Lamellar granules: Membrane-coating granules produced by Golgi. Release contents by exocytosis to seal intercellular space.

4. Stratum Lucidum

  • Identifiable only in thick skin (palms and soles). Not seen in thin skin.
  • Independent layer or subdivision of stratum corneum.
  • 3 to 5 layers of flattened eosinophilic keratinocytes.
  • Nuclei and organelles have been lost.
  • Filaments embedded in a protein called eleidin (transformation product of keratohyalin).

5. Stratum Corneum

  • Superficial layer consisting of 15 to 20 layers of flattened, cornified, dead cells called corneocytes.
  • Cells are devoid of nuclei and organelles.
  • Dead cells are called squames.
  • Desquamation: Dead corneocytes are constantly exfoliated from skin surface; degraded by special proteolytic enzymes released from lamellar granules.

The Dermis (Corium)

▼

A sheet of connective tissue that supports the epidermis and gives skin flexibility and strength. Ranges from 0.5 mm (eyelids) to 3 mm (palms/soles).

1. Papillary Layer

  • Outer layer, lies just beneath epidermis.
  • Consists of loose connective tissue.
  • Dermal papillae: Projections that protrude into overlying epidermis. Interdigitate with epidermal ridges.
  • Contains capillary loops.
  • Contains sensory receptors: Meissner's corpuscles (tactile) and Krause end bulbs.

2. Reticular Layer

  • Thicker layer, composed of dense, irregularly arranged connective tissue.
  • Consists of interlacing bundles of collagen fibers (mainly Type I) and coarse elastic fibers.
  • Cells: Fibroblasts, macrophages, mast cells, adipose cells.
  • Contains smooth muscle fibers (arrector pili).
  • Contains encapsulated sensory receptors: Pacinian corpuscles and Ruffini corpuscles.
  • Areolae of nipples, penis, and scrotum contain scattered smooth muscle fibers.

Hair and Hair Follicles

▼
  • Hair Follicle: Tubular invagination of epidermis extending into dermis.
  • Hair Bulb: Expanded lower end.
  • Dermal Papilla: Vascularized loose connective tissue indenting the base of the bulb. Provides oxygen/nutrients.
  • Matrix: Germinative zone of cells capping the papilla. Responsible for growth. Contains melanocytes (transfer pigment to cortex).

Structure of Hair Shaft

  • 1. Medulla: Central axis. 2 or 3 layers of shrunken, moderately keratinized cuboidal cells. Absent in very thin hair.
  • 2. Cortex: Main bulk of hair. Long, flattened, heavily keratinized fusiform cells. Contains melanin pigment.
  • 3. Cuticle: Outermost covering. Single layer of heavily keratinized, squamous cells which partially overlap each other.

Structure of Hair Follicle

  • Internal Epithelial Root Sheath: Derived from epidermis. Does not extend above the duct of sebaceous gland.
  • External Epithelial Root Sheath: Represents stratum corneum/granulosum. Continuous with stratum basale/spinosum.
  • Connective Tissue Root Sheath: Outer layer, dense irregular connective tissue.
  • Glassy Membrane: Acellular hyaline layer (thickened basement membrane) separating epithelial follicle from connective tissue sheath.

Arrectores Pilorum

  • Oblique bundles of smooth muscle fibers found in the dermis.
  • Attached at one end to papillary layer of dermis, other end to connective tissue root sheath.
  • Innervated by sympathetic nerve fibers.
  • Contraction causes erection of hair ("goose flesh") and aids in sebum discharge.

Hair Growth Cycle

  • Anagen: Growing phase. Lasts 2-7 years.
  • Catagen: Regression phase. Follicle shrinks. Lasts about 10 days.
  • Telogen: Resting phase. Hair is dormant. Lasts 3-4 months.

Nails

▼
  • Hard, translucent, roughly rectangular, horny plates.
  • Nail Plate: Consists of hard keratin.
    • Body: Uncovered visible part.
    • Free edge: Distal part beyond skin.
    • Root: Proximal part beneath a fold of epidermis.
  • Nail Bed: Skin under the nail plate. Consists only of stratum basale and stratum spinosum.
    • Dermis here does not bear typical papillae but longitudinal ridges.
  • Nail Matrix: Germinative zone responsible for growth. Epithelial cells divide and move distally.
  • Lunula: Crescent-shaped whitish area on proximal part of body (reflection of partially keratinized cells).
  • Eponychium (Cuticle): Stratum corneum of proximal nail fold extending onto nail surface.
  • Hyponychium: Stratum corneum of skin beneath the free edge.

Glands of the Skin

▼

Sebaceous Glands

  • Location: Dermis. Absent in palms and soles.
  • Ectopic locations: Lips, glans penis (Tyson's glands), eyelids (Meibomian glands).
  • Pilosebaceous unit: Sebaceous gland + hair follicle + arrector pili.
  • Structure: Simple branched acinar.
  • Secretion type: Holocrine (cells are filled with secretion, die, and break down).
  • Sebum: Oily mixture of lipids, triglycerides, cholesterol.
    • Maintains skin texture and hair flexibility.
    • Weak antifungal and antibacterial properties.
  • Acne Vulgaris: Inflammation of obstructed glands (comedones).

Eccrine Sweat Glands

  • Simple tubular glands. Distributed throughout skin (except lips, nail beds, glans penis).
  • Abundant in palms and soles.
  • Secretory portion: Coiled, deep in dermis. Lined by simple cuboidal/columnar epithelium.
    • Clear cells: Rich in glycogen, secrete water/electrolytes.
    • Dark cells: Rich in RER/glycoprotein granules, secrete bactericidal proteins.
    • Myoepithelial cells: Contractile, lie between secretory cells and basal lamina.
  • Duct: Stratified cuboidal epithelium. Reabsorbs Na+ and Cl-.
  • Secretion: Clear, odorless, watery, hypotonic fluid.
  • Innervation: Cholinergic sympathetic nerve fibers (response to heat).

Apocrine Sweat Glands

  • Restricted distribution: Axilla, areola of breast, circumanal region, labia majora.
  • Become functional at puberty.
  • Structure: Simple coiled tubular. Lumen is much larger than eccrine.
  • Secretion: Viscous fluid (proteins, carbohydrates, ammonia).
    • Odorless when released; distinctive musky odor due to bacterial decomposition.
  • Method: EM studies reveal they release secretion by merocrine method (despite the name).
  • Innervation: Adrenergic sympathetic nerve fibers (response to emotional/sensory stimuli).

Hypodermis

▼
  • Also called subcutaneous tissue or superficial fascia.
  • Amount of fat varies in different regions and nutritional status.
  • More distinct in lower abdomen, perineum, and limbs. Thin on dorsal aspect of hands/feet.
  • Functions:
    • Padding and energy store (fat).
    • Thermoregulation (conserves body heat).
  • Contents:
    • Mammary glands.
    • Lowermost portions of hair follicles.
    • Blood vessels, lymph vessels, cutaneous nerves.
    • Ruffini corpuscles and Pacinian corpuscles.
    • Skeletal muscle in face, neck, and scrotum.
