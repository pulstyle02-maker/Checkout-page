Renal Physiology

Detailed Notes: Reabsorption, Secretion & Clearance

1. Introduction to Renal Processes

Urine formation and the composition of urine represent the sum of three basic renal processes. As filtrate flows sequentially through the proximal tubule, loop of Henle, distal tubule, collecting tubule, and collecting duct, substances are selectively handled.

Urinary Excretion = Glomerular Filtration − Tubular Reabsorption + Tubular Secretion

Quantitative Importance

For most substances, tubular reabsorption plays a much more important role than secretion in determining final excretion. However, secretion is significant for potassium ions, hydrogen ions, and a few other substances.

Filtration = GFR × Plasma Concentration

Example: If plasma glucose is 1 g/L and GFR is 180 L/day, the filtered load is 180 g/day. Since virtually no glucose is excreted, reabsorption is also 180 g/day.

Key Observations from Renal Handling

  1. Magnitude: Glomerular filtration and tubular reabsorption are quantitatively very large compared to urinary excretion. A small change in reabsorption (e.g., a 10% decrease) can cause a massive increase in urine volume (e.g., 13-fold increase) if GFR is constant. In reality, GFR and reabsorption are closely coordinated.
  2. Selectivity: Unlike filtration (which filters essentially all plasma solutes except proteins), reabsorption is highly selective.
    • Glucose/Amino Acids: Almost completely reabsorbed.
    • Ions (Na, Cl, HCO3): Highly reabsorbed, but variable based on body needs.
    • Waste (Urea, Creatinine): Poorly reabsorbed. Creatinine is not reabsorbed at all.

By controlling reabsorption of different substances, the kidneys regulate solutes independently.

2. Mechanisms of Tubular Transport

Reabsorption involves transport across the tubular epithelial membranes into the interstitial fluid, and then through the peritubular capillary membrane into the blood.

Transport Pathways

  • Transcellular Route: Substances move through the cell membranes.
  • Paracellular Route: Substances move between the cell junctions.
  • Ultrafiltration (Bulk Flow): Transport from interstitium into peritubular capillaries, mediated by hydrostatic and osmotic forces.

Active Transport

Moves solute against an electrochemical gradient; requires energy.

  • Primary Active Transport: Coupled directly to ATP hydrolysis (e.g., Na+-K+ ATPase).
  • Secondary Active Transport: Coupled indirectly to an energy source, usually an ion gradient (e.g., Glucose reabsorption).

Primary Active Transport: Sodium

The Na+-K+ ATPase pump functions throughout most of the renal tubule. It is located on the basolateral membrane.

  1. The pump hydrolyzes ATP to move Na+ out of the cell into the interstitium and K+ into the cell.
  2. This creates low intracellular Na+ (12 mEq/L) and high K+. It also creates a net negative intracellular charge (-70 mV).
  3. This gradient favors passive diffusion of Na+ from the tubular lumen into the cell across the luminal (apical) membrane.
  4. Proximal tubules have an extensive brush border to multiply surface area for this transport.

Other Primary Transporters: Hydrogen ATPase, Hydrogen-Potassium ATPase, Calcium ATPase.

Secondary Active Transport

Two or more substances interact with a carrier protein. One substance (Na+) moves down its gradient, releasing energy to drive another substance uphill.

  • Co-transport (Symport): Sodium-Glucose (SGLT) and Sodium-Amino Acids. SGLT2 (early proximal) reabsorbs 90% of glucose; SGLT1 (late proximal) reabsorbs the rest. Glucose exits the basolateral side via GLUT transporters.
  • Counter-transport (Antiport): Sodium-Hydrogen Exchanger (NHE). Na+ moves into the cell, and H+ is forced out into the lumen (Secretion).

Pinocytosis

An active mechanism for reabsorbing large molecules like proteins (mainly in proximal tubule). The membrane invaginates to form a vesicle. The protein is digested into amino acids within the cell.

3. Transport Maximum (Tm) & Gradient-Time

Transport Maximum (Tm)

For actively reabsorbed/secreted substances, there is a limit to the rate of transport due to saturation of carrier proteins. This limit is the Transport Maximum.

Glucose Example:

  • Normal Filtered Load: ~125 mg/min.
  • Transport Maximum: ~375 mg/min.
  • Threshold: The plasma concentration at which glucose first appears in urine. This occurs before the overall Tm is reached because not all nephrons have the same Tm (heterogeneity).

Glucose375 mg/min
Phosphate0.10 mmol/min
Sulfate0.06 mmol/min
Amino Acids1.5 mmol/min
Plasma Protein30 mg/min

Secretory Tm: Creatinine (16 mg/min), PAH (80 mg/min).

Gradient-Time Transport

Some actively transported substances (like Sodium in the proximal tubule) do not exhibit a classic Tm. Their rate depends on:

  1. The electrochemical gradient.
  2. The permeability of the membrane (backleak).
  3. The time the fluid remains in the tubule (flow rate).

In proximal tubules, backleak is high. Therefore, the slower the flow rate, the greater the percentage of sodium reabsorbed.

4. Passive Reabsorption (Water, Urea, Cl-)

Water (Osmosis)

Active transport of solutes creates a concentration gradient, causing osmosis of water in the same direction.

  • Proximal Tubule: Highly permeable to water (via Aquaporin-1 and "leaky" tight junctions). Water reabsorption keeps pace with solute reabsorption.
  • Solvent Drag: Water moving across paracellular junctions carries dissolved solutes (K+, Mg+, Cl-) with it.
  • Tight Junctions Distally: In the loop of Henle and distal tubule, junctions are tighter. Water permeability here is controlled by ADH (Antidiuretic Hormone).

Chloride

Coupled to Sodium. When Na+ leaves the lumen, the lumen becomes negatively charged, pushing Cl- out (passive paracellular diffusion). Also, water reabsorption concentrates Cl- in the lumen, creating a chemical gradient for diffusion.

Urea

Water reabsorption concentrates Urea in the lumen. This creates a gradient for passive urea reabsorption. However, urea is less permeant than water. Only about 50% of filtered urea is reabsorbed. Creatinine is essentially impermeant (0% reabsorbed).

5. Proximal Tubule Characteristics

Normally reabsorbs about 65% of filtered Na+, Water, Cl-, HCO3-, and K+.

Cellular Structure

Highly metabolic (many mitochondria). Extensive brush border on the luminal side and labyrinth of channels on the basolateral side for massive surface area.

Early vs. Late Proximal Tubule

  • First Half: Na+ is reabsorbed via co-transport with Glucose, Amino Acids, and organic solutes.
  • Second Half: Most glucose is gone. Na+ is now reabsorbed mainly with Chloride. The high Cl- concentration (140 mEq/L) here favors diffusion.

Concentrations

Although total amount of Na+ decreases, Na+ concentration remains constant (isosmotic) because water follows. Glucose and Amino Acids concentrations decrease markedly. Creatinine concentration increases.

Secretion

Important site for secretion of organic acids and bases (bile salts, oxalate, urate, catecholamines). Also secretes drugs (penicillin, salicylates) and toxins. PAH is secreted so rapidly (90% clearance) it is used to estimate Renal Plasma Flow.

6. Loop of Henle

Consists of three functionally distinct segments.

1. Thin Descending Limb

  • Highly permeable to Water (20% of filtered water reabsorbed here).
  • Moderately permeable to solutes.
  • Function: Allows simple diffusion.

2. Thin Ascending Limb

  • Virtually impermeable to water.
  • Reabsorbs some Na/Cl.

3. Thick Ascending Limb

  • Thick epithelial cells, high metabolic activity.
  • Impermeable to Water. (Urine becomes dilute here).
  • Reabsorbs 25% of filtered Na+, Cl-, K+.
  • Mechanism: Na-K ATPase (basolateral). NKCC2 Co-transporter (Luminal) moves 1-Na, 2-Cl, 1-K into cell.
  • Loop Diuretics: Furosemide, Ethacrynic acid, Bumetanide inhibit the NKCC2 transporter here.
  • Paracellular Transport: Slight backleak of K+ into lumen creates a +8 mV positive charge in the lumen. This drives Mg++ and Ca++ reabsorption paracellularly.
7. Distal Tubule & Collecting Ducts

Early Distal Tubule (Diluting Segment)

  • Macula Densa: Part of juxtaglomerular complex; feedback control of GFR.
  • Avidly reabsorbs ions (Na, K, Cl) but is impermeable to water and urea.
  • Mechanism: Na-Cl co-transporter.
  • Thiazide Diuretics: Inhibit the Na-Cl co-transporter here.

Late Distal & Cortical Collecting Tubule

Composed of two cell types:

1. Principal Cells:

  • Reabsorb Na+ and Water; Secrete K+.
  • Driven by Na-K ATPase.
  • Controlled by Aldosterone.
  • Potassium-Sparing Diuretics:
    • Aldosterone Antagonists: Spironolactone, Eplerenone.
    • Na+ Channel Blockers: Amiloride, Triamterene.

2. Intercalated Cells:

  • Important for Acid-Base balance.
  • Type A (Acidosis): Secrete H+ (via Hydrogen-ATPase and H-K ATPase). Reabsorb HCO3- and K+. Can secrete against large gradient (1000:1).
  • Type B (Alkalosis): Secrete HCO3- and K+. Reabsorb H+. Uses Pendrin (Cl-HCO3 exchanger).

Permeability: Water permeability here is strictly controlled by ADH.

8. Medullary Collecting Duct & Concentrations

Medullary Collecting Duct

  • Final processing of urine.
  • Water Permeability: Controlled by ADH. With high ADH, water is avidly reabsorbed to concentrate urine.
  • Urea Permeability: Unlike the cortical segment, this part IS permeable to urea. Urea transporters facilitate diffusion into the interstitium, helping raise medullary osmolality.
  • H+ Secretion: Capable of secreting H+ against concentration gradient.

Concentration Summary (Inulin Ratio)

Inulin is used to assess water reabsorption because it is filtered but not reabsorbed/secreted.

  • At end of proximal tubule: Inulin concentration ratio is 3.0 (means 1/3 water remains).
  • At end of collecting ducts: Ratio can rise to 125 (means >99% water reabsorbed).
  • Substances with ratio > 1.0 are reabsorbed less avidly than water (Creatinine, Urea).
  • Substances with ratio < 1.0 are reabsorbed more avidly than water (Glucose, Amino Acids).
9. Glomerulotubular Balance & Physical Forces

Glomerulotubular Balance

The intrinsic ability of tubules to increase reabsorption rate in response to increased tubular load. This works independently of hormones.

Example: If GFR increases from 125 to 150 ml/min, proximal reabsorption increases from 81 to 97.5 ml/min, keeping the percentage constant (65%). This prevents overloading of distal segments.

Peritubular Capillary Physical Forces

Reabsorption = Kf × Net Reabsorptive Force

Normal Rate: 124 ml/min. Net Force: 10 mm Hg.

The Forces:

  • Peritubular Hydrostatic Pressure (Pc): 13 mm Hg (Opposes reabsorption).
  • Interstitial Hydrostatic Pressure (Pif): 6 mm Hg (Favors reabsorption).
  • Plasma Colloid Osmotic Pressure (πc): 32 mm Hg (Favors reabsorption).
  • Interstitial Colloid Osmotic Pressure (πif): 15 mm Hg (Opposes reabsorption).

Regulation of Forces

  1. Arterial Pressure: Increase raises Pc → Decreases Reabsorption.
  2. Arteriolar Resistance: Constriction (Afferent or Efferent) lowers Pc → Increases Reabsorption.
  3. Systemic Plasma Protein: Increase raises πc → Increases Reabsorption.
  4. Filtration Fraction (FF): Higher FF (GFR/RPF) concentrates the proteins remaining in the capillary → Higher πc → Increases Reabsorption.

Backleak Mechanism: Reduced reabsorptive force raises interstitial pressure, increasing "backleak" of Na+ and water into the lumen via tight junctions.

10. Pressure Natriuresis & Hormonal Control

Pressure Natriuresis & Diuresis

Even small increases in arterial pressure cause marked increases in urinary excretion of sodium and water. This occurs via:

  1. Slight increase in GFR (if autoregulation is impaired).
  2. Increase in renal interstitial hydrostatic pressure (enhancing backleak).
  3. Reduced Angiotensin II formation.
  4. Internalization of sodium transporters.

Hormonal Control

1. Aldosterone: (Zona glomerulosa). Stimuli: High K+, Angiotensin II. Site: Principal cells (Collecting tubule). Effect: Stimulates Na+-K+ ATPase and Na+ channels. Result: ↑Na reabsorption, ↑K secretion, ↑H secretion. (Addison's = loss of Na; Conn's = retention).

2. Angiotensin II: Body's most powerful Na-retaining hormone. Increases reabsorption via 3 mechanisms:

  • Stimulates Aldosterone secretion.
  • Constricts efferent arterioles (Lowers Pc, Raises Filtration Fraction/πc).
  • Directly stimulates Na+ reabsorption in proximal tubule/loop/distal (Stimulates NHE, Na-K ATPase, Na-HCO3 co-transport).

3. Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH): Increases water permeability in distal/collecting tubules. Mechanism: Binds V2 receptors → cAMP → Protein Kinase A → Insertion of Aquaporin-2 (AQP-2) into luminal membrane. (Lack of ADH = Diabetes Insipidus).

4. Atrial Natriuretic Peptide (ANP): Secreted by stretched cardiac atria. Directly inhibits Na/Water reabsorption and inhibits Renin/Ang II. Increases excretion.

5. Parathyroid Hormone (PTH): Increases Ca++ reabsorption (Distal/Connecting tubule). Inhibits Phosphate reabsorption (Proximal). Stimulates Mg++ reabsorption (Loop).

6. Sympathetic Nervous System: Activation constricts arterioles (↓GFR). Also directly stimulates Na+ reabsorption via alpha-adrenergic receptors.

11. Clearance Concepts

Renal Clearance: The volume of plasma completely cleared of a substance per unit time.

C = (U × V) / P

(U = Urine Conc, V = Urine Flow, P = Plasma Conc).

Inulin (Measuring GFR)

Inulin is freely filtered, NOT reabsorbed, NOT secreted. Therefore, Rate of Excretion = Rate of Filtration.

GFR = Clearance of Inulin

Creatinine (Estimating GFR)

Creatinine is a byproduct of muscle metabolism. It is filtered and slightly secreted. The error in secretion cancels the error in plasma measurement, making it a good GFR estimate. Pcr is inversely proportional to GFR. If GFR falls by 50%, Plasma Creatinine doubles.

PAH (Measuring Renal Plasma Flow)

PAH is filtered and secreted so avidly that it is ~90% cleared from plasma in one pass.

RPF ≈ Clearance of PAH

Total RPF = PAH Clearance / Extraction Ratio (0.9). From this, Renal Blood Flow (RBF) can be calculated using Hematocrit.

Filtration Fraction (FF)

FF = GFR / RPF

Normal FF is ~0.19 (19%).

Calculation of Reabsorption/Secretion

  • If Clearance < Inulin Clearance: Substance is Reabsorbed.
  • If Clearance > Inulin Clearance: Substance is Secreted.
  • Reabsorption Rate = (GFR × P) − (U × V).
