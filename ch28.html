I. INTRODUCTION TO TUBULAR PROCESSING

The kidney handles different substances via three distinct processes: Filtration, Reabsorption, and Secretion.

Urinary Excretion = Glomerular Filtration - Tubular Reabsorption + Tubular Secretion

Quantitative Importance:

  • Filtration is Huge: The GFR is 180 Liters/day. Since the plasma volume is only 3 Liters, the entire plasma volume is filtered 60 times per day.
  • Reabsorption is Selective: Unlike filtration (which is non-selective for solutes < 69,000 Da), reabsorption is highly selective.
    • Glucose & Amino Acids: Completely reabsorbed (Excretion = 0).
    • Ions (Na, Cl, HCO3): Highly reabsorbed (>99%). Excretion varies with intake.
    • Waste (Urea, Creatinine): Poorly reabsorbed. Large amounts excreted.

Comparison of GFR and Reabsorption (Values to Memorize):

  • Glucose: Filtered 180 g/day ‚Üí Reabsorbed 180 g/day ‚Üí Excreted 0.
  • Bicarbonate: Filtered 4320 mEq/day ‚Üí Reabsorbed 4318 mEq/day ‚Üí Excreted ~2.
  • Sodium: Filtered 25,560 mEq/day ‚Üí Reabsorbed 25,410 ‚Üí Excreted 150.
  • Creatinine: Filtered 1.8 g/day ‚Üí Reabsorbed 0 ‚Üí Excreted 1.8.

II. MECHANISMS OF TRANSPORT

1. Active vs. Passive Transport

  • Passive Transport (Diffusion): Movement down an electrochemical gradient. No energy required. (e.g., Water via osmosis, Urea).
  • Active Transport: Movement against a gradient. Requires energy (ATP).
    • Primary Active: Direct ATP hydrolysis (e.g., Na+-K+ ATPase).
    • Secondary Active: Uses energy stored in an ion gradient created by primary transport (e.g., Sodium-Glucose Co-transport).

2. Sodium Transport (The Central Mechanism)

Sodium reabsorption powers the reabsorption of most other substances.

  • Basolateral Membrane (Primary Active): The Na+-K+ ATPase pump hydrolyzes ATP to pump 3 Na+ OUT of the cell (into interstitium) and 2 K+ INTO the cell.
    • Result 1: Low Intracellular Na+ (12 mEq/L) vs Tubular Lumen (140 mEq/L).
    • Result 2: Electronegative cell interior (-70 mV).
  • Luminal Membrane (Passive Entry): Na+ moves from the lumen into the cell down its huge electrochemical gradient. It uses carrier proteins (e.g., SGLT, NHE) or channels (ENaC).
  • Ultrafiltration into Capillaries: Na+ and water in the interstitium move into peritubular capillaries via Bulk Flow governed by Starling Forces.

3. Secondary Active Transport (Glucose & Amino Acids)

These substances enter the cell against their gradient by coupling with Sodium moving down its gradient.

  • SGLT (Sodium-Glucose Linked Transporter): Located on the brush border (Luminal side).
    • SGLT2: Proximal Tubule S1 segment (90% of glucose).
    • SGLT1: Proximal Tubule S3 segment (10% of glucose).
  • GLUT (Glucose Transporter): Facilitated diffusion carriers on the Basolateral side. Glucose exits the cell into the blood passively. (GLUT2 in S1, GLUT1 in S3).

4. Secretion Mechanisms

  • Counter-Transport: One substance moves down its gradient to drive another against its gradient in the opposite direction.
  • NHE (Sodium-Hydrogen Exchanger): Na+ moves IN, H+ moves OUT (Secreted). This is secondary active secretion.

5. Pinocytosis

Large proteins (Albumin) are reabsorbed by attaching to the brush border, invaginating, and forming vesicles. This requires ATP (Active).

III. TRANSPORT MAXIMUM (Tm) AND THRESHOLD

1. Concept of Tm

Active transport systems become saturated when the tubular load exceeds the capacity of carrier proteins.

  • Transport Maximum for Glucose ($T_mG$): 375 mg/min.
  • Filtered Load: $GFR \times Plasma Glucose$. Normal is $125 \times 1 = 125 mg/min$.

2. Renal Threshold

The plasma concentration at which glucose first appears in the urine.

  • Actual Threshold: 200 mg/dl (Venous plasma).
  • Theoretical Threshold: 300 mg/dl (Calculated from Tm).
  • Splay: The difference between actual and theoretical threshold.
    • Reason: Not all nephrons have the same Tm. Some saturate earlier than others.

IV. REABSORPTION ALONG THE NEPHRON

1. Proximal Convoluted Tubule (PCT) - "The Workhorse"

  • Capacity: Reabsorbs 65% of Filtered Na+, H2O, Cl-, and K+. Reabsorbs 100% of Glucose and Amino Acids.
  • Histology: Extensive brush border (high surface area), lots of mitochondria (high active transport).
  • Mechanism:
    • First Half: Na+ is reabsorbed with Glucose/Amino acids (Co-transport).
    • Second Half: Na+ is reabsorbed with Chloride. (Chloride concentration rises in the lumen as water is reabsorbed, driving diffusion).
  • Secretion: Organic Acids/Bases (Bile salts, Oxalate, Urate, Catecholamines). Also drugs (Penicillin, PAH).
  • Isosmotic Reabsorption: The PCT is highly permeable to water (Aquaporin-1). Water follows solutes instantly. Tubular fluid osmolarity remains 300 mOsm/L.

2. Loop of Henle

Consists of three segments with distinct functions.

  • Thin Descending Limb:
    • Highly permeable to Water (Aquaporin-1).
    • Impermeable to Solutes.
    • Function: Water leaves, urine becomes Hypertonic.
  • Thin Ascending Limb:
    • Impermeable to Water.
    • Permeable to NaCl (Passive diffusion).
  • Thick Ascending Limb (TAL):
    • Impermeable to Water.
    • Active Transport: Strong Na+ reabsorption.
    • Transporter: NKCC2 (Na-K-2Cl Co-transporter).
      • Target of Loop Diuretics (Furosemide).
    • Positive Lumen Potential (+8 mV): Potassium leaks back into the lumen via ROMK channels. This positive charge drives the passive paracellular reabsorption of Cations (Mg++, Ca++, Na+).
    • Diluting Segment: Since solutes leave but water stays, tubular fluid becomes Hypotonic (~100 mOsm/L).

3. Distal Tubule

  • Early Distal Tubule:
    • Properties similar to Thick Ascending Limb (Impermeable to water, "Diluting segment").
    • Transporter: NCC (Na-Cl Co-transporter).
    • Target of Thiazide Diuretics.
    • Contains the Macula Densa.
  • Late Distal Tubule & Cortical Collecting Duct:
    • Contains two cell types:
    • 1. Principal Cells (Majority):
      • Reabsorb Na+ (via ENaC channels) and Secrete K+.
      • Hormonal Control: Aldosterone stimulates the Na-K pump and ENaC channels.
      • Target of K-Sparing Diuretics (Spironolactone antagonists Aldosterone; Amiloride blocks ENaC).
      • Water permeability is controlled by ADH (Vasopressin).
    • 2. Intercalated Cells (Type A and B):
      • Type A: Secrete H+ (active H+-ATPase) to correct acidosis. Reabsorb K+.
      • Type B: Secrete HCO3- to correct alkalosis.

4. Medullary Collecting Duct

  • Final processing of urine.
  • Water: Permeability controlled by ADH.
  • Urea: Permeable (UT-A1 transporters). Urea diffuses into the medulla to help concentrate urine.
  • Acid: Can secrete H+ against a huge gradient (900-fold).

V. REGULATION OF TUBULAR REABSORPTION

1. Glomerulotubular Balance

  • Definition: The intrinsic ability of the tubules to increase their reabsorption rate in response to increased tubular load.
  • Effect: If GFR increases from 125 to 150 ml/min, the PCT reabsorption increases automatically (from 81 to 97.5 ml/min) to maintain the constant fraction (65%).
  • Mechanism: Prevents overloading of distal segments.

2. Peritubular Physical Forces (Starling Forces)

  • Reabsorption = $K_f \times Net Reabsorptive Force$.
  • Forces Favoring Reabsorption:
    • Peritubular Capillary Oncotic Pressure ($\pi_c$): High (32 mmHg) because filtration concentrated the proteins.
    • Interstitial Hydrostatic Pressure ($P_{if}$): Low (6 mmHg).
  • Forces Opposing Reabsorption:
    • Peritubular Capillary Hydrostatic Pressure ($P_c$): Low (13 mmHg).
    • Interstitial Oncotic Pressure ($\pi_{if}$): Low.
  • Pressure Natriuresis: If Arterial Pressure rises ‚Üí $P_c$ rises ‚Üí Reabsorption decreases ‚Üí Urine output increases.

3. Hormonal Regulation (High Yield)

  • Aldosterone:
    • Site: Late Distal & Cortical Collecting Tubule.
    • Action: Increases Na+ reabsorption, Increases K+ secretion.
    • Stimulus: Angiotensin II, High K+.
    • Pathology: Addison‚Äôs (loss of Na), Conn‚Äôs (retention of Na).
  • Angiotensin II: The most powerful Na-retaining hormone.
    • Action 1: Stimulates Aldosterone.
    • Action 2: Constricts Efferent arteriole ‚Üí Reduces $P_c$ and increases $\pi_c$ ‚Üí Increases physical reabsorption.
    • Action 3: Directly stimulates Na-H exchange in PCT.
  • ADH (Vasopressin):
    • Site: Distal Tubule & Collecting Ducts.
    • Action: Inserts Aquaporin-2 channels. Increases Water reabsorption.
  • ANP (Atrial Natriuretic Peptide):
    • Source: Cardiac atria (stretch).
    • Action: Inhibits Na/Water reabsorption. Decreases Renin/Aldosterone.
  • PTH (Parathyroid Hormone):
    • Action: Increases Calcium reabsorption (Distal tubule). Inhibits Phosphate reabsorption (PCT).
  • Sympathetic Nervous System: Increases Na+ reabsorption directly and via Renin release.

VI. RENAL CLEARANCE

The volume of plasma that is completely cleared of a substance by the kidneys per unit time.

C = (U √ó V) / P
  • Inulin Clearance: Used to measure GFR. Inulin is freely filtered, not reabsorbed, not secreted. Clearance = 125 ml/min.
  • Creatinine Clearance: Clinical estimate of GFR. Small amount is secreted, so it slightly overestimates GFR, but measurement methods balance this error.
  • PAH (Para-aminohippurate) Clearance: Used to measure Renal Plasma Flow (RPF). PAH is filtered and completely secreted. Clearance = 650 ml/min.
  • Calculation of Reabsorption/Secretion:
    • If Clearance < GFR: Substance is Reabsorbed (e.g., Urea, Glucose).
    • If Clearance > GFR: Substance is Secreted (e.g., Creatinine, PAH).
    • If Clearance = 0: Substance is fully reabsorbed (Glucose) or not filtered.
