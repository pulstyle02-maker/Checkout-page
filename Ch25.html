THE BODY FLUID COMPARTMENTS: Extracellular and Intracellular Fluids; Edema

PART I: GENERAL PRINCIPLES OF FLUID HOMEOSTASIS

Core Concept: Homeostasis is not a static state but a dynamic equilibrium. The maintenance of fluid volume and electrolyte composition is the bedrock of physiology. While the kidneys are the primary effectors (adjusting output), the system relies on a precise balance between Input and Output.

1. FLUID INTAKE (THE INPUT SIDE)

Water is added to the total body pool via two distinct physiological pathways.

A. Ingested Water (Exogenous)
  • Mechanism: Regulated by the Thirst Center in the hypothalamus (specifically the organum vasculosum of the lamina terminalis - OVLT) and the lateral preoptic area.
  • Volume: Averages 2100 ml/day.
  • Variability: This is the most variable component of fluid balance.
    • Climate: Heat induces thirst via dehydration.
    • Culture/Habit: Social drinking (coffee, tea, alcohol) often exceeds physiological need.
    • Physical Exertion: Exercise increases intake demand.
B. Metabolic Water (Endogenous)
  • Mechanism: This is "new" water created inside the body. It is a byproduct of Cellular Respiration (Oxidative Phosphorylation) in the mitochondria.
  • Chemical Reaction:
    C6H12O6 + 6O2 ‚Üí 6CO2 + 6H2O + Energy
  • Volume: Averages 200 ml/day.
  • Clinical Relevance: In patients with anuria (renal failure), even if they drink nothing, they still generate 200ml of fluid internally, which must be accounted for in fluid restriction protocols.

TOTAL DAILY INTAKE: 2100 + 200 = 2300 ml/day

2. FLUID OUTPUT (THE LOSS SIDE)

Water elimination occurs via four routes. To maintain balance, Total Output must equal Total Input (2300 ml/day).

A. Insensible Water Loss (The "Silent" Loss)
  • Definition: Water loss via evaporation that occurs continuously and without conscious awareness. It is pure water loss (solute-free).
  • Total Volume: 700 ml/day.

Sub-Mechanism 1: Cutaneous Diffusion (Skin)

  • Volume: 300‚Äì400 ml/day.
  • Physiology: Water molecules possess kinetic energy and diffuse through the cells of the skin into the air.
  • The Barrier: The Cornified Layer (Stratum Corneum) of the epidermis is packed with intracellular Cholesterol and keratin. This lipid barrier reduces evaporation by 90-95%.
  • Clinical - Burn Physiology: In extensive burns, the stratum corneum is denuded. The rate of evaporation increases 10-fold, potentially reaching 3‚Äì5 Liters/day. This massive evaporative heat loss causes hypothermia and hypovolemic shock.

Sub-Mechanism 2: Respiratory Evaporation (Lungs)

  • Volume: 300‚Äì400 ml/day.
  • The Physics: Gas exchange surfaces must be moist. Air entering the respiratory tract is warmed to 37¬∞C and saturated to 100% Humidity.
  • Vapor Pressure: At 37¬∞C, the water vapor pressure (PH2O) is 47 mmHg.
  • The Loss Calculation: We inhale air with variable humidity (usually < 47 mmHg) and exhale air with exactly 47 mmHg. The water difference is lost from the body.
  • Cold Weather Physiology: Cold air holds very little moisture (vapor pressure near 0 mmHg). Therefore, the gradient between dry inhaled air and moist exhaled air is maximal. This leads to rapid respiratory water loss, causing the subjective feeling of a dry throat and increased thirst in winter.
B. Sweat (Sudoriferous Loss)
  • Nature: Sensible perspiration produced by sweat glands.
  • Volume: Highly variable.
    • Basal: ~100 ml/day.
    • Maximal: 1‚Äì2 Liters/hour during heavy exertion.
  • Composition: Sweat is Hypotonic to plasma. It contains Na+ and Cl-, but at lower concentrations than blood (due to ductal reabsorption of ions).
  • Clinical: Profuse sweating causes the loss of more water than salt. This results in Hypernatremic Dehydration (concentrating the remaining body fluids).
C. Fecal Loss
  • Volume: 100 ml/day.
  • Physiology: The GI tract secretes ~7-8 Liters of fluid daily, but the colon reabsorbs almost all of it.
  • Pathology: In cholera or severe viral gastroenteritis, reabsorption fails (or secretion increases), and loss can exceed 10 Liters/day, leading to rapid vascular collapse.
D. Renal Loss (Urine)
  • Role: The kidney is the controller of balance. It adjusts output to match input.
  • Volume:
    • Average: 1400 ml/day.
    • Minimum (Obligatory Urine Volume): 0.5 L/day. (Calculated as: 600 mOsm solute load / 1200 mOsm max concentration).
    • Maximum: 20 L/day (in Polydipsia).
  • Key Principle: The kidney can decouple water excretion from solute excretion. It can excrete large volumes of water without losing excess salt, or conserve water while excreting salt.

PART II: THE BODY FLUID COMPARTMENTS

The body acts as a "bag of water" divided by membranes with differing permeabilities.

1. TOTAL BODY WATER (TBW)

  • The 60-40-20 Rule:
  • Standard Man (70 kg): TBW is 60% of body weight.
  • Calculation:
    70 kg √ó 0.60 = 42 Liters
  • Physiological Variations:
    • Adipose Tissue Effect: Fat cells are hydrophobic and contain almost no water (< 10%). Muscle/organ tissue is water-rich (~75%).
    • Females: Average TBW is 50%. Reason: Higher percentage of subcutaneous fat and lower muscle mass compared to males.
    • Obesity: As BMI increases, the % of TBW decreases.
    • Infants: TBW is 70‚Äì75%.
      • Why? Very low fat mass and underdeveloped muscle.
      • Risk: An infant losing 10% of body water via diarrhea is in critical condition compared to an adult losing the same percentage.
    • Aging: TBW declines to ~50% in men and ~45% in women over age 60, due to sarcopenia (muscle loss).

2. INTRACELLULAR FLUID (ICF)

  • Definition: The aggregate fluid contained within the ~75 trillion cells of the body.
  • Volume: 40% of body weight.
  • Calculation:
    70 √ó 0.40 = 28 Liters
  • Fraction: 2/3 of Total Body Water.
  • Homogeneity: Despite cells having different functions, the ionic "soup" inside them is remarkably similar (High K+, Low Na+) due to the universal presence of the Na-K ATPase Pump.

3. EXTRACELLULAR FLUID (ECF)

  • Definition: The "Internal Environment" (Milieu Int√©rieur) bathing the cells.
  • Volume: 20% of body weight.
  • Calculation:
    70 √ó 0.20 = 14 Liters
  • Fraction: 1/3 of Total Body Water.
  • Sub-Compartments of ECF:
    • Interstitial Fluid (ISF): The fluid directly outside capillaries and between cells. Volume: 11 Liters (approx 3/4 of ECF).
    • Plasma: The non-cellular fluid of the vascular system. Volume: 3 Liters (approx 1/4 of ECF). Exchange: Plasma communicates freely with ISF through capillary pores.
    • Transcellular Fluid: A collection of specialized fluids separated by an epithelial layer. Includes: Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), Synovial fluid (joints), Intraocular fluid, Peritoneal/Pleural/Pericardial fluids, Digestive secretions. Volume: 1‚Äì2 Liters. Note: Clinically, this is often considered a "Third Space" where fluid can become sequestered (e.g., Ascites) and unavailable for circulation.

4. BLOOD VOLUME (A Mixed Compartment)

Blood is unique because it is contained in a closed chamber (vessels) but consists of both ECF (plasma) and ICF (RBCs/WBCs).

  • Total Blood Volume: ~7% of body weight (‚âà 5 Liters).
  • Composition:
    • Plasma: 60% (‚âà 3 L).
    • Formed Elements (Red Cell Mass): 40% (‚âà 2 L).

5. HEMATOCRIT (Hct)

  • Definition: The packed cell volume; the fraction of blood composed of RBCs.
  • Centrifugation: Blood is spun in a tube; cells settle to the bottom.
  • The "Trapped Plasma" Error: About 3-4% of plasma gets stuck between the packed red cells.
  • Correction:
    True Hematocrit = 0.96 √ó Measured Hematocrit
  • Normal Values: Males: 0.40 (40%). Females: 0.36 (36%).
  • Clinical Pathologies:
    • Anemia: Hct drops to 0.10. Oxygen carrying capacity is severely compromised.
    • Polycythemia: Hct rises to 0.65. Blood viscosity increases dramatically, increasing resistance to flow and cardiac workload.

PART III: CHEMICAL CONSTITUENTS OF FLUIDS

1. The Barrier Principles

  • Cell Membrane: Separates ECF from ICF. Highly permeable to water. Highly selective for ions. Result: Drastic difference in composition (High Na outside, High K inside).
  • Capillary Membrane: Separates Plasma from Interstitium. Highly permeable to water AND ions. Impermeable to Proteins. Result: Composition is almost identical, except for proteins.

2. THE GIBBS-DONNAN EQUILIBRIUM (The Donnan Effect)

This physical principle explains the slight ionic differences between Plasma and ISF.

  • The Cause: Plasma proteins (Albumin/Globulin) are negatively charged anions and are too large to leave the capillary.
  • The Effect on Cations (+): The negative proteins attract positive ions (Na+, K+) to the plasma side to maintain electrical neutrality. Result: Plasma cation concentration is ~2% higher than ISF.
  • The Effect on Anions (-): The negative proteins repel other negative ions (Cl-). Result: ISF anion concentration is ~2% higher than Plasma.

3. DETAILED IONIC COMPOSITION (The "Table of Life")

Students must memorize these values for anion gap and osmolarity calculations.

A. Cations (+)
  • Sodium (Na+): ECF: 142 mEq/L (Determinant of ECF osmolarity). ICF: 14 mEq/L.
  • Potassium (K+): ECF: 4.2 mEq/L. ICF: 140 mEq/L.
  • Calcium (Ca++): ECF: 2.4 mEq/L. ICF: < 0.0001 mEq/L.
  • Magnesium (Mg++): ECF: 1.2 mEq/L. ICF: 20 mEq/L.
B. Anions (-)
  • Chloride (Cl-): ECF: 108 mEq/L. ICF: 4 mEq/L.
  • Bicarbonate (HCO3-): ECF: 24 mEq/L. ICF: 10 mEq/L.
  • Phosphates: ECF: 2 mEq/L. ICF: 11 mEq/L.
  • Proteins: ECF: 1.2 mEq/L (14 mEq/L by charge). ICF: 4 mEq/L.

PART IV: MEASUREMENT OF FLUID VOLUMES

1. The Indicator-Dilution Principle

Volume = (Mass Injected - Mass Excreted) / Final Concentration

2. Criteria for a Valid Indicator

  • Dispersal, Confinement, Inert, Stability.

3. Specific Markers

  • Total Body Water (TBW): Radioactive Water (Tritium 3H2O), Deuterium (2H2O), Antipyrine.
  • Extracellular Fluid (ECF): Inulin (Gold Standard), Radioactive Sodium (22Na), Thiosulfate.
  • Plasma Volume: Evans Blue Dye (T-1824), 125I-Albumin.

4. Indirect Calculations

  • ISF = ECF - Plasma.
  • ICF = TBW - ECF.
  • Blood Volume = Plasma Volume / (1 - Hematocrit).

PART V: OSMOSIS AND OSMOTIC PRESSURE

1. Biophysical Definitions

  • Osmosis: Net diffusion of Water across a semi-permeable membrane.
  • The Rate: Water moves through "Aquaporins" 100x faster than solutes. Equilibrium is reached almost instantly.

2. Osmole vs. Mole

  • Glucose: 1 mole = 1 osmole.
  • NaCl: 1 mole = 2 osmoles.
  • Na2SO4: 1 mole = 3 osmoles.

3. Calculating Body Osmolarity

  • Calculated Osmolarity (sum of ions) = ~300 mOsm/L.
  • Correction Factor (Van der Waals forces) = 0.93.
  • Actual Osmolarity:
    300 √ó 0.93 = 280 mOsm/L

4. Van't Hoff's Law

  • At 37¬∞C, 1 mOsm/L creates 19.3 mmHg of pressure.
  • Total Body Pressure:
    280 √ó 19.3 = 5400 mmHg

PART VI: FLUID SHIFTS (DARROW-YANNET ANALYSIS)

Scenario A: Isotonic Saline (0.9% NaCl): ECF Volume increases. No change in Osmolarity or ICF.

Scenario B: Hypertonic Saline (3% NaCl): ECF Volume increases drastically (infused + fluid from cells). ICF Volume decreases. Osmolarity increases.

Scenario C: Hypotonic Fluid (5% Dextrose): ECF Volume increases slightly. ICF Volume increases significantly. Osmolarity decreases.

PART VII: PATHOLOGY OF SODIUM

1. Hyponatremia (Na < 142 mEq/L)
  • Effect: Cell Swelling.
  • Causes: Addison's, Diuretics (Na loss). SIADH (Water retention).
  • Brain Adaptation: Glial cells pump out K+, Cl-, Glutamate to lower intracellular osmolarity.
  • Risk: Rapid correction leads to Osmotic Demyelination Syndrome (ODS).
2. Hypernatremia (Na > 142 mEq/L)
  • Effect: Cell Shrinkage.
  • Causes: Diabetes Insipidus (No ADH), Sweat loss, Conn's Syndrome.

PART VIII: EDEMA

1. Intracellular Edema

Caused by metabolic depression (Ischemia ‚Üí No ATP ‚Üí Na-K pump fails) or inflammation.

2. Extracellular Edema Mechanisms
  • Increased Capillary Pressure: Heart failure, Renal failure.
  • Decreased Plasma Proteins: Nephrotic syndrome, Cirrhosis, Kwashiorkor.
  • Increased Permeability: Inflammation, Burns.
  • Lymphatic Obstruction: Filariasis, Cancer.
3. Safety Factors Against Edema
  • Low Compliance of Interstitium (Negative Pressure): 3 mmHg.
  • Increased Lymph Flow: 7 mmHg.
  • Protein Washout: 7 mmHg.
  • TOTAL SAFETY FACTOR: 17 mmHg.

PART IX: FLUIDS IN POTENTIAL SPACES

Pleural, Pericardial, Peritoneal, Synovial. Kept empty by lymphatic drainage creating negative pressure. Effusion in peritoneum is Ascites.
