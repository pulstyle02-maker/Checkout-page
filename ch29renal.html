Urine Concentration & Dilution

Chapter 29: Regulation of ECF Osmolarity & Sodium

1. Introduction & Basic Concepts

Extracellular Fluid Regulation

For body cells to function, they must be bathed in extracellular fluid with a relatively constant concentration of electrolytes. Osmolarity (total solute concentration) must be regulated to prevent cell shrinkage or swelling.

Osmolarity is determined by the amount of solute (mainly NaCl) divided by the volume of extracellular fluid. Therefore, regulation depends largely on the amount of extracellular water.

Total body water is controlled by:

  • Fluid Intake: Regulated by thirst.
  • Renal Water Excretion: Regulated by glomerular filtration and tubular reabsorption.

Capability of the Kidneys

The kidneys can vary the proportions of solutes and water in urine tremendously:

  • Excess Water: Kidneys excrete urine as dilute as 50 mOsm/L (1/6th of normal ECF).
  • Water Deficit: Kidneys excrete concentrated urine up to 1200 to 1400 mOsm/L.

Crucially, the kidney can excrete large volumes of dilute urine or small volumes of concentrated urine without major changes in the excretion rates of solutes like sodium and potassium.

Role of Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH)

Also called Vasopressin. It controls urine concentration independently of solute excretion.

  • High Osmolarity: Posterior pituitary secretes ADH → Increases permeability of distal tubules/collecting ducts to water → Water reabsorbed → Concentrated urine.
  • Low Osmolarity (Excess Water): ADH secretion decreases → Permeability decreases → Large amounts of dilute urine excreted.
2. Mechanism for Excretig Dilute Urine

When there is excess water, the kidney can excrete up to 20 L/day of dilute urine (50 mOsm/L). This is achieved by reabsorbing solutes without reabsorbing water in the distal nephron.

Renal Response to Water Ingestion

After ingesting 1 liter of water, urine volume increases 6x within 45 minutes. Total solute excretion remains constant, but urine osmolarity drops from 600 to 100 mOsm/L. This prevents plasma osmolarity from decreasing markedly.

Mechanisms per Tubular Segment

  • Proximal Tubule: Fluid remains isosmotic (300 mOsm/L). Solutes and water reabsorbed in equal proportions.
  • Descending Loop of Henle: Water is reabsorbed by osmosis. Fluid reaches equilibrium with the hypertonic renal medulla (becoming more concentrated).
  • Ascending Loop of Henle: Avid reabsorption of Na+, K+, Cl- (especially in thick segment). This segment is impermeable to water (even with ADH). Fluid becomes dilute (~100 mOsm/L) by the time it reaches the distal tubule.
  • Distal & Collecting Tubules: In the absence of ADH, these segments are impermeable to water. Additional NaCl is reabsorbed, further diluting the fluid to as low as 50 mOsm/L.
3. Mechanism for Excreting Concentrated Urine

Essential for survival on land to match fluid loss from lungs, GI tract, skin, and kidneys. The human kidney can concentrate urine to 1200-1400 mOsm/L (4-5x plasma).

Comparison: Australian hopping mouse can concentrate to 10,000 mOsm/L (survives without drinking). Beavers only 500 mOsm/L.

Obligatory Urine Volume

The minimal volume required to excrete the daily metabolic waste and ingested electrolytes (approx. 600 mOsm/day for a 70kg person).

600 mOsm/day ÷ 1200 mOsm/L = 0.5 L/day

This explains dehydration from drinking seawater:

  • Seawater osmolarity = 1200 mOsm/L (mostly NaCl).
  • To excrete 1L of seawater (1200 mOsm), the kidney needs 1L of urine.
  • BUT, the kidney must also excrete urea (600 mOsm load).
  • Max NaCl excretion is only about 600 mOsm/L if urea is included.
  • Result: For every 1L of seawater drunk, 1.5L of urine is needed, causing a net loss of 0.5L fluid.

Urine Specific Gravity

Used clinically to estimate concentration. Measures weight of solutes (depends on number AND size of molecules), whereas osmolarity depends only on number.

  • Normal Range: 1.002 to 1.028 g/ml.
  • Rises 0.001 for every 35-40 mOsm/L increase.
  • Can be falsely high if large molecules (glucose, radiocontrast, antibiotics) are present.
4. Countercurrent Multiplier Mechanism

Requirements for Concentrated Urine

  1. High ADH levels: Allows distal segments to reabsorb water.
  2. Hyperosmotic Renal Medulla: Provides the osmotic gradient.

The renal medullary interstitium accumulates solutes (up to 1200-1400 mOsm/L). This is produced by the Countercurrent Multiplier.

Loop of Henle Characteristics

  • Descending Limb: Permeable to water, impermeable to solutes. Fluid equilibrates with interstitium.
  • Ascending Limb (Thick): Actively pumps Na+, K+, Cl- out. Impermeable to water. Can establish a 200 mOsm/L gradient.

Steps of the Multiplier (How it works)

  1. Assume loop is filled with 300 mOsm/L fluid.
  2. Active pump in Thick Ascending Limb creates 200 mOsm/L gradient (Interstitium rises to 400).
  3. Fluid in Descending Limb equilibrates with interstitium (Water moves out).
  4. Flow pushes hyperosmotic fluid toward the tip.
  5. Active transport in Ascending Limb adds more solute to interstitium.
  6. Repetition "multiplies" the gradient, reaching 1200-1400 mOsm/L at the tip.

Net Effect: Repetitive reabsorption of NaCl by the thick ascending limb and inflow of new NaCl from proximal tubule traps solutes in the medulla.

5. Role of Distal Tubule & Collecting Ducts

Fluid leaves the Loop of Henle dilute (100 mOsm/L). As it enters the cortex:

  • Early Distal Tubule: Further dilution (active NaCl transport, impermeable to water).
  • Cortical Collecting Tubule: Amount of water reabsorbed depends on ADH.
    - High ADH: Permeable to water. Water is reabsorbed into the cortex (preserving medullary osmolarity).
    - No ADH: Impermeable. Urine remains dilute.
  • Medullary Collecting Ducts: With high ADH, water is reabsorbed into the medulla until urine reaches equilibrium with interstitium (1200 mOsm/L). Thus, a small volume of concentrated urine is excreted.
6. Role of Urea

Urea contributes 40-50% (500-600 mOsm/L) of the medullary osmolarity. Unlike NaCl, urea is passively reabsorbed.

Mechanism of Urea Trapping

  1. Ascending Loop, Distal, and Cortical Collecting tubules are impermeable to urea.
  2. As water is reabsorbed (due to ADH), urea concentration in the tubule rises efficiently.
  3. Inner Medullary Collecting Ducts: High urea concentration + ADH activation of transporters UT-A1 and UT-A3 causes urea to diffuse into the interstitium.
  4. Recirculation: Urea diffuses into the thin loop of Henle (via UT-A2) and passes back up through the distal tubules, recirculating multiple times.

Significance: High protein diets (more urea) improve urine concentrating ability. Malnutrition reduces it.

7. Vasa Recta & Countercurrent Exchange

Blood flow to the medulla must supply metabolic needs without washing out the solute gradient. The Vasa Recta do this via two features:

  1. Low Blood Flow: Less than 5% of total renal blood flow.
  2. Countercurrent Exchange: The U-shape of the capillaries.

Mechanism:

  • Descending Limb: Blood becomes concentrated (water loss, solute entry).
  • Ascending Limb: Blood becomes less concentrated (water entry, solute loss).

The U-shape prevents dissipation of the gradient. Increased medullary blood flow (vasodilators or high BP) can wash out the gradient and reduce concentrating ability.

8. Summary of Osmolarity Changes
  • Proximal Tubule: 65% reabsorbed. High water permeability (AQP-1). Remains isosmotic (300 mOsm/L).
  • Descending Loop: Water absorbed (AQP-1). Conc rises to 1200 mOsm/L.
  • Thin Ascending: Impermeable to water. Passive NaCl reabsorption. Conc decreases. Urea diffuses in.
  • Thick Ascending: Impermeable to water. Active NaCl transport. Dilutes fluid to 100 mOsm/L.
  • Late Distal/Cortical Collecting: ADH dependent. Water reabsorbed if ADH present. Urea impermeable (conc rises).
  • Inner Medullary Duct: ADH dependent. Water reabsorbed. Urea reabsorbed (transporters). Final urine 1200 mOsm/L.

Note: Large quantities of dilute urine can be excreted without increasing sodium excretion by decreasing ADH.

9. Quantifying Concentration (Clearance)

Osmolar Clearance (Cosm)

The volume of plasma cleared of solutes each minute.

Cosm = (Uosm × V) / Posm

Free Water Clearance (CH2O)

Rate at which solute-free water is excreted.

CH2O = V - Cosm = V - (Uosm × V / Posm)
  • Positive Value: Excess water excreted (Dilute urine).
  • Negative Value: Excess solutes removed/Water conserved (Concentrated urine).
10. Disorders of Concentrating Ability
Central Diabetes Insipidus:

Failure to produce ADH (head injury, infection, congenital). Distal segments cannot reabsorb water. Urine volume can exceed 15 L/day. Thirst mechanism prevents dehydration if water is available.
Treatment: Synthetic ADH analogue Desmopressin (selectively acts on V2 receptors).

Nephrogenic Diabetes Insipidus:

Normal ADH levels, but kidneys cannot respond. Causes: Loop of Henle failure, Drug toxicity (Lithium, Tetracyclines), Renal disease.
Diagnosis: No response to Desmopressin injection.
Treatment: Correct underlying cause. Thiazide diuretics + Low sodium diet (to reduce polyuria).

11. Osmoreceptor-ADH Feedback System

Regulation of ECF osmolarity and Sodium concentration are closely linked (Sodium = 94% of ECF solutes).

Estimating Plasma Osmolarity

Posm = 2.1 × Plasma Sodium

More exact: 2 × [Na+] + [Glucose] + [Urea].

The Feedback Loop

  1. Increased Osmolarity (Water Deficit) causes shrinkage of Osmoreceptor cells (Anterior Hypothalamus/Supraoptic nuclei).
  2. Shrinkage signals supraoptic nuclei to fire.
  3. Signals travel to Posterior Pituitary.
  4. ADH is released from secretory granules.
  5. ADH increases water permeability in distal nephron.
  6. Water is conserved.
12. Anatomy of ADH & Thirst Control

ADH Synthesis

Synthesized in Supraoptic (5/6ths) and Paraventricular (1/6th) nuclei of hypothalamus. Transported down axons to posterior pituitary. Released in response to calcium entry.

AV3V Region

Located along the anteroventral third ventricle. Contains:

  • Subfornical Organ (Upper).
  • Organum Vasculosum of the Lamina Terminalis (Lower).
  • Median Preoptic Nucleus (Between).

These regions lack the blood-brain barrier, allowing them to detect ion changes in blood rapidly. They control ADH, Thirst, and Sodium Appetite.

13. Cardiovascular & Other Stimuli

ADH is also stimulated by:

  1. Arterial Baroreceptor Reflexes: (Carotid sinus/Aortic Arch). Respond to low BP.
  2. Cardiopulmonary Reflexes: (Atria). Respond to low blood volume.

Sensitivity: ADH is much more sensitive to Osmolarity (1% change triggers release) than to Volume (needs 10% change).

Table: Other Stimuli

  • Increase ADH: Nausea (potent, 100x increase), Nicotine, Morphine.
  • Decrease ADH: Alcohol, Clonidine, Haloperidol.
14. Thirst Mechanism

Thirst is the conscious desire for water. The "Thirst Center" (AV3V region + Preoptic nucleus) responds to:

  1. Increased Osmolarity: Intracellular dehydration of thirst centers.
  2. Decreased Volume/Pressure: Independent of osmolarity (e.g., hemorrhage).
  3. Angiotensin II: Acts on Subfornical organ.
  4. Dry Mouth: Provides immediate but temporary relief.
  5. GI Distention: Metering mechanism to prevent overhydration (takes 30-60 mins for water to absorb).

Threshold for Drinking: Sodium increase of 2 mEq/L.

15. Integrated Control & Salt Appetite

ADH and Thirst work in parallel. If one fails, the other can compensate (if fluid is available). If both fail, control is lost.

Role of Angiotensin II and Aldosterone

They increase sodium reabsorption, but also water reabsorption. Therefore, they increase the Volume/Quantity of sodium but have little effect on the Concentration.

The ADH-Thirst system far overshadows Angiotensin/Aldosterone for regulating Sodium Concentration.

Salt Appetite

Behavioral drive to obtain salt. Stimuli:

  1. Sodium Deficit (e.g., Addison's disease/Low Aldosterone).
  2. Decreased Blood Volume/Pressure.

Neuronal centers are in the AV3V region (same as thirst).
