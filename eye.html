Chapter 23: Eye

General Structure
The Eyes: Pair of complex photosensitive organs consisting of a globe called the eyeball.
Location: Located in a bony cavity within the skull called the orbit.

Layers (Coats)

The wall of the eyeball is composed of three concentric layers:

  • Outer fibrous coat (or corneoscleral layer).
  • Middle vascular coat (or uveal layer).
  • Innermost neural coat (or retinal layer).

I. The Fibrous Coat (Corneoscleral Layer)

Composition: Consists mainly of collagenous fibers.
Components: Comprises the cornea and sclera.
Junction: The corneoscleral junction is known as the corneal limbus.

A. Cornea

Forms the anterior one-sixth of the fibrous coat.
Appearance: Clear and transparent (unlike the opaque sclera).
Vascularity: No blood vessels are present.
Dimensions: Slightly thicker than sclera; 0.8 mm in the center and 1.0 mm at the periphery.

Microscopic Structure (5 Layers)

  1. Corneal Epithelium
    • Outermost layer.
    • Type: Typical stratified squamous nonkeratinized epithelium.
    • Layers: Composed of 5–7 layers of cells.
    • Renewal: Undergoes constant renewal; basal cells proliferate, superficial cells desquamate.
    • Surface: Superficial cells have projections (microvilli and microplicae) to stabilize tear film.
    • Innervation: Richly supplied by free nerve endings (extremely sensitive).
    • Stem Cells: Limbus serves as a reservoir for limbal stem cells which add new cells to the basal layer.
  2. Bowman's Membrane (Anterior Limiting Membrane)
    • Location: Acellular layer between corneal epithelium and stroma.
    • Function: Serves as basement membrane for corneal epithelium.
    • Dimensions: 8–12 µm thick.
    • Appearance: Homogeneous under light microscope.
    • Composition: Network of fine type I collagen fibrils embedded in ground substance.
    • Regeneration: Does not regenerate; damage leads to opaque scar formation.
  3. Corneal Stroma (Substantia Propria)
    • Size: Thickest layer (constitutes about 90% of corneal thickness).
    • Composition: About 60 lamellae of type I collagen fibers.
    • Ground Substance: Proteoglycans like lumican, keratan sulfate, and chondroitin sulfate.
    • Orientation: Adjacent lamellae oriented at right angles to each other (orthogonal array).
    • Cells: Flattened fibroblast-like cells called keratocytes lie between lamellae with slender cytoplasmic processes extending between collagen fibers.
    • Factors for Transparency:
      • Complete absence of blood vessels.
      • Uniform, orthogonal array of collagen fiber lamellae.
      • Strict regulation of water content (accumulation of water causes opacity).
  4. Descemet's Membrane (Posterior Limiting Membrane)
    • Appearance: Thin, acellular, homogeneous, highly refractile layer.
    • Nature: Modified basement membrane for corneal endothelium.
    • Thickness: 5 µm in newborns; thickens with age to 17 µm in old people.
  5. Corneal Endothelium
    • Type: Simple squamous epithelium lining the posterior surface.
    • Organelles: Many mitochondria, Golgi apparatus, RER, and numerous pinocytotic vesicles.

B. Limbus

Definition: Border between transparent cornea and opaque sclera.
Dimensions: Anatomically about 1.5 mm wide band.

  • Structural Transitions:
    • Corneal epithelium → Continuous with conjunctival epithelium.
    • Bowman's membrane → Disappears.
    • Corneal stroma → Continuous with scleral connective tissue.
    • Descemet's membrane and endothelium → Replaced by trabecular meshwork.
  • Trabecular Meshwork: Endothelium-lined, irregular channels separated by connective tissue trabeculae.
  • Canal of Schlemm (Scleral Venous Sinus):
    • Ring-like vascular channel in the posterior part of the corneoscleral junction.
    • Connected to episcleral veins by multiple connector vessels.
    • Drains aqueous humor from trabecular meshwork.

C. Sclera

Forms the posterior five-sixths of the fibrous coat.
Appearance: Opaque white. Thickness: Average 0.5 mm.
Composition: Dense fibrous tissue. Attachments: Tendons of extraocular muscles inserted into anterior region.

Layers (from without inwards):

  1. Episclera: Thin layer of loose connective tissue. Connects sclera proper to fascial sheath of eyeball. Contains system of small veins called episcleral veins.
  2. Sclera Proper: Composed of flat bundles of type I collagen fibers. Arrangement: Parallel to surface but intersect in irregular, complicated manner. Content: Collagen bundles, elastic fibers, moderate ground substance, few fibroblasts.
  3. Suprachoroidal Lamina (Lamina Fusca): Zone of transition between sclera and choroid. Composition: Delicate collagenous and elastic fibers. Cells: Many fibroblasts, macrophages, and melanocytes.

II. The Vascular Coat (Uvea)

Location: Between fibrous coat and neural coat. Synonyms: Tunica vasculosa, uveal layer, uvea. Parts: Choroid, Ciliary Body, Iris.

A. Choroid

Thin, highly vascular, dark brown sheet forming posterior part of uvea.
Location: Between sclera and retina.
Thickness: 0.25 mm posteriorly; thins to 0.1 mm anteriorly.
Layers:

  • Choroid Stroma: Loose meshwork of type I collagen and elastic fibers. Numerous melanocytes, macrophages, fibroblasts. Main vessels run here.
  • Choriocapillary Layer: Plexus of unusually large fenestrated capillaries.
  • Bruch's Membrane (Lamina Vitrea): Thin, shiny, homogeneous layer bordering retinal pigment epithelium. 2–4 µm thick.
    • 5 Sub-layers (EM): Basal lamina of choriocapillary endothelium, External collagen, Elastic meshwork, Internal collagen, Basal lamina of RPE.

B. Ciliary Body

Thickened extension of uveal layer anterior to ora serrata. 6 mm wide ring.
Zones of Inner Surface:

  • Pars Plana: Posterior 2/3, smooth, darkly pigmented.
  • Pars Plicata: Anterior 1/3, pale, bears 70–80 ciliary processes.
Zonular Fibers: Radiate from ciliary processes to lens (suspensory ligament).

Layers of Ciliary Body

  1. Ciliary Muscle: Smooth muscle. Forms bulk. Groups: Longitudinal, radial, circular.
  2. Ciliary Stroma: Loose CT, fenestrated capillaries (concentrated in processes).
  3. Ciliary Epithelium (2 Layers):
    • Outer Pigmented Layer: Continuation of RPE. Cuboidal, melanin-filled.
    • Inner Nonpigmented Layer: Continuation of sensory retina. Columnar, unpigmented. Sodium pumps. Tight junctions (Blood-Aqueous Barrier).

Aqueous Humor: Plasma filtrate, protein-free. Secreted by ciliary epithelium. Flow: Post chamber → Pupil → Ant chamber → Trabecular meshwork → Canal of Schlemm → Scleral veins.

C. Iris

Contractile diaphragm dividing aqueous compartment into anterior/posterior chambers.
Pupil: Circular aperture.
Microscopic Layers:

  • Iris Stroma (Anterior): Derived from mesenchyme. Contains Anterior Border Layer (collagen, fibroblasts, melanocytes).
    • Sphincter Pupillae: Smooth muscle ring around pupil (Parasympathetic).
    • Dilator Pupillae: Myoepithelium extending from iris root to sphincter (Sympathetic).
  • Two Epithelial Layers (Posterior): Derived from neuroectoderm.
    • Posterior Pigment Epithelium: Faces posterior chamber. Packed with melanin.
    • Anterior Pigment Myoepithelium: Basal part contains contractile myofilaments (dilator pupillae).

Eye Color: Depends on number of melanocytes in stroma (Blue=small, Brown=high).

III. The Neural Coat (Retina)

Innermost layer. Developed from optic cup (Outer wall → Pigment epithelium; Inner wall → Neural retina).

A. Retinal Pigment Epithelium (RPE)

Single layer of low columnar cells. Basal nuclei. Tight junctions. Apical processes interdigitate with photoreceptors. Abundant melanin, mitochondria, SER, lysosomes, phagosomes.
Functions: Light absorption, Vitamin A storage, Phagocytosis of shed discs, Blood-Retina Barrier.

B. Layers of Neural Retina (10 Layers)

  1. Retinal Pigment Epithelium.
  2. Layer of Rods and Cones (Outer/Inner segments).
  3. Outer Limiting Layer (Junctions between Muller cells and photoreceptors).
  4. Outer Nuclear Layer (Nuclei of rods/cones).
  5. Outer Plexiform Layer (Synapses: Photoreceptors + Bipolar + Horizontal).
  6. Inner Nuclear Layer (Nuclei of Bipolar, Horizontal, Amacrine, Muller).
  7. Inner Plexiform Layer (Synapses: Bipolar + Ganglion + Amacrine).
  8. Ganglion Cell Layer (Ganglion cell bodies).
  9. Optic Nerve Fiber Layer (Axons of ganglion cells).
  10. Inner Limiting Layer (Basement membrane of Muller cells).

C. Elements of Neural Retina

  • Photoreceptors (1st Order):
    • Rods (120 million): Thin, cylindrical. Rhodopsin. Discs independent of membrane. Vision in dim light.
    • Cones (6 million): Conical. Iodopsin (Red, Green, Blue). Discs attached to membrane. Acuity/Color.
  • Direct Conducting Neurons:
    • Bipolar Cells (2nd Order): Diffuse or Monosynaptic (Midget).
    • Ganglion Cells (3rd Order): Large multipolar. Axons form optic nerve.
  • Association Neurons: Horizontal Cells, Amacrine Cells, Interplexiform Cells.
  • Supporting Cells: Muller Cells (Retinal Gliocytes), Astrocytes, Microglia.

D. Specialized Areas

Fovea Centralis: Depression at posterior pole. Most precise acuity. Contains only cones. Inner retinal layers displaced.

IV. Lens & V. Accessory Structures

Lens

Transparent, biconvex. Suspended by zonular fibers.

  • Capsule: Type IV collagen. Thick basal lamina.
  • Subcapsular Epithelium: Cuboidal, anterior only. Elongate at equator to form fibers.
  • Lens Substance: Elongated prismatic fibers. Filled with Crystallins. Nucleus and organelles lost.

Vitreous Body

Jelly-like. 99% water, collagen type XI, hyaluronic acid. Cells: Hyalocytes.

Conjunctiva

Mucous membrane. Palpebral, Bulbar, Fornix. Epithelium: Stratified columnar with goblet cells.

Eyelids

  • Skin, Subcutaneous (no fat), Muscle (Orbicularis oculi), Tarsal Plate (Dense fibrous), Conjunctiva.
  • Glands: Tarsal/Meibomian (Sebaceous in tarsal plate), Zeis (Sebaceous w/ lashes), Moll (Modified sweat).

Lacrimal Apparatus

Gland: Compound tubuloacinar (serous). Myoepithelial cells present.
Passages: Canaliculi (St. Squamous) → Sac & Duct (Pseudostratified ciliated columnar with goblet cells).

VI. Clinical Applications

  • Glaucoma Increased IOP due to defective drainage.
  • Retinal Detachment Separation of neural retina from RPE.
  • Presbyopia Loss of accommodation with age.
  • Cataract Opacity of lens (denatured crystallins).
  • Stye (Hordeolum) Infection of Zeis, Moll, or lash follicle.
  • Chalazion Inflammation of blocked Meibomian gland.
  • Conjunctivitis Pink eye.

Short Essay Questions (SEQs)

Q1. Draw and label the microscopic structure of the Cornea. Explain the factors responsible for its transparency.

Answer Key Points

Layers: Epithelium, Bowman's, Stroma (90%), Descemet's, Endothelium.
Transparency Factors: Avascularity, Orthogonal array of collagen, dehydration mechanism (sodium pumps).

Q2. Enumerate the 10 layers of the Retina from outside inwards. Name the cells found in the Inner Nuclear Layer.

Answer Key Points

Layers: RPE, Rods/Cones, OLL, ONL, OPL, INL, IPL, Ganglion, Nerve Fiber, ILL.
INL Cells: Bipolar, Horizontal, Amacrine, Muller, Interplexiform.

Q3. Describe the histology of the Ciliary Body. What constitutes the Blood-Aqueous barrier?

Answer Key Points

Structure: Ciliary muscle (longitudinal, radial, circular), Stroma, Epithelium (Outer pigmented, Inner non-pigmented).
Barrier: Tight junctions between cells of the inner nonpigmented ciliary epithelium.

Q4. Compare and contrast the histological structure of the Eyelid skin vs. general body skin.

Answer Key Points

Eyelid skin is very thin. Subcutaneous tissue contains no fat. Contains unique glands (Moll, Zeis, Meibomian).

High Yield UHS MCQs

Which layer of the retina contains the cell bodies of the First Order Neurons of the visual pathway?
  • A. Inner Nuclear Layer
  • B. Outer Nuclear Layer
  • C. Ganglion Cell Layer
  • D. Retinal Pigment Epithelium
Answer

B. Outer Nuclear Layer (Rods and Cones are 1st order)

The canal of Schlemm is located at the:
  • A. Fovea Centralis
  • B. Corneoscleral junction (Limbus)
  • C. Optic Disc
  • D. Pars Plana
Answer

B. Corneoscleral junction (Limbus)

Which of the following is responsible for regeneration of the corneal epithelium?
  • A. Keratocytes
  • B. Corneal Endothelium
  • C. Limbal Stem Cells
  • D. Bowman's Membrane
Answer

C. Limbal Stem Cells

In the eyelid, the glands of Moll are modified:
  • A. Sebaceous glands
  • B. Apocrine sweat glands
  • C. Eccrine sweat glands
  • D. Lacrimal glands
Answer

B. Apocrine sweat glands (Often simply termed modified sweat glands in text, specific type is apocrine)
