CHAPTER 25: THE BODY FLUID COMPARTMENTS

(Extracellular and Intracellular Fluids; Edema)

I. GENERAL PRINCIPLES OF FLUID HOMEOSTASIS

The maintenance of a relatively constant volume and stable composition of the body fluids is essential for homeostasis.

1. Daily Intake of Water

Water enters the body via two sources.

  • A. Ingested Water: Fluid intake is regulated by the thirst mechanism and habits.
    • Average: 2100 ml/day.
    • Variability: Changes significantly with climate, culture, and physical exertion.
  • B. Metabolic Water: Produced by the oxidation of carbohydrates within mitochondria.
    • Average: 200 ml/day.

TOTAL INTAKE: 2300 ml/day.

2. Daily Loss of Body Water

Water is eliminated via four routes.

A. Insensible Water Loss (700 ml/day)

  • Definition: Continuous loss of water by evaporation that we are consciously unaware of.
  • Mechanism 1: Skin Diffusion (300‚Äì400 ml/day)
    • Occurs independently of sweat glands.
    • The Cholesterol Barrier: The cornified layer of the skin is filled with cholesterol. This limits diffusion.
    • Burn Injury: If the cornified layer is lost (burns), evaporation increases 10-fold (up to 3‚Äì5 Liters/day). This is a major cause of death in burn victims due to shock.
  • Mechanism 2: Respiratory Evaporation (300‚Äì400 ml/day)
    • Air entering the respiratory tract is heated to body temperature (37¬∞C) and saturated with moisture.
    • Vapor Pressure: At body temperature, water vapor pressure is 47 mmHg.
    • Cold Weather Physiology: Atmospheric vapor pressure decreases with temperature (to nearly 0 mmHg in winter). The body must add large amounts of water to humidify this dry air, which is then exhaled. This causes the sensation of a dry throat and lungs in winter.

B. Fluid Loss in Sweat (Variable)

  • Normal: ~100 ml/day.
  • Heavy Exercise/Heat: Can increase to 1‚Äì2 Liters/hour.
  • Composition: Sweat is hypotonic (mostly water, less salt). Excessive sweating leads to depletion of ECF volume and increase in Osmolarity (Hypertonic Dehydration).

C. Water Loss in Feces

  • Normal: 100 ml/day.
  • Pathology: In severe diarrhea, loss can be several liters, leading to rapid fatal dehydration.

D. Water Loss by Kidneys (Urine)

  • Normal: ~1400 ml/day.
  • Range:
    • Minimum: 0.5 L/day. (Limited by the kidney's concentrating ability and the obligatory solute load).
    • Maximum: 20 L/day. (In cases of massive water ingestion).
  • Key Concept: The kidney adjusts volume without significantly altering the excretion of total solutes.

II. BODY FLUID COMPARTMENTS

1. Total Body Water (TBW)

  • Reference Man (70 kg): Water is 60% of body weight (‚âà 42 Liters).
  • Females: Water is 50% of body weight.
    • Reason: Females have more subcutaneous adipose tissue. Fat is effectively anhydrous (water-free). Therefore, the fraction of water relative to total weight is lower.
  • Infants: High TBW (70‚Äì75%).
    • Clinical: Infants are extremely vulnerable to dehydration due to high surface area-to-weight ratio and high metabolic rate.
  • Aging: TBW percentage decreases with age (due to muscle atrophy and fat gain).

2. Intracellular Fluid (ICF)

  • Definition: Fluid inside the ~75 trillion cells.
  • Volume: 40% of body weight (‚âà 28 Liters).
  • Ratio: 2/3 of TBW.
  • Composition: High in K+, Mg++, PO4---, Proteins. Low in Na+, Cl-.

3. Extracellular Fluid (ECF)

  • Definition: Fluid outside the cell.
  • Volume: 20% of body weight (‚âà 14 Liters).
  • Ratio: 1/3 of TBW.
  • Sub-compartments:
    • Interstitial Fluid (ISF): 11 Liters (3/4 of ECF).
    • Plasma: 3 Liters (1/4 of ECF).
    • Transcellular Fluid: ~1‚Äì2 Liters (CSF, intraocular, synovial, peritoneal, pericardial). These are separated by epithelium and do not exchange freely, but are technically ECF.

4. Blood Volume

  • Blood is a mixed compartment (Plasma = ECF; RBCs = ICF).
  • Total Volume: ~7% of body weight (5 Liters).
  • Plasma: 60% of blood.
  • Cells: 40% of blood.

5. Hematocrit (Packed Cell Volume)

  • Definition: The fraction of blood volume occupied by RBCs.
  • Values: Men (0.40), Women (0.36).
  • "True" vs. Venous Hematocrit: In clinical centrifugation, about 3-4% of plasma is trapped between cells. Therefore, true hematocrit is 0.96 √ó measured hematocrit.
  • Pathology:
    • Severe Anemia: 0.10.
    • Polycythemia: 0.65.

III. CHEMICAL COMPOSITION OF FLUIDS

1. The Donnan Effect (Gibbs-Donnan Equilibrium)

Because plasma and interstitial fluid are separated by a capillary membrane that is permeable to ions but impermeable to proteins:

  • Plasma proteins have a net Negative Charge.
  • They bind Cations (Na+, K+). Result: Plasma cation concentration is ~2% higher than ISF.
  • They repel Anions (Cl-). Result: ISF anion concentration is slightly higher than Plasma.

IV. MEASUREMENT OF FLUID VOLUMES

1. Indicator-Dilution Principle

Volume (V) = (C1 √ó V1) / C2

(Where C1V1 is mass injected, C2 is final concentration. Subtract amount excreted from numerator).

2. Requirements for Indicators

  • Disperse evenly.
  • Stay in the compartment.
  • Non-toxic.
  • Not metabolized.

3. Specific Markers (MCQ Data)

  • Total Body Water: Tritium (3H2O), Deuterium (2H2O), Antipyrine (highly lipid soluble, penetrates cells quickly).
  • Extracellular Fluid:
    • Inulin (large polysaccharide, doesn't enter cells). Gold standard.
    • Radioactive Sodium (22Na), Radioactive Chloride, Thiosulfate.
    • Sodium Space: Sodium enters cells slightly, so sodium space > Inulin space.
  • Plasma Volume:
    • Evans Blue Dye (T-1824) (binds albumin).
    • 125I-Albumin.
  • Blood Volume:
    • Calculated: Plasma Volume / (1 ‚àí Hematocrit).
    • Measured: 51Cr-labeled Red Blood Cells.

4. Calculated Volumes

  • ICF = TBW - ECF.
  • ISF = ECF - Plasma.

V. OSMOSIS AND OSMOTIC PRESSURE

1. Definitions

  • Osmole: Number of osmotically active particles.
    • Glucose = 1 particle per mole.
    • NaCl = 2 particles (Na+ + Cl-).
    • Na2SO4 = 3 particles (2Na+ + SO4--).
  • Osmolality: Osmoles per kg of water.
  • Osmolarity: Osmoles per Liter of solution. (In dilute body fluids, difference is <1%, so Osmolarity is used clinically).

2. Calculation of Osmotic Pressure (Van't Hoff's Law)

  • Osmotic pressure is determined solely by the number of particles, not their mass.
  • At 37¬∞C, 1 mOsm/L = 19.3 mmHg pressure.

3. Total Body Osmolarity

  • Calculated Osmolarity: Sum of cations + anions + non-electrolytes = 300 mOsm/L.
  • Corrected Osmolar Activity: Because ions attract each other (Van der Waals forces), they don't act entirely independently.
  • Correction factor: 0.93.
  • Actual Osmolarity: 300 √ó 0.93 = 280 mOsm/L.
  • Total Osmotic Pressure: 280 √ó 19.3 = 5443 mmHg.

VI. FLUID SHIFTS (DARROW-YANNET LOGIC)

Principle: Cell membranes are permeable to water (instant flow) but impermeable to solutes (NaCl stays in ECF). Equilibrium occurs when ICF Osmolarity = ECF Osmolarity.

1. Isotonic Infusion (0.9% NaCl)

Isotonic = 280 mOsm/L.

  • Effect: ECF Volume Increases. Osmolarity No Change. ICF Volume No Change.

2. Hypertonic Infusion (3% NaCl)

  • Immediate: ECF Osmolarity increases.
  • Response: Water moves ICF ‚Üí ECF.
  • Final State: ECF Vol: ‚Üë‚Üë (Initial volume + fluid from cells). ICF Vol: ‚Üì. Osmolarity: ‚Üë (in both compartments).

3. Hypotonic Infusion (Pure Water / 0.45% NaCl)

  • Immediate: ECF Osmolarity decreases.
  • Response: Water moves ECF ‚Üí ICF.
  • Final State: ECF Vol: ‚Üë. ICF Vol: ‚Üë (Swelling). Osmolarity: ‚Üì (in both compartments).

VII. SODIUM DISORDERS (HYPO/HYPERNATREMIA)

1. Hyponatremia (Na < 142 mEq/L)

  • Pathophysiology: ECF is hypotonic. Water enters cells. Brain edema is the critical danger.
  • Causes:
    • Loss of NaCl (Hypo-osmotic dehydration): Diarrhea, Vomiting, Diuretics, Addison‚Äôs Disease (Aldosterone deficiency).
    • Retention of Water (Hypo-osmotic overhydration): SIADH (Lung tumors secreting ADH).
  • Brain Adaptation (Crucial Detail):
    • In acute hyponatremia, brain swells causing herniation/coma.
    • In chronic hyponatremia (slow onset), glial cells pump out ions (K+, Cl-) and organic solutes (Glutamate) to lower intracellular osmolarity. This stops the swelling.
    • Time Course: Adaptation takes 24 hours.
  • Treatment Risk: If chronic hyponatremia is corrected rapidly with hypertonic saline, the brain cannot uptake solutes fast enough. The cells shrink rapidly, pulling away from the myelin sheath.
  • Result: Osmotic Demyelination Syndrome (Central Pontine Myelinolysis).

2. Hypernatremia (Na > 142 mEq/L)

  • Pathophysiology: ECF is hypertonic. Cells shrink.
  • Causes:
    • Water Loss: Diabetes Insipidus (Central = No ADH; Nephrogenic = Kidney resistant to ADH), Excessive Sweating.
    • Sodium Excess: Hypertonic saline, Cushing‚Äôs Disease (Aldosterone excess).
  • Defense: Intense Thirst. Hypernatremia is rare in conscious patients with access to water.

VIII. EDEMA

1. Intracellular Edema

  • Cause A: Metabolic Depression: Ischemia ‚Üí No ATP ‚Üí Na-K Pump fails ‚Üí Na accumulates inside ‚Üí Water follows.
  • Cause B: Inflammation: Increases membrane permeability.

2. Extracellular Edema (Mechanisms)

  • A. Increased Capillary Pressure (Pc):
    • Heart Failure (Right or Left).
    • Kidney Failure (Salt retention = high blood volume).
    • Venous pumps failure (Paralysis, Immobilization).
  • B. Decreased Plasma Proteins (œÄc):
    • Nephrotic Syndrome: Glomeruli lose negative charge ‚Üí Albuminuria. Edema starts when plasma protein < 2.5 g/dl.
    • Cirrhosis: Fibrosis constricts portal vein (Ascites) + Hepatocytes fail to make albumin.
    • Kwashiorkor: Protein malnutrition.
  • C. Increased Permeability: Immune (Histamine), Toxins, Burns, Vitamin C deficiency.
  • D. Lymphatic Blockage: Filariasis (Elephantiasis): Nematode Wuchereria bancrofti. Cancer/Surgery.

3. Safety Factors Against Edema (The Exact Math)

Why don't we swell with minor BP changes?

  • Low Compliance of Interstitium (Safety Factor = 3 mmHg):
    • Interstitial fluid pressure (Pif) is normally -3 mmHg.
    • The tissues contain Proteoglycan filaments. These act as a "spacer" and prevent fluid from flowing freely. The fluid exists as a Tissue Gel.
    • As long as pressure is negative, suction holds tissues together.
    • Pressure must rise from -3 to 0 before fluid becomes mobile.
  • Increased Lymph Flow (Safety Factor = 7 mmHg):
    • Lymphatics can handle 10-50x normal flow.
    • The terminal lymphatics have "anchoring filaments" that pull the valves open when tissues swell.
  • Protein Washout (Safety Factor = 7 mmHg):
    • High filtration ‚Üí high flow into interstitium ‚Üí washes interstitial proteins into lymph.
    • This lowers Interstitial Colloid Osmotic Pressure (œÄif), reducing the force drawing water out of capillaries.
  • TOTAL Safety Factor = 17 mmHg.

4. Pitting vs. Non-Pitting Edema

  • Pitting: Presence of "Free Fluid" (pressure > 0 mmHg). Fluid flows freely between spaces when pressed.
  • Non-Pitting: Cells are swollen or fluid is coagulated with Fibrinogen (typical of Lymphedema).

IX. FLUIDS IN POTENTIAL SPACES

1. Definition

Pleural, Pericardial, Peritoneal, and Synovial cavities.

2. Maintenance of "Empty" State

  • These spaces are lined by mesothelium.
  • Large lymphatic vessels drain these cavities directly.
  • This creates a Negative Pressure (Vacuum) that keeps the surfaces pulled together (e.g., lungs held against chest wall).

3. Effusion

  • Accumulation of fluid in these spaces.
  • Ascites: Large effusion in abdominal cavity. Caused by high pressure in liver capillaries (Cirrhosis) causing fluid to "weep" into the abdomen faster than lymphatics can remove it.
