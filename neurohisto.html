1. Nervous Tissue: Overview

  • The nervous system of the body is composed mainly of nervous tissue (also called nerve tissue or neural tissue).
  • Connective tissue also makes contributions by forming capsules of ganglia, tubular investments of nerves, and meninges.
  • Nervous tissue consists of two main components:
    1. Nerve cells (Neurons): Structural and functional units. Possess special physiologic properties of excitability and conductivity.
    2. Supporting cells: Protect, nourish, and maintain nerve cells. They are non-excitable and non-conducting.
      • In CNS: Collectively known as Neuroglia (Astrocytes, Oligodendrocytes, Microglia).
      • In PNS: Schwann cells (support nerve fibers) and Satellite cells (support neurons in ganglia).

Special Properties of Neurons

  • i. Excitability: Ability to respond to physical or chemical stimuli by generating an electrical signal called an electrical impulse.
  • ii. Conductivity: Ability to conduct this electrical signal to other neurons or effector cells (muscles/glands) located at variable distances. Conduction occurs along the processes.
  • iii. Ability to Secrete Neurotransmitters: When the impulse reaches the end of a process, the neuron secretes a chemical substance (neurotransmitter) that jumps the gap to stimulate the next cell.

2. The Nerve Cell Body (Soma)

  • Also called soma or perikaryon.
  • Contains the nucleus and cytoplasm.
  • Function: Serves as a nourishing center, supplies organelles/macromolecules to processes, and is receptive to excitatory/inhibitory stimuli.
  • Size: Varies considerably. Small (4 µm, granule cells of cerebellum) to Large (135 µm, anterior horn cells of spinal cord).
  • Shape: Depends on number/orientation of processes.
    • Unipolar: Globular.
    • Bipolar: Elongated/Fusiform.
    • Multipolar: Stellate, Pyramidal, or Spherical.

Nucleus

  • Most contain a single, large, pale-staining, vesicular nucleus.
  • Spherical and central in position.
  • Vesicular appearance: Indicates large amount of euchromatin (active protein synthesis).
  • Contains a relatively large, deeply basophilic nucleolus ("Owl's eye" appearance).

Cytoplasm

  • Nissl Bodies (Chromatophilic Substance): Clumps of basophilic material. Consist of abundant RER and free polyribosomes. Presence indicates high protein synthesis (e.g., neurotransmitters). Amount depends on functional state.
  • Golgi Apparatus: Well-developed, perinuclear. Packages proteins.
  • Mitochondria: Numerous, provide energy.
  • Lysosomes: Large number. Correlates with high turnover of membrane/components.
  • Cytoskeleton:
    • Neurofilaments: Intermediate filaments (10 nm). Silver impregnation makes them visible as neurofibrils.
    • Microtubules: Transport organelles/materials along the axon.
  • Inclusions: Lipofuscin pigment (yellowish-brown granules). Remnants of lysosomal activity. Accumulates with age.

3. Neuronal Processes (Neurites)

The processes form the most characteristic feature of neurons. Two types: Axons and Dendrites.

Dendrites

  • Nature: Afferent processes (receive impulses and convey them to the cell body).
  • Structure: Usually multiple, short, tapering, and branch repeatedly like a tree (Greek dendron = tree).
  • Content: Contain nearly all organelles found in soma (Nissl, mitochondria, cytoskeleton) except the Golgi apparatus.
  • Spines: Outer surface shows numerous small knob-like projections called dendritic spines. These are sites of synapses.
  • Function: Increase receptive area of the cell.

Axon

  • Nature: Single, long process. Carries impulse away from cell body.
  • Origin: Arises from a pyramid-shaped region of soma called the Axon Hillock.
  • Characteristics:
    • Uniform diameter and smooth contour (unlike tapering dendrites).
    • Length varies: Fraction of a mm to over a meter.
    • Does not contain Nissl substance or Golgi (indicating no protein synthesis in axon).
    • Contains mitochondria, microtubules, and neurofilaments.
  • Terminology: Cytoplasm = Axoplasm. Membrane = Axolemma.
  • Branching: Generally does not branch profusely, but may give off side branches (collaterals) at right angles.
  • Terminals: Ends by dividing into terminal branches (telodendria) which end in small knob-like expansions called boutons (make synaptic junctions).

4. Axonal Transport

Because the axon lacks protein-synthesizing machinery, materials must be transported from the perikaryon.

  • Anterograde Transport: Movement from perikaryon to axon terminals.
    • Carries: Proteins, lipids, mitochondria, synaptic vesicles.
    • Molecular Motor: Kinesin (associated with microtubules).
  • Retrograde Transport: Movement from axon terminals to perikaryon.
    • Carries: Worn-out mitochondria, damaged membranes for disposal/recycling.
    • Pathology: Viruses (Rabies, Herpes, Polio) and toxins (Tetanus) use this route to reach the soma.
    • Molecular Motor: Dynein.

5. Classification of Neurons

A. Morphological (Number of Processes)

  • 1. Multipolar Neurons: Single axon, multiple dendrites. Most common type in body (e.g., Pyramidal cells of cortex, Anterior horn cells of spinal cord).
  • 2. Bipolar Neurons: Single axon, single dendrite emerging from opposite poles. Rare (e.g., Retina, Olfactory epithelium, Vestibular/Cochlear ganglia).
  • 3. Unipolar (Pseudounipolar) Neurons: Single short process divides into T-shape. One branch acts as dendrite (periphery), one as axon (CNS). Found in Dorsal Root Ganglia (Sensory ganglia).
  • 4. Anaxonic Neurons: Multiple dendrites, no axon. Do not produce action potentials but regulate neighbors. Found in Retina and Brain.

B. Functional Classification

  • 1. Sensory (Afferent): Receive stimuli from environment/tissues and pass to CNS. Almost all are Unipolar.
  • 2. Motor (Efferent): Transmit impulses from CNS/Ganglia to effector cells (muscles/glands). Most are Multipolar.
  • 3. Interneurons: Form communicating/integrating circuits between sensory and motor neurons. 99% of neurons in CNS are interneurons.

6. Nerve Fibers

A nerve fiber consists of an axon and its coverings.

Myelinated Nerve Fibers

  • Surrounded by a layer of lipoprotein called myelin.
  • Myelin Sheath: An interrupted, insulating covering that facilitates saltatory conduction (increases velocity up to 100x). Highly refractile (appears white).
  • Formation (PNS): Formed by Schwann cells.
    • Schwann cell wraps its plasma membrane spirally around the axon.
    • Outer layer containing nucleus/cytoplasm is called Neurilemma (Sheath of Schwann).
  • Formation (CNS): Formed by Oligodendrocytes. One oligodendrocyte myelinates multiple axons. No neurilemma.
  • Nodes of Ranvier: Gaps in the myelin sheath between adjacent Schwann cells. Axon is naked here. Site of ion exchange for impulse.
  • Internode: Segment of myelin between two nodes. Corresponds to one Schwann cell.
  • Schmidt-Lanterman Clefts: Areas where Schwann cell cytoplasm is trapped within the myelin layers (look like funnel-shaped interruptions).

Unmyelinated Nerve Fibers

  • Do not have a myelin sheath.
  • PNS: Axons invaginate into the cytoplasm of Schwann cells but no spiral wrapping occurs. One Schwann cell encloses many axons.
  • CNS: Run free without specific sheath.
  • Conduct impulses slowly (continuous conduction).

7. Synapses

Sites where nerve impulses are transmitted from one neuron to another (or to an effector).

Types

  • Electrical Synapses: Gap junctions connect cells. Ions pass directly. Very fast. Rare in mammals.
  • Chemical Synapses: Use neurotransmitters. Most common.

Structure of Chemical Synapse

  • 1. Presynaptic Element: Usually an axon terminal (bouton). Contains Synaptic Vesicles filled with neurotransmitter (e.g., Acetylcholine). Contains mitochondria.
  • 2. Synaptic Cleft: 20-30 nm gap separating the neurons.
  • 3. Postsynaptic Membrane: Cell membrane of the receiving neuron. Contains specific Receptors for the neurotransmitter. Often shows Postsynaptic Density.

Mechanism

  1. Nerve impulse reaches bouton.
  2. Voltage-gated Ca++ channels open. Ca++ influx.
  3. Vesicles fuse with presynaptic membrane (exocytosis).
  4. Neurotransmitter released into cleft.
  5. Binds to receptors on postsynaptic membrane.
  6. Opens ion channels (e.g., Na+), causing depolarization (excitation) or hyperpolarization (inhibition).

8. Nerve Endings & Receptors

A. Motor Nerve Endings (Efferent)

  • Somatic Efferent: Terminate on skeletal muscle.
    • Neuromuscular Junction (Motor End Plate): Axon loses myelin, branches, and invaginates into a trough on the muscle fiber surface (Synaptic trough). Muscle membrane forms Junctional folds to increase surface area. Neurotransmitter: Acetylcholine.
  • Visceral Efferent: Terminate on smooth muscle/glands. Postganglionic fibers form bulbous enlargements (varicosities) that release transmitter into the vicinity.

B. Sensory Nerve Endings (Receptors)

1. Unencapsulated (Free) Endings

  • Free Nerve Endings: Naked axons in epidermis/cornea/dermis. Senses: Pain, Temperature, Light touch.
  • Merkel Discs: Expanded disc-like ending associated with a Merkel cell in epidermis. Sense: Light touch (discriminative).
  • Peritrichial: Wrap around hair follicles. Sense: Hair movement.

2. Encapsulated Endings (CT Capsule)

  • Meissner's Corpuscles (Tactile): Ovoid, stacks of flattened Schwann cells within capsule. Location: Dermal papillae of hairless skin (fingertips). Function: Light touch.
  • Pacinian Corpuscles (Lamellated): Large, onion-like concentric lamellae. Location: Deep dermis, hypodermis, joints. Function: Pressure and Vibration.
  • Ruffini Corpuscles: Fusiform shape, collagen fibers inside. Location: Dermis. Function: Stretch and sustained pressure (Heat also mentioned in some texts, but mainly mechanoreceptor).
  • Krause End Bulbs: Spherical. Location: Conjunctiva, mucosa. Function: Cold/Touch.

3. Proprioceptors (Muscle Sense)

  • Muscle Spindles: Stretch receptors within skeletal muscle.
    • Encapsulated. Contains modified muscle fibers called Intrafusal fibers.
    • Nuclear Bag Fibers: Central dilatation with nuclei.
    • Nuclear Chain Fibers: Single row of nuclei.
    • Innervation: Annulospiral endings (Primary) and Flower spray endings (Secondary).
    • Function: Detect muscle length and rate of change (stretch reflex).
  • Golgi Tendon Organs: Found at muscle-tendon junctions. Collagen fibers enclosed in capsule. Detect Tension. Prevent muscle damage by inhibition.

9. Nerves and Ganglia

Peripheral Nerves

Bundles of nerve fibers held together by connective tissue.

  • Epineurium: Dense irregular CT surrounding the entire nerve.
  • Perineurium: Specialized CT surrounding each fascicle (bundle). Cells joined by tight junctions forming the Blood-Nerve Barrier. Maintains ionic milieu.
  • Endoneurium: Loose delicate CT surrounding individual nerve fibers (outside Schwann cell). Contains reticular fibers.

Ganglia

Aggregations of nerve cell bodies outside the CNS.

  • 1. Sensory Ganglia (e.g., Dorsal Root Ganglia):
    • Neurons: Pseudounipolar.
    • Arrangement: Grouped in clusters.
    • Nucleus: Central.
    • Satellite Cells: Form a complete ring around the soma.
    • Synapses: None. (Trophic function only).
  • 2. Autonomic Ganglia (Sympathetic/Parasympathetic):
    • Neurons: Multipolar.
    • Arrangement: Scattered.
    • Nucleus: Eccentric.
    • Satellite Cells: Incomplete/sparse covering.
    • Synapses: Present (Preganglionic fibers synapse here).

10. Neuroglia (CNS Supporting Cells)

More numerous than neurons. capable of mitosis.

  • 1. Astrocytes: Star-shaped. Most abundant.
    • Protoplasmic: Gray matter. Short branching processes.
    • Fibrous: White matter. Long straight processes.
    • Functions: Form Blood-Brain Barrier (perivascular feet), structural support, regulate ionic environment, form scar tissue (gliosis).
  • 2. Oligodendrocytes: Small, few processes. Form Myelin in CNS. One cell myelinates multiple axons.
  • 3. Microglia: Smallest, elongated nuclei. Derived from monocytes (Mesoderm). Function: Phagocytes (Defense/Macrophages of CNS). Active in inflammation.
  • 4. Ependymal Cells: Cuboidal/Columnar epithelial-like lining of ventricles and central canal.
    • Apical surface: Cilia (move CSF) and Microvilli (absorb).
    • Basal surface: No basement membrane (processes extend into brain).
    • Choroid Plexus epithelium: Modified ependymal cells that secrete CSF.

11. Meninges & CSF

Meninges (Coverings of Brain/Cord)

  • 1. Dura Mater (Pachymenx): Outermost, tough.
    • Periosteal Layer: Attached to skull bone.
    • Meningeal Layer: Inner. Forms folds (Falx cerebri).
    • Venous Sinuses: Formed between the two layers.
  • 2. Arachnoid Mater: Middle, web-like.
    • Avascular.
    • Subarachnoid Space: Between Arachnoid and Pia. Contains CSF and trabeculae.
    • Arachnoid Villi/Granulations: Protrusions into venous sinuses for CSF reabsorption.
  • 3. Pia Mater: Innermost, delicate, vascular. Adheres closely to brain surface (gyri/sulci).
  • Glymphatic System: Recently discovered waste clearance system. CSF flows into perivascular spaces, mixes with ISF, washes wastes (e.g., amyloid beta) into lymphatics of dura mater.

Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF)

  • Production: By Choroid Plexus (invaginations of pia mater + capillaries + modified ependymal cells) in ventricles.
  • Blood-CSF Barrier: Tight junctions between choroid plexus epithelial cells prevent leakage.
  • Circulation: Lateral Ventricles → 3rd Ventricle → 4th Ventricle → Subarachnoid Space → Arachnoid Villi → Venous Blood.
  • Functions: Shock absorber (water cushion), buoyancy, nutrient delivery, waste removal.
  • Clinical: Hydrocephalus: Accumulation of CSF (blockage or poor absorption). Causes head enlargement in infants.

12. Cerebral Cortex (Gray Matter)

Surface has convolutions (gyri) and fissures (sulci). 2-4 mm thick.

Neurons of Cortex

  • Pyramidal Cells: Cone-shaped soma. Apex points to surface. Long apical dendrite. Axon leaves from base. Main output cells. Giant Pyramidal cells of Betz found in motor cortex.
  • Non-Pyramidal (Stellate/Granule): Small, star-shaped. Interneurons.
  • Fusiform Cells: Spindle shaped. Deepest layer.
  • Horizontal Cells of Cajal: Superficial layer.
  • Martinotti Cells: Axon ascends to surface.

6 Layers of Neocortex (Superficial to Deep)

  1. I. Molecular Layer (Plexiform): Mostly fibers (dendrites/axons). Few Horizontal cells.
  2. II. External Granular Layer: Packed with small pyramidal and stellate cells.
  3. III. External Pyramidal Layer: Medium-sized pyramidal cells.
  4. IV. Internal Granular Layer: Packed with stellate (granule) cells. Main input station for sensory signals (Thalamus). Prominent in sensory cortex.
  5. V. Internal Pyramidal Layer (Ganglionic): Large pyramidal cells (including Betz cells). Main output station (motor fibers). Prominent in motor cortex.
  6. VI. Multiform Layer: Fusiform cells. Adjacent to white matter.

13. Cerebellar Cortex

Coordinates voluntary movements, balance, and muscle tone. Three distinct layers.

Layers (Superficial to Deep)

  1. 1. Molecular Layer: Few cells (Basket cells, Stellate cells). Mostly fibers (dendritic trees of Purkinje cells + Parallel fibers of granule cells).
  2. 2. Purkinje Cell Layer: Single row of huge, flask-shaped Purkinje Cells.
    • Dendrites: Fan-shaped arborization into molecular layer.
    • Axon: The only efferent output from cortex. Inhibitory (GABA). Projects to deep nuclei.
  3. 3. Granular Layer: Densely packed with small Granule cells.
    • Axons ascend to molecular layer, split into T, running as "Parallel fibers".
    • Also contains Golgi cells.
    • "Glomeruli": Synaptic complexes between mossy fibers and granule dendrites.

Afferent Fibers (Input)

  • Mossy Fibers: From spinal cord/brainstem. End in granular layer (Glomeruli). Excitatory.
  • Climbing Fibers: From Inferior Olivary Nucleus. Wrap around Purkinje dendrites like a vine. Excitatory.

14. Spinal Cord

  • Gray Matter (Inner): H-shaped or Butterfly-shaped.
    • Ventral (Anterior) Horn: Contains large motor neurons (Alpha motor neurons) supplying skeletal muscle.
    • Dorsal (Posterior) Horn: Receives sensory fibers. Slender.
    • Lateral Horn: Present only in Thoracic/Upper Lumbar (T1-L2). Contains Sympathetic neurons.
    • Central Canal: Lined by ependyma.
  • White Matter (Outer): Nerve fibers (tracts). Divided into:
    • Anterior Funiculus (Column)
    • Lateral Funiculus
    • Posterior Funiculus
