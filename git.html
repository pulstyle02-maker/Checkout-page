1. Components of the Enteric Nervous System (ENS)

The Enteric Nervous System is a highly developed nervous network situated entirely within the wall of the gut, extending from the esophagus to the anus. It contains approximately 100 million neurons (nearly equivalent to the spinal cord) and primarily governs gastrointestinal movements and secretions. It is classified into two major plexuses:

FeatureMyenteric (Auerbach) PlexusSubmucosal (Meissner) Plexus
LocationOuter plexus located between the longitudinal and circular smooth muscle layers.Inner plexus located entirely within the submucosa.
Significance / RolePrincipally controls muscle activity and long-distance movements along the entire length of the gut.Principally controls local functions within short segments: local secretion, absorption, and local blood flow.
Effect of ExcitationIncreases muscular tone, intensity of rhythmic contractions, and the conduction velocity of excitatory waves (enhancing peristalsis).Integrates sensory signals from the gastrointestinal epithelium to coordinate local secretory processes and mucosal infolding.
Inhibitory ElementsContains inhibitory neurons (secreting VIP) to relax sphincters (e.g., pyloric and ileocecal valves) to allow forward passage of food.Primarily focused on local absorptive and secretory regulation rather than direct long-distance muscle inhibition.

2. Mechanisms of Electrical Activity in GIT Smooth Muscle

Gastrointestinal smooth muscle functions as a syncytium connected by gap junctions. Its almost continuous intrinsic electrical activity is characterized by two types of waves: Slow Waves and Spike Potentials.

Slow Waves (Basic Electrical Rhythm)

Slow waves are not true action potentials. They are slow, undulating changes in the resting membrane potential (varying between 5 to 15 mV). They are generated by specialized cells known as the Interstitial Cells of Cajal (ICC), which act as electrical pacemakers. The ICCs undergo cyclic changes due to unique ion channels that periodically open to produce inward pacemaker currents (primarily sodium entry). By themselves, slow waves usually do not cause muscle contraction (except possibly in the stomach), but they set the maximum rhythm of contraction (e.g., 3/min in stomach, 12/min in duodenum, 8-9/min in ileum).

Spike Potentials

Spike potentials are true action potentials. They occur automatically when the resting membrane potential becomes more positive than -40 millivolts. Unlike nerve fibers that rely on rapid sodium channels, GIT spike potentials are generated by slow Calcium-Sodium channels. These channels allow massive influxes of calcium (and some sodium). The entry of calcium binds with calmodulin, which initiates the contractile process. Because these channels are slow to open and close, GIT action potentials last 10 to 40 times longer than large nerve fiber action potentials.

3. Factors Altering the GIT Membrane Potential

The normal resting membrane potential of GIT smooth muscle is approximately -56 millivolts. Various physiological factors can alter this baseline, making the muscle more or less excitable.

Depolarize (Make More Excitable)Hyperpolarize (Make Less Excitable)
  • Stretch of the gastrointestinal muscle.
  • Acetylcholine released by parasympathetic nerves.
  • Specific gastrointestinal hormones (e.g., Gastrin, Motilin).
  • Norepinephrine or Epinephrine action on the fiber membrane.
  • Stimulation of the sympathetic nervous system.

4. Neurotransmitters of the Enteric Nervous System

The enteric neurons release a variety of neurotransmitter substances. While acetylcholine is predominantly excitatory and norepinephrine is inhibitory, others serve mixed or specialized roles.

  • Excitatory: Acetylcholine (enhances motility and secretion), Substance P, Serotonin.
  • Inhibitory: Norepinephrine (inhibits most GI activity), Epinephrine, Vasoactive Intestinal Polypeptide (VIP - crucial for relaxing sphincters), Somatostatin.

5. Autonomic Control of the GIT

Although the ENS can function independently, extrinsic autonomic nerves powerfully modulate its activity.

  • Parasympathetic System (Excitatory): Divided into cranial (vagus nerves supplying the esophagus, stomach, pancreas, and proximal colon) and sacral (pelvic nerves supplying the distal colon and rectum) divisions. Stimulation generally increases ENS activity, enhancing motility and secretion.
  • Sympathetic System (Inhibitory): Originates from spinal segments T5-L2, passing through prevertebral ganglia (e.g., celiac, mesenteric). Sympathetic stimulation secretes mainly norepinephrine, which directly inhibits smooth muscle (except mucosal muscle) and intensely inhibits ENS neurons. Strong stimulation can completely block food movement.

6. Gastrointestinal Reflexes

The anatomical arrangement of the ENS and autonomic nerves supports three key levels of reflexes:

Level of IntegrationFunctions & Examples
Within the Gut Wall (ENS)Controls local secretion, mixing contractions (segmentation), and local peristalsis.
Gut to Prevertebral Ganglia to GutTransmits long-distance signals. Examples:
• Gastrocolic reflex: Stomach distention promotes colonic evacuation.
• Enterogastric reflex: Duodenum/colon distention inhibits stomach emptying.
• Colonoileal reflex: Colon distention inhibits ileal emptying.
Gut to Brain Stem/Spinal Cord to Gut• Vagovagal reflexes: Stomach to brain stem back to stomach to control gastric secretion/motor activity.
• Defecation reflexes: Colon/rectum to spinal cord back to colon/rectum for powerful expulsive contractions.

8. Types and Control of GIT Movements

Two fundamental types of movements occur in the gastrointestinal tract:

  • Propulsive Movements (Peristalsis): A contractile ring forms around the gut and moves forward, propelling food. The primary stimulus is distention. It requires an active myenteric plexus. The "Law of the Gut" dictates that peristalsis moves anal-ward, accompanied by downstream "receptive relaxation."
  • Mixing Movements: Keeps contents thoroughly mixed. Depending on the gut segment, mixing is achieved either by blocked forward peristalsis (e.g., against a sphincter) or by local intermittent constrictive contractions (segmentation) that chop and shear the chyme.

9. Splanchnic Circulation and Blood Flow

The splanchnic circulation encompasses blood flow through the gut, spleen, pancreas, and liver. Blood from these organs flows into the liver via the portal vein, allowing reticuloendothelial cells to remove bacteria and hepatic cells to process water-soluble nutrients. Fats largely bypass this system via lymphatics.

Metabolic Control

Blood flow is directly proportional to local activity. During active absorption, villus blood flow can increase up to eightfold. Causes include:

  • Release of peptide vasodilators (CCK, VIP, Gastrin, Secretin) during digestion.
  • Release of kinins (kallidin, bradykinin) by secreting glands.
  • Decreased tissue oxygen concentration triggers up to a fourfold increase in adenosine, a potent vasodilator.

Nervous Control

  • Parasympathetic: Indirectly increases blood flow by driving glandular secretion.
  • Sympathetic: Causes intense direct vasoconstriction. However, within minutes, local ischemic metabolites build up and override the sympathetic tone, a mechanism known as autoregulatory escape. Sympathetic venoconstriction is also critical for displacing 200-400 mL of blood from the splanchnic reservoir during circulatory shock.

Oral Cavity & Esophagus

1. Mastication (Chewing)

Most muscles of chewing are innervated by the motor branch of Cranial Nerve V (Trigeminal), controlled by brain stem nuclei. The process largely operates via a Chewing Reflex Arc:

Reflex Arc: Presence of food bolus in the mouth → reflex inhibition of muscles of mastication → jaw drops → drop initiates a stretch reflex in jaw muscles → rebound contraction raises the jaw to compress the bolus → renewed inhibition, and the cycle repeats.

Importance: Chewing is vital for digesting raw fruits and vegetables, which possess indigestible cellulose membranes. Furthermore, breaking food into smaller particles vastly increases the surface area exposed to digestive enzymes, preventing excoriation of the mucosa, and ensuring smooth passage into the stomach.

2. Stages of Swallowing (Deglutition)

Swallowing is divided into three distinct stages:

A. Voluntary Stage

Food is voluntarily squeezed and rolled posteriorly into the pharynx by the upward and backward pressure of the tongue against the palate. From this point forward, swallowing is entirely automatic and cannot be stopped.

B. Pharyngeal Stage (Involuntary)

Reflex Arc: Tactile receptors near the pharyngeal opening (especially the tonsillar pillars) sense the bolus → Afferent sensory impulses travel via CN V (Trigeminal) and CN IX (Glossopharyngeal) to the tractus solitarius in the medulla → The medullary Swallowing Center integrates the signal → Efferent motor impulses travel via CN V, IX, X (Vagus), and XII (Hypoglossal) to pharyngeal muscles.

Specific Steps Involved:

  • The soft palate is pulled upward to close the posterior nares (prevents nasal reflux).
  • Palatopharyngeal folds pull medially, forming a slit to restrict large particles.
  • Vocal cords strongly approximate, and the larynx is pulled upward and forward by neck muscles. The epiglottis swings backward over the laryngeal opening. Tight closure of the vocal cords is the absolute most critical step to prevent aspiration.
  • Laryngeal elevation stretches open the esophagus, and the Upper Esophageal Sphincter (UES) relaxes.
  • Pharyngeal muscles contract sequentially from top to bottom, propelling the bolus into the esophagus. The entire process takes less than 2 seconds.

Effect on Respiration: The swallowing center specifically inhibits the medullary respiratory center for the brief duration of the pharyngeal stage (less than 6 seconds), halting respiration at any point in its cycle without noticeable discomfort.

C. Esophageal Stage of Swallowing

The esophagus functions solely to conduct food rapidly from the pharynx to the stomach using two types of peristalsis:

Type of PeristalsisMechanism and Role
Primary PeristalsisA simple continuation of the pharyngeal peristaltic wave. It sweeps down the esophagus, transferring the bolus to the stomach in about 8-10 seconds (faster if upright due to gravity).
Secondary PeristalsisIf primary peristalsis fails to clear the bolus, localized distention of the esophagus triggers secondary waves. Mediated by intrinsic myenteric circuits and vagovagal reflexes, these waves continue until the esophagus is entirely empty.

3. The Lower Esophageal Sphincter (LES)

Located at the lower end of the esophagus (extending about 3 cm above the stomach juncture), the LES remains tonically constricted at a resting pressure of about 30 mmHg. This high pressure barrier prevents the reflux of highly acidic and proteolytic stomach contents into the sensitive esophageal mucosa.

As the esophageal peristaltic wave approaches, myenteric inhibitory neurons release VIP and NO, causing receptive relaxation of the LES, allowing food to pass smoothly into the stomach. Additionally, an intra-abdominal valvelike configuration of the distal esophagus caves inward when intra-abdominal pressure rises, preventing reflux during coughing or straining.

4. Dysphagia: Causes and Mechanisms

Dysphagia (difficulty swallowing) or complete paralysis of the swallowing mechanism can occur due to:

  • Cranial Nerve Damage: Lesions affecting CN V, IX, or X interrupt the reflex arc.
  • Brain Stem Damage: Conditions like poliomyelitis or encephalitis can destroy the swallowing center, leading to an inability to swallow and severe risk of aspiration.
  • Muscle Diseases: Muscular dystrophy leading to weakness of pharyngeal muscles.
  • Neuromuscular Junction Failure: Myasthenia gravis or botulism toxicity.
  • Deep Anesthesia: Blocks the reflex mechanisms, posing a massive aspiration risk if the patient vomits.

5. Achalasia & Megaesophagus

Pathophysiology: Achalasia is a pathological condition wherein the Lower Esophageal Sphincter fails to relax during swallowing. This failure stems from neurological damage to the myenteric plexus network in the lower two-thirds of the esophagus. The absence of functioning inhibitory neurons means the "receptive relaxation" signal is lost, leaving the LES spastically contracted.

Features (Megaesophagus): Because food cannot pass efficiently into the stomach, it accumulates. Over months to years, the esophagus can dilate enormously (Megaesophagus), holding up to 1 Liter of food. The retained food undergoes putrefaction, leading to severe mucosal ulceration, chest pain, and potential rupture or death.

Treatment: Mechanical tearing of the spastic sphincter using balloon dilation. Pharmacological use of smooth muscle relaxants (antispasmodics) can provide symptomatic relief.

Stomach: Storage, Emptying, & Pathophysiology

Learning Objectives Covered

1. Motor Functions of the Stomach

The stomach executes three primary motor functions: (1) Storage of massive quantities of food, (2) Mixing food with gastric secretions to form a semifluid paste called chyme, and (3) Slow, highly regulated emptying of chyme into the small intestine.

Storage Function

As food enters the stomach, it forms concentric circles in the orad (proximal) portion. Distention of the gastric wall elicits a vagovagal reflex passing to the brain stem and back, which reduces the muscular tone of the stomach body. This "receptive relaxation" allows the stomach to accommodate anywhere from 0.8 to 1.5 liters of food with very little increase in internal pressure.

Basic Electrical Rhythm & Mixing

The stomach's basic electrical rhythm consists of slow waves occurring approximately every 15 to 20 seconds. These spontaneous slow waves initiate weak peristaltic mixing waves starting in the mid-stomach. As the waves travel toward the antrum, they gain intensity. Because the pyloric opening is tiny, only a few milliliters of chyme escape. The bulk of the antral contents are violently squeezed backward against the advancing peristaltic ring, a crucial mixing mechanism known as retropulsion.

2. Gastric Emptying: Pyloric Pump vs. Sphincter

ComponentMechanism in Emptying
Pyloric PumpApproximately 20% of the time food is present, mixing waves turn into highly intense, action-potential-driven constrictor rings. These powerful peristaltic waves can generate pressures of 50–70 cm H2O, physically forcing chyme out of the stomach.
Pyloric SphincterThe circular muscle of the pylorus is 50-100% thicker than the antral muscle, remaining mildly tonically contracted. While it permits water to flow freely, it prevents large, undigested food particles from exiting until they achieve a chyme-like fluidity.

3. Regulation of Gastric Emptying

Emptying is controlled by a balance of gastric (promotive) and duodenal (inhibitory) factors. Duodenal feedback is vastly more potent, ensuring the intestine is never overwhelmed.

Factors Promoting Stomach Emptying (Gastric)

  • Increased Food Volume: Stretching the stomach wall elicits local myenteric reflexes that heavily accentuate the pyloric pump while inhibiting the pyloric sphincter. (Note: Increased pressure itself is not the trigger, stretch is).
  • Gastrin: Released by G cells in response to meat breakdown products. Gastrin stimulates acidic secretions and mildly enhances pyloric pump motor activity.

Factors Inhibiting Stomach Emptying (Duodenal)

The duodenum relies on robust enterogastric inhibitory reflexes and hormonal feedback to delay gastric emptying. The enterogastric reflexes travel via three paths: directly through the ENS, via sympathetic ganglia, and centrally via the vagus nerve. They inhibit the pyloric pump and constrict the sphincter.

Factors initiating the enterogastric reflexes include:

  • Duodenal distention or mucosal irritation.
  • High Acidity: A duodenal pH falling below 3.5-4 instantly blocks further gastric emptying until neutralized by pancreatic bicarbonate.
  • Osmolality: Hypertonic fluids trigger inhibition to prevent rapid, dangerous fluid shifts in the extracellular space.
  • Protein & Fat Products: Fat acts primarily via hormonal feedback by causing the massive release of Cholecystokinin (CCK), which is the most potent hormonal inhibitor of gastric emptying. GIP and Secretin play minor roles.

4. Gastritis

Gastritis is the inflammation of the gastric mucosa. It can be superficial or penetrate deeply, leading to chronic mucosal atrophy.

Causes & Pathophysiology

The normal stomach is protected by a robust "gastric barrier" comprised of highly resistant tight junctions and a thick layer of viscid, alkaline mucus. Gastritis typically stems from agents that break down this barrier:

  • Chronic Bacterial Infection: Helicobacter pylori is the most common cause.
  • Chemical Irritants: Excessive alcohol consumption or chronic use of aspirin / NSAIDs.
  • Autoimmunity: Immune destruction of parietal cells (autoimmune gastritis).

Features & Physiological Basis

Feature of GastritisPhysiological Basis
Erosion & UlcerationWhen the mucosal barrier breaks down, highly concentrated hydrogen ions (H+) rapidly back-diffuse into the epithelium. This creates a vicious cycle of progressive tissue damage, inviting active digestion by pepsin.
Achlorhydria & HypochlorhydriaChronic deep inflammation causes widespread atrophy of gastric glands. The loss of parietal cells eliminates the stomach's ability to secrete hydrochloric acid. Without an acidic environment, pepsinogen cannot be activated to pepsin.
Pernicious AnemiaParietal cells secrete Intrinsic Factor alongside HCl. Intrinsic factor is absolutely necessary to bind and protect Vitamin B12 for absorption in the terminal ileum. Destruction of parietal cells means zero B12 absorption, leading to a failure of red blood cell maturation in the bone marrow.

Recommended Treatment

For bacteria-induced gastritis, intensive antibacterial therapy is recommended alongside the removal of offending chemical irritants (alcohol, NSAIDs) and administration of acid suppressants.

5. Peptic Ulcer Disease

A peptic ulcer is an excoriated area of stomach or intestinal mucosa caused by the digestive action of gastric acid and pepsin. The most common site is the first few centimeters of the duodenum, followed by the lesser curvature of the stomach antrum.

Pathophysiology & Causes

Peptic ulcers occur due to an imbalance between aggressive factors (acid and pepsin) and protective factors (mucosal barrier and bicarbonate neutralization).

  • H. pylori Infection: Responsible for >75% of ulcers. The bacteria burrows through the mucosal barrier and releases ammonium, which directly liquefies the barrier and stimulates excess HCl secretion.
  • Excess Acid Secretion: Overstimulation of parietal cells (e.g., severe stress, smoking, Zollinger-Ellison gastrinoma) overwhelms the duodenal neutralizing capacity.
  • Diminished Mucosal Protection: NSAIDs and aspirin suppress protective prostaglandin synthesis, weakening the mucus layer.

Physiological Basis of Treatment

Modern therapy targets both the infection and the acid production. Antibiotics eradicate H. pylori. H2-Receptor Antagonists (e.g., ranitidine) or Proton Pump Inhibitors block parietal cell acid secretion by 70–100%, allowing the excoriated mucosa the safe, neutral environment required to heal.

1. Secretions of the Small Intestine

The small intestine features specialized glands that provide lubrication, protection, and a fluid medium for absorption.

2. Movements of the Small Intestine

  • Segmentation (Mixing): When chyme distends the gut, localized concentric constrictions divide the bowel into sausage-like segments. They chop the chyme 8–12 times per minute, mixing it thoroughly with digestive juices. The rate is strictly dictated by the slow-wave pacemaker rhythm.
  • Peristalsis (Propulsive): Weak waves traveling at 0.5–2.0 cm/sec propel chyme anal-ward. They die out rapidly, yielding a slow net forward progression of just ~1 cm/min. It takes 3 to 5 hours for chyme to transit from the pylorus to the ileocecal valve. Regulated heavily by the gastroenteric reflex (distention of stomach ramps up intestinal peristalsis).

Peristaltic Rush

Normally, small intestinal peristalsis is weak. However, in conditions of severe mucosal irritation (such as infectious diarrhea), rapid and exceedingly powerful peristaltic waves are triggered. This Peristaltic Rush utilizes both enhanced intrinsic ENS reflexes and extrinsic autonomic brain-stem reflexes to sweep the irritating chyme over long distances straight into the colon within minutes, effectively clearing the pathogen at the expense of absorption.

3. Functions of the Ileocecal Valve and Sphincter

The ileocecal valve protrudes forward into the cecum. Its structural lips are forced shut when cecal pressure rises, effortlessly resisting 50–60 cm H2O of reverse pressure, strictly preventing colonic bacteria from backflowing into the sterile ileum. Upstream of the valve, the thickened circular muscle forms the ileocecal sphincter. This sphincter maintains a mild tonic constriction, intentionally slowing ileal emptying to maximize the time available for nutrient absorption. Roughly 1500–2000 mL of chyme enters the cecum daily.

4. Intestinal Sprue (Malabsorption Syndrome)

Sprue refers to a group of diseases characterized by profound malabsorption of nutrients by the small intestinal mucosa, even though pancreatic and biliary digestion processes are completely normal.

Types and Features of Sprue

Type of SprueFeatures & Pathophysiology
Nontropical Sprue (Celiac Disease)Also known as gluten enteropathy or idiopathic sprue. In susceptible individuals, exposure to the protein gluten (found in wheat and rye) elicits a toxic immune response. This directly destroys the microvilli of enterocytes. Severe disease can completely flatten the intestinal villi, destroying the vast surface area required for absorption. Condition reverses with strict adherence to a gluten-free diet.
Tropical SpruePredominantly found in tropical regions. It presents similarly but is believed to result from an unidentified infectious inflammatory agent targeting the mucosa. Often highly responsive to prolonged antibacterial therapy.

Consequences of Sprue on the Body

In the early stages, fat absorption is uniquely vulnerable and is the first to fail. As the villous destruction progresses, multiple other nutritional deficits emerge, culminating in severe systemic issues:

  • Steatorrhea: Unabsorbed fats are lost in the stool, producing bulky, foul-smelling, fatty feces. Because the fat appears as fatty acid salts (rather than intact triglycerides), it proves that pancreatic digestion occurred correctly, but the mucosa simply failed to absorb the products.
  • Severe Wasting: Extensive malabsorption of proteins and carbohydrates leads to massive caloric deficits, severe nutritional wasting, and profound weight loss.
  • Osteomalacia: Malabsorption of calcium and fat-soluble Vitamin D leads to severe bone demineralization and bone pain.
  • Bleeding Disorders: Failure to absorb lipid-soluble Vitamin K leads to impaired synthesis of hepatic clotting factors, resulting in prolonged coagulation times and hemorrhage.
  • Pernicious (Macrocytic) Anemia: The loss of functional terminal ileum mucosa prevents the absorption of the Intrinsic Factor-Vitamin B12 complex and folic acid, causing a failure of red blood cell maturation.
Clinical Integration: Short Bowel Syndrome mimics severe sprue. When massive portions of the intestine are surgically resected (e.g., following mesenteric ischemia or Crohn's disease), the remaining mucosa is insufficient to absorb the required daily 8-9 liters of fluid and nutrients, leading to lifelong diarrhea and dependence on Total Parenteral Nutrition (TPN).

Large Intestine: Motility, Defecation, & Constipation

1. Functions and Movements of the Colon

The colon absorbs water/electrolytes in its proximal half to form solid feces, and stores feces in its distal half. Because rapid transit isn't needed, colonic movements are extremely sluggish.

Type of MovementMechanism & Characteristics
Haustrations (Mixing)Combined contraction of a 2.5 cm circular muscle ring and the longitudinal muscle strips (teniae coli) causes the unstimulated colon wall to bulge into bag-like sacs called haustra. Each haustration lasts about 60-90 seconds. This slowly "spades" and rolls the feces, progressively exposing all material to the mucosa for maximum fluid absorption.
Mass Movements (Propulsive)A modified, heavy-duty peristalsis. A constrictive ring forms, and then 20+ cm of the distal colon loses its haustrations and violently contracts as a single unit, pushing the fecal mass solidly forward. This occurs only 1 to 3 times a day, typically for about 15 minutes after eating breakfast.

Control via Gastrocolic & Duodenocolic Reflexes

The sudden appearance of mass movements following meals is facilitated by the Gastrocolic and Duodenocolic reflexes. Distention of the stomach and duodenum immediately transmits neural signals via extrinsic autonomic nerves directly to the colon, ramping up mass movements to clear older feces and make room for the new meal.

2. Defecation Reflexes

The rectum is usually completely empty. When a mass movement finally forces feces into the rectum, the sharp distention triggers the desire to defecate. Continence is maintained by the tonically contracted smooth muscle of the Internal Anal Sphincter and the striated, voluntarily controlled External Anal Sphincter (supplied by the somatic pudendal nerve).

Defecation relies on two integrated reflex arcs:

  1. Intrinsic Myenteric Reflex: Distention of the rectum triggers afferent signals in the ENS -> spreading via the myenteric plexus to initiate peristalsis in the descending/sigmoid colon and rectum -> inhibitory signals relax the internal anal sphincter. On its own, this reflex is quite weak.
  2. Parasympathetic Defecation Reflex: Rectal distention -> afferent signals travel via pelvic nerves to the Sacral Spinal Cord -> parasympathetic efferents fire back via pelvic nerves -> producing massive, powerful peristalsis in the colon and rectum. This effectively transforms the weak intrinsic reflex into a mighty expulsive force.

If the individual consciously relaxes the external anal sphincter and coordinates an abdominal squeeze (Valsalva), defecation succeeds.

3. Other Autonomic Reflexes Affecting the Bowel

Systemic distress in the abdomen can trigger powerful inhibitory reflexes that halt all GI motility, protecting the body by resting the gut during injury or severe irritation.

  • Peritoneointestinal Reflex: Irritation of the peritoneum (e.g., peritonitis from a ruptured appendix) sends massive inhibitory signals to the enteric nerves. This causes a complete, life-threatening intestinal paralysis known as paralytic ileus.
  • Renointestinal Reflex: Severe kidney irritation (such as a passing kidney stone) strongly inhibits intestinal motor activity.
  • Vesicointestinal Reflex: Bladder irritation (e.g., severe cystitis) similarly suppresses overall bowel motility.

4. Pathophysiology of Constipation

Constipation refers to the abnormally slow transit of feces through the large intestine, resulting in excessively dry, hard feces accumulating in the descending colon. This hardness occurs because the prolonged transit time allows the colon to continuously extract water from the mass.

Causes & Mechanisms

EtiologyPathophysiological Mechanism
Suppression of ReflexesIgnoring the urge to defecate or habitual overuse of chemical laxatives over months/years leads to the progressive weakening of the natural defecation reflexes. The colon eventually loses its muscle tone (atonic colon).
Segmental SpasmBecause colonic propulsion is normally weak, a severe localized spasm in a small segment of the sigmoid colon can completely halt fecal progression. Accumulation of irritative feces above the spastic segment may eventually provoke hypersecretion, leading to a sudden burst of diarrhea, creating a pattern of alternating constipation and diarrhea.
Mechanical ObstructionTumors (colon cancer), strictures, or severe adhesions physical block transit, causing immense upstream accumulation and constipation.

5. Hirschsprung's Disease (Megacolon)

In severe instances, severe constipation allows tremendous quantities of feces to build up, distending the colon up to 3 or 4 inches in diameter—a condition termed Megacolon.

Cause and Pathophysiology: The classic cause of megacolon is Hirschsprung's Disease. This is a congenital condition characterized by the complete absence of ganglion cells in the myenteric plexus of a distinct segment of the distal colon (often the sigmoid).

Without the enteric nervous system, the affected segment cannot conduct peristaltic waves or undergo receptive relaxation. The segment becomes a narrow, spastic bottleneck. Fecal matter cannot pass through, leading to massive, debilitating accumulation and extreme distention (megacolon) of the healthy ascending and transverse colon located upstream of the defect.

Liver: Bile Secretion & Gallstones

Learning Objectives Covered

  • Explain the functions of the liver.
  • Differentiate between liver and gallbladder bile and the hormones acting on them.
  • Enumerate the causes and composition of developing gallstones.

1. Functions of the Liver

Operating as a central metabolic and secretory hub, the liver processes essentially all blood returning from the splanchnic circulation via the portal vein.

  • Metabolic Storage: Hepatocytes extract, temporarily store, and process ½ to ¾ of all water-soluble nutrients absorbed from the gut before they reach the systemic circulation.
  • Bile Secretion: The liver secretes 600–1000 mL of bile daily, which is strictly required for fat emulsification and absorption.
  • Excretion of Waste: The liver utilizes bile to excrete lipid-soluble waste products, particularly bilirubin (a toxic end-product of hemoglobin breakdown) and excess systemic cholesterol.
  • Immune Filtration: Reticuloendothelial cells lining the sinusoids rapidly phagocytose gut-derived bacteria from portal blood.

2. Liver Bile vs. Gallbladder Bile

The liver secretes bile continuously, but it is stored in the gallbladder (max capacity 30-60 mL) until needed. To store up to 450 mL of daily secretion, the gallbladder intensely absorbs water, Na+, and Cl−, concentrating the bile by 5- to 20-fold.

Hormones Acting on the Biliary System

  • Secretin: Targets the epithelial cells of the bile ducts. In response to acid in the duodenum, it stimulates copious secretion of a watery, bicarbonate-rich fluid into the bile ducts, drastically increasing total bile volume and neutralizing duodenal acid.
  • Cholecystokinin (CCK): The definitive trigger for gallbladder emptying. Released in response to fatty foods in the duodenum, CCK causes powerful, rhythmic contraction of the gallbladder wall and simultaneous relaxation of the Sphincter of Oddi.

3. Pathophysiology of Gallstones (Cholelithiasis)

Cholesterol is completely insoluble in water. It is normally kept suspended in solution by bile salts and lecithin, which encapsulate it in water-soluble micelles. When this delicate chemical balance is lost, cholesterol crystallizes and forms cholesterol gallstones.

Four main causes of cholesterol precipitation:

  1. Too much cholesterol in bile: Typically resulting from a long-term high-fat diet, which forces the liver to synthesize and excrete excessive cholesterol.
  2. Too much absorption of water from bile: Over-concentrates the bile, forcing cholesterol out of solution.
  3. Too much absorption of bile acids: Removes the crucial micelle components needed to solubilize the cholesterol.
  4. Inflammation of the gallbladder epithelium: Low-grade chronic infection alters the mucosal absorptive characteristics, causing disproportionate removal of bile salts and water while leaving cholesterol stranded in the lumen to crystallize.

Pancreas: Function & Pancreatitis

Learning Objectives Covered

  • Explain the function and secretions of pancreas.
  • Enlist the causes and pathophysiology of acute and chronic pancreatitis.
  • Enumerate the features of acute pancreatitis and explain the physiological basis of each feature.

1. Functions and Secretions of the Pancreas

The pancreas is a massive compound gland parallel to the stomach. It serves crucial exocrine digestive functions, secreting roughly 1 Liter of pancreatic juice daily into the duodenum via the papilla of Vater.

Pancreatic juice consists of two entirely distinct components:

  • Digestive Enzymes (from Acinar cells): Stimulated primarily by CCK and Acetylcholine. Contains Trypsin, Chymotrypsin, and Carboxypolypeptidase (for proteins); Pancreatic Amylase (for carbohydrates); and Pancreatic Lipase, Cholesterol Esterase, and Phospholipase (for fats). All proteolytic enzymes are secreted as inactive zymogens (e.g., trypsinogen) to prevent autodigestion.
  • Aqueous Sodium Bicarbonate (from Ductal cells): Stimulated predominantly by Secretin. The ducts secrete high concentrations of HCO3− (up to 145 mEq/L) into the fluid. This rapidly neutralizes highly acidic gastric chyme, protecting the duodenal mucosa and establishing an optimal pH (7-8) for the digestive enzymes to operate.

2. Causes & Pathophysiology of Pancreatitis

Pancreatitis is inflammation of the pancreas, manifesting as acute severe attacks or chronic progressive disease. Two causes account for over 90% of all cases:

  1. Gallstone Blockage: A stone migrating from the gallbladder lodges in the papilla of Vater, completely blocking both the main pancreatic duct and common bile duct.
  2. Excess Alcohol Consumption: Chronic alcohol abuse precipitates protein plugs in small ducts and directly injures acinar cells.

The Pathophysiological Cascade (Autodigestion)

Normally, the pancreas secretes trypsin inhibitor to prevent premature enzyme activation. When a gallstone blocks the duct, immense quantities of pancreatic secretion become dammed up inside the acini. The sheer volume eventually overwhelms the trypsin inhibitor. A minute amount of trypsinogen activates into trypsin. Trypsin is an autocatalytic activator—it immediately sparks a runaway chain reaction, activating all remaining trypsinogen, chymotrypsinogen, and phospholipases. These active, lethal proteases rapidly begin to digest the pancreas itself.

3. Features of Acute Pancreatitis & Their Physiological Basis

Clinical FeaturePhysiological Basis
Rapid Gland DestructionThe unchecked cascade of activated proteolytic enzymes aggressively digests the cellular and structural components of the pancreas within hours.
Severe Circulatory ShockThe massive tissue necrosis triggers intense systemic inflammation. Furthermore, vasoactive peptides and active enzymes leak into the systemic blood, causing massive vasodilation and extreme fluid shift ("third-spacing") into the abdominal cavity, dropping blood pressure to lethal levels.
Permanent InsufficiencyIf the patient survives the acute shock, the digested acinar tissue is often replaced by useless fibrotic scar tissue, permanently disabling the pancreas's ability to secrete enzymes.
Copious Fatty Feces (Steatorrhea)With the acini destroyed, the gut lacks pancreatic lipase and proteases. Up to 60% of dietary fat and half of all proteins remain totally undigested and unabsorbed, exiting the body as foul, greasy stools.

The Vomiting Reflex

Learning Objectives Covered

  • Describe the stages of vomiting act.
  • Trace the reflex arc of vomiting.
  • Explain the role of chemoreceptor trigger zone for initiation of vomiting by drugs or by motion sickness.

1. Trace the Reflex Arc of Vomiting

Vomiting is an emergency reflex utilized to rapidly empty the upper GI tract when it becomes excessively irritated, distended, or overexcitable (particularly in the duodenum).

The Vomiting Reflex Arc:
Stimulus: Excessive distention or mucosal irritation in the pharynx, stomach, or duodenum.
Afferents: Sensory impulses travel via Vagal and Sympathetic nerve fibers.
Integration: The "Vomiting Center" in the medullary and pontine reticular formation integrates the signals.
Efferents: Motor impulses fire via CN V, VII, IX, X, and XII to the upper GI tract; sympathetic/vagal nerves to the lower tract; and spinal somatic nerves to the diaphragm and abdominal wall muscles.
Response: The coordinated Vomiting Act expels contents.

2. Stages of the Vomiting Act

Vomiting is not merely a stomach contraction; it is a complex somatic and visceral reflex.

  • Antiperistaltic Prelude: Often minutes before vomiting, reversed peristalsis (antiperistalsis) begins deep in the ileum, sweeping rapidly backwards (2-3 cm/sec) to push intestinal contents into the duodenum and stomach, causing severe overdistention.
  • Preparation: A deep breath is taken, and the Lower Esophageal Sphincter partially relaxes.
  • Airway Protection: The hyoid bone and larynx are sharply raised to pull open the upper esophageal sphincter. Simultaneously, the glottis tightly closes (critical to prevent lethal aspiration into the lungs), and the soft palate lifts to close the posterior nares.
  • Abdominal Pressurization: The diaphragm forcefully contracts downward while all abdominal wall muscles contract inward. This intensely squeezes the stomach between the diaphragm and abdominal wall, spiking intragastric pressure to immense levels.
  • Expulsion: The Lower Esophageal Sphincter undergoes complete relaxation. The intense pressure instantly drives the gastric contents forcefully upward through the open esophagus and out the mouth.

3. Role of the Chemoreceptor Trigger Zone (CTZ)

Aside from direct gut irritation, vomiting can be initiated directly by central chemical or neurological signals via the Chemoreceptor Trigger Zone. The CTZ is a specialized bilateral area located in the area postrema on the lateral walls of the fourth ventricle. Because it lies outside the strict blood-brain barrier, it constantly samples blood for toxins.

Trigger SourceMechanism via the CTZ
Circulating Drugs & ToxinsCertain emetic drugs (e.g., apomorphine, morphine, chemotherapy agents) and endogenous toxins (uremia) directly stimulate the CTZ. The CTZ then relays a powerful excitatory signal to the adjacent Vomiting Center, initiating the motor vomiting act. (Destruction of the CTZ blocks drug-induced vomiting, but leaves GI-irritation vomiting intact).
Motion SicknessRapid, changing motion overstimulates receptors in the vestibular labyrinth of the inner ear. Impulses travel via the vestibular nuclei to the cerebellum, then to the CTZ, and finally to the Vomiting Center.

Acute & Chronic Diarrhea

Learning Objectives Covered

  • Define Acute Diarrhea.
  • Define Chronic Diarrhea.
  • Enlist various causes for acute and chronic diarrhea.

1. Definitions

Diarrhea is broadly defined as the rapid movement of fecal matter through the large intestine, resulting in an increased fluidity and an increased frequency of bowel movements. The excessive speed severely reduces the time available for fluid absorption, leading to massive water and electrolyte losses.

  • Acute Diarrhea: Defined as a sudden onset of frequent, watery stools typically lasting less than 2 weeks. It is almost always caused by an acute infectious agent or toxin that triggers massive, temporary mucosal hypersecretion and hypermotility. The body utilizes this as an emergency mechanism to "wash away" the dangerous pathogen.
  • Chronic Diarrhea: Defined as persistent or recurrent episodes of loose, watery stools lasting beyond 4 weeks. It is rarely due to acute infection; rather, it stems from chronic inflammatory bowel diseases, persistent malabsorption syndromes, or functional neurological imbalances in the gut.

2. Causes and Pathophysiology of Diarrhea

Causes of Acute Diarrhea

EtiologyPathophysiological Mechanism
Enteritis (Viral or Bacterial)The most common cause of acute infectious diarrhea. Rampant infection in the distal ileum and colon severely irritates the mucosa. The inflamed mucosa secretes extra quantities of water, electrolytes, and viscid alkaline mucus to dilute the irritant, while motility markedly increases to rapidly flush the infection toward the anus.
Cholera (Secretory Diarrhea)The cholera toxin acts directly on the crypts of Lieberkühn. The toxin penetrates the cells and triggers a massive overproduction of cAMP, which wedges open apical chloride channels. Immense quantities of Cl− flood the lumen, dragging Na+ and massive amounts of osmotic water with it. Secretions can hit 10–12 liters/day, vastly outstripping the colon's 8-liter maximum absorptive capacity, leading to lethal dehydration within days if IV fluids are not administered.

Causes of Chronic Diarrhea

EtiologyPathophysiological Mechanism
Ulcerative ColitisA chronic disease characterized by extensive, unhealing inflammation and ulceration of the colonic walls. The severely damaged colon operates in a constant state of hypersecretion and hypermotility, resulting in continuous mass movements and repeated diarrheal bouts. Often possesses a strong hereditary component.
Intestinal Sprue (Malabsorption)Destruction of the small intestinal villi (e.g., Celiac disease) leaves the gut unable to absorb digested fats and nutrients. The massive osmotic load of unabsorbed nutrients in the lumen severely limits water reabsorption, producing chronic, bulky, fatty diarrhea (steatorrhea).
Psychogenic Emotional DiarrheaSevere nervous tension (e.g., exam stress, battle fatigue) triggers a massive, systemic overdrive of the parasympathetic nervous system. This overstimulates distal colonic motility and mucus secretion, resulting in frequent, loose stools entirely devoid of any infectious or physical pathology.

Digestion & Absorption

Supplemental Coverage

  • Explain the digestion of carbohydrates, proteins, and fats by hydrolysis.
  • Describe the anatomical basis of absorption in the small intestine.
  • Explain the mechanism of absorbing water, electrolytes, and nutrients.

1. The Chemistry of Digestion: Hydrolysis

The primary mechanism underlying all digestion is hydrolysis. Polymeric foods (carbohydrates, fats, proteins) are formed via condensation (removal of water). Digestive enzymes reverse this by physically inserting the hydrogen (H+) and hydroxyl (OH−) ions from a water molecule back into the target bond, breaking the complex molecules into simple, absorbable monomers.

2. Digestion of Major Food Groups

Carbohydrates

The diet consists mainly of starches, sucrose (table sugar), and lactose (milk).

  • Salivary Amylase: Begins starch digestion in the mouth (20-40% complete) before acid deactivates it.
  • Pancreatic Amylase: Extremely powerful; completes the breakdown of starches into maltose within 30 minutes in the duodenum.
  • Brush Border Enzymes: Lactase, sucrase, and maltase situated directly on the enterocyte microvilli perform the final split into monosaccharides (Glucose, Fructose, Galactose).

Proteins

  • Pepsin (Stomach): Active only at a highly acidic pH (2-3). It is uniquely essential for digesting collagen, allowing other enzymes to penetrate meats.
  • Pancreatic Proteases: Trypsin, Chymotrypsin, and Elastase cleave whole proteins into small polypeptides. Carboxypolypeptidase snaps individual amino acids off the ends.
  • Brush Border Peptidases: Perform the final hydrolysis, breaking peptides into individual amino acids which are absorbed into the portal blood.

Fats (Triglycerides)

  • Emulsification: Bile salts and lecithin act like powerful detergents, lowering fat surface tension. Agitation shatters fat into tiny globules, multiplying the surface area 1000-fold.
  • Digestion: Pancreatic lipase aggressively attacks the vast surface area, splitting triglycerides into free fatty acids and 2-monoglycerides.
  • Micelle Transport: Bile salts instantly encapsulate the fatty acids to form microscopic micelles, making them water-soluble. These micelles act as ferries, delivering the fat directly to the epithelial brush border for absorption, before returning to ferry more.

3. Anatomical Basis of Absorption

To absorb 8 to 9 liters of daily fluid alongside all nutrients, the small intestine utilizes an immense surface area, amplified nearly 1000-fold (equivalent to a tennis court) through three structures:

  • Folds of Kerckring (Valvulae conniventes): Circular folds increasing area ~3-fold.
  • Villi: Millions of millimeter-long projections increasing area ~10-fold.
  • Microvilli (Brush Border): 1000 microvilli per enterocyte increasing area ~20-fold.

4. Absorption of Ions and Water

SubstanceTransport Mechanism
WaterAbsorbed entirely by osmosis. As ions and nutrients are pumped into the lateral spaces, water instantly follows them down the osmotic gradient.
Sodium (Na+)Powered by the Na+/K+ ATPase pump on the basolateral membrane. This drops intracellular sodium levels, creating a massive electrochemical vacuum that aggressively pulls Na+ from the gut lumen into the cell. Aldosterone heavily upregulates this process, preventing fatal salt loss.
Chloride (Cl−)Rapidly follows the positively charged Sodium ions via passive diffusion.
BicarbonateIn the duodenum, secreted H+ combines with HCO3− forming CO2 and water; the CO2 is exhaled. In the colon, HCO3− is actively secreted in exchange for Chloride to neutralize bacterial acids.

5. Absorption of Nutrients

The transport of nearly all nutrients is heavily dependent on the active transport of Sodium.

  • Secondary Active Transport (Co-transport): The steep inward pull of Sodium is used to "drag" other molecules into the cell against their concentration gradients. Both Glucose and Amino Acids rely on highly specific sodium co-transporter proteins on the brush border. If sodium is absent, glucose and protein absorption completely halts.
  • Facilitated Diffusion: Fructose bypasses sodium entirely, entering via facilitated diffusion carriers. Its absorption rate is only half that of glucose.
  • Fat Absorption: Monoglycerides and fatty acids diffuse out of their bile micelles and effortlessly pass through the lipid-bilayer cell membrane. Inside the cell's endoplasmic reticulum, they are rebuilt into triglycerides, packaged into chylomicrons, and exocytosed into the central lacteal (lymphatic system) because they are too large for blood capillaries.
Clinical Integration: Certain diarrheal toxins (like cholera) artificially wedge open chloride channels in the crypts of Lieberkühn. The resulting tidal wave of chloride actively pulls sodium and water into the lumen, causing deadly secretory diarrhea. Oral rehydration solutions exploit the Sodium-Glucose Co-transporter: by providing both glucose and sodium, the transport protein is activated, actively pumping sodium and dragging water back into the body, bypassing the toxin's mechanism.
