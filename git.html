General Principles of GIT Function

Learning Objectives

  • Classify the enteric nervous system; locate & compare myenteric and Meissner plexuses
  • Summarize GI hormones by stimulus, site, and action
  • Classify GIT movements and explain their functions and control
  • Explain local-metabolic and nervous control of splanchnic blood flow

1. Enteric Nervous System

The ENS lies entirely in the gut wall from esophagus to anus. It contains about 100 million neurons—nearly as many as the spinal cord—and governs GI movement and secretion independently. Parasympathetic and sympathetic input can markedly enhance or inhibit its activity.

FeatureMyenteric (Auerbach) PlexusSubmucosal (Meissner) Plexus
LocationOuter plexus between longitudinal & circular smooth-muscle layers; linear chain along entire tractInner plexus in the submucosa
Main RoleControls muscle activity & movement along the gutControls local functions within short segments: secretion, absorption, local blood flow, submucosal muscle contraction
IntegrationLong-distance motility coordinationIntegrates epithelial sensory signals to adjust local secretion, absorption, and mucosal infolding
On StimulationIncreases tone, intensity of rhythmic contraction, slightly increases rhythm rate, and increases conduction velocity of excitatory wavesCoordinates local secretory and absorptive functions rather than long-distance motility
Inhibitory RoleSome neurons are inhibitory (VIP/peptide); relax sphincters such as pyloric and ileocecal sphinctersLocal inhibitory modulation
One-line difference: Myenteric = movement along the gut; Meissner = secretion, absorption, mucosal movement, and local blood flow within short segments.

2. Electrical Activity of GI Smooth Muscle

Gut smooth muscle acts as a syncytium. Gap junctions allow low-resistance ion movement and spread of excitation. Intrinsic electrical activity consists of slow waves and spike potentials.

FeatureSlow WavesSpike Potentials
NatureSlow undulating changes in resting membrane potential; not action potentialsTrue action potentials
GeneratorComplex smooth-muscle interaction with interstitial cells of Cajal (electrical pacemakers)Unique ion channels open cyclically and create inward pacemaker currents
Ion BasisMainly Na+ entry; normally do not themselves cause contractionSlow Ca2+-Na+ channels admit much Ca2+; calcium entry produces contraction via calmodulin
Size / DurationAmplitude ~5–15 mVEach lasts ~10–20 ms (~10–40× longer than large-nerve AP)
Frequency~3/min in stomach body; up to 12/min in duodenum; 8–9/min in terminal ileumUsually 1–10 spikes/sec; higher slow-wave peaks produce more spikes
Mechanical EffectSets maximum rhythm; usually no direct contraction except perhaps in stomachCauses most GI smooth-muscle contraction

Membrane Sequence

  • Slow-wave peak crosses approximately −40 mV
  • Spike potentials appear
  • Ca2+ enters through slow Ca2+-Na+ channels
  • Calmodulin activates myosin light-chain kinase → contraction

Normal resting potential lies about −50 to −60 mV (average ~−56 mV). Tonic contraction may result from continuous repetitive spikes, partial depolarization by hormones, or continuous Ca2+ entry without membrane-potential change.

Gut afferents respond to mucosal irritation, excessive distention, and specific chemicals. About 80% of vagal fibers are afferent. Their medullary input can generate vagal reflex output back to the GI tract.

6. Functional Movements & Their Control

TypeFunctionCore Control
Propulsive movementMoves food forward at a rate suited to digestion and absorptionPeristalsis: distention is the usual stimulus; chemical or epithelial irritation and strong parasympathetic signals also stimulate. Effective peristalsis requires an active myenteric plexus.
Mixing movementKeeps contents thoroughly mixedMay result from blocked forward peristalsis or local intermittent constrictions every few centimeters lasting ~5–30 sec, chopping and shearing contents
Tonic contractionSustained narrowing or tone for minutes to hoursContinuous repetitive spikes, partial depolarization by hormones/factors, or continuous Ca2+ entry

Law of the Gut

Distention produces contraction on the oral side and receptive relaxation downstream. The anal direction of the myenteric/peristaltic reflex constitutes the law of the gut.

7. Splanchnic Circulation & GI Blood Flow

The splanchnic circulation includes the gut, spleen, pancreas, and liver. Blood from gut, spleen, and pancreas enters the liver through the portal vein, passes through liver sinusoids, and leaves through hepatic veins. Reticuloendothelial cells remove bacteria and particles; hepatic cells temporarily store roughly ½ to ¾ of absorbed water-soluble nutrients and process them. Most absorbed fat bypasses portal blood through intestinal lymphatics and the thoracic duct.

Control / FactorEffect & Significance
Local activityFlow follows activity. During active absorption, villus and adjacent submucosal flow may rise up to eightfold. Post-meal flow returns toward rest over 2–4 hr.
Peptide vasodilatorsCCK, VIP, gastrin, and secretin are released during digestion
KininsKallidin and bradykinin released by glands are powerful vasodilators
Low tissue oxygenCan increase intestinal flow by 50–100%; adenosine may rise up to fourfold
ParasympatheticIncreases flow to stomach and lower colon indirectly by increasing glandular secretion
SympatheticDirect intense arteriolar vasoconstriction lowers flow. Local ischemic vasodilators later override it: autoregulatory escape
Blood reservoirConstriction of large intestinal and mesenteric veins can shift about 200–400 mL into the general circulation during low-volume states

Villus Countercurrent Trap

Arterial and venous flow run in opposite directions and close together. Up to 80% of oxygen may diffuse directly from arteriole to venule; severe low flow can therefore cause ischemic death of villus tips and reduced absorption.

Oral Cavity & Esophagus

Learning Objectives

  • Trace the mastication reflex; explain its process and significance
  • List swallowing stages; explain voluntary and involuntary stages, reflex arc, and respiratory pause
  • Explain esophageal peristalsis and the lower esophageal sphincter
  • List causes of dysphagia / failed swallowing
  • Explain achalasia and megaesophagus, including features and treatment

1. Mastication (Chewing)

Incisors cut and molars grind. All jaw muscles together can close with about 55 lb at the incisors and 200 lb at the molars. Most chewing muscles are supplied by the motor branch of cranial nerve V; brain-stem nuclei coordinate the process. Reticular taste areas, hypothalamus, amygdala, and nearby cortex can evoke chewing.

Chewing Reflex Arc

  • Food bolus in mouth → reflex inhibition of muscles of mastication
  • Jaw drops → stretch reflex in jaw muscles → rebound contraction
  • Jaw closes and compresses bolus → renewed inhibition → cycle repeats

Why Chewing Matters

Physiological BasisMechanism
Exposes nutrientsBreaks indigestible cellulose membranes around nutrient portions of many fruits and raw vegetables
Accelerates enzyme actionDigestive enzymes act on particle surfaces; smaller particles create more total surface area
Protects the gutFine particles reduce excoriation
Improves transitFine consistency makes gastric emptying and subsequent passage easier

2. Swallowing: Three Stages

StageControlKey Event
VoluntaryVoluntary initiationTongue presses upward and backward against palate, rolling food into pharynx. Thereafter swallowing is almost entirely automatic and ordinarily cannot be stopped.
PharyngealInvoluntary reflexMoves bolus through pharynx into esophagus while protecting nose and airway; completed in <2 sec
EsophagealInvoluntaryPrimary and, if required, secondary peristalsis move food to stomach

Five Automatic Events in the Pharyngeal Stage

  • Soft palate rises and closes the posterior nares, preventing nasal regurgitation
  • Palatopharyngeal folds move medially to form a sagittal slit; this selectively permits adequately chewed food
  • Vocal cords approximate; larynx moves upward and anteriorly; epiglottis swings backward. Tight vocal-cord closure is the most essential airway protection
  • Laryngeal elevation enlarges the esophageal opening; the upper 3–4 cm of esophagus (upper esophageal / pharyngoesophageal sphincter) relaxes. Between swallows it stays strongly contracted to prevent air entry
  • The pharyngeal wall contracts from superior to inferior, propelling the bolus by peristalsis into the esophagus

Involuntary Swallowing Reflex Arc

Tactile receptors around the pharyngeal opening (most sensitive at tonsillar pillars) → sensory CN V and IX → medulla near tractus solitarius → swallowing center in medulla and lower pons → motor output through CN V, IX, X, XII and a few superior cervical nerves → pharynx and upper esophagus.

The swallowing center inhibits the medullary respiratory center during the pharyngeal phase. The interruption is brief: the pharyngeal phase usually occupies <6 sec and only a fraction of a normal respiratory cycle.

3. Esophageal Stage & Peristalsis

TypeOrigin & RoleControl / Timing
Primary peristalsisContinuation of the pharyngeal peristaltic wave; normally carries food from pharynx to stomachWave takes about 8–10 sec. In an upright person, gravity may bring food to the lower esophagus in 5–8 sec.
Secondary peristalsisTriggered by esophageal distention from retained food; repeats until esophagus emptiesIntrinsic myenteric circuits plus reflexes: vagal afferents to medulla, then glossopharyngeal and vagal efferents

The pharynx and upper one-third of the esophagus contain striated muscle; the lower two-thirds contain smooth muscle. The vagus controls the lower portion through the myenteric system. After vagal section, the myenteric plexus can become sufficiently excitable within days to generate secondary waves without vagal input.

Receptive Relaxation

As an esophageal peristaltic wave approaches, a myenteric inhibitory wave relaxes the lower esophageal sphincter and also relaxes the stomach (and, less strongly, the duodenum) so they are ready to receive food.

4. Lower Esophageal Sphincter (LES)

  • A broad circular-muscle sphincter extends about 3 cm above the esophagogastric junction
  • Normally tonically constricted at about 30 mmHg, while the mid-esophagus is ~0 mmHg
  • Swallowing wave relaxes it to allow food into the stomach
  • Tonic contraction prevents reflux of acidic, proteolytic gastric secretion into an esophageal mucosa that is poorly resistant except in its lower one-third
  • A short intra-abdominal esophageal segment acts like a valve: raised abdominal pressure caves it inward and adds reflux resistance

Stomach

Learning Objectives

  • Explain gastric storage, basic electrical rhythm, pyloric pump and pyloric resistance
  • Explain gastric and duodenal factors controlling emptying, including enterogastric reflexes
  • Summarize causes and physiological basis of peptic ulcer

1. Motor Functions & Storage

The stomach stores food, mixes it with gastric secretions to form chyme, and empties chyme slowly at a rate the small intestine can digest and absorb. Physiologically the orad portion is the first two-thirds of the body; the caudad portion is the rest of the body plus the antrum.

Storage Mechanism

Incoming food forms concentric layers in the orad stomach: newest food nearest the esophageal opening and oldest near the outer wall. Stretch triggers a vagovagal reflex to the brain stem and back, reducing body-wall tone. The relaxed stomach accommodates about 0.8–1.5 L with little pressure rise until the limit is reached.

2. Basic Electrical Rhythm, Mixing, & Retropulsion

  • Weak mixing waves begin in the mid-to-upper stomach and move toward the antrum about once every 15–20 sec; they arise from spontaneous slow waves in the stomach body
  • As waves reach the antrum, they become stronger action-potential-driven constrictor rings that push contents toward the pylorus
  • Only a few milliliters or less pass with each wave because the pyloric opening is small and often contracts as the wave reaches it
  • Most antral contents are squeezed backward through the ring toward the body; this upstream mixing is retropulsion
  • Chyme is a murky semifluid or paste whose fluidity depends on food, water, secretion, and degree of mixing

3. Gastric Emptying: Pyloric Pump vs. Pyloric Resistance

ComponentMechanism
Pyloric pumpFor about 20% of the time food is present, contractions become intense from mid-stomach through caudad stomach. Tight rings can create 50–70 cm H2O pressure, about six times ordinary mixing waves, and force several milliliters into the duodenum.
Pyloric sphincterCircular muscle at pylorus is 50–100% thicker than earlier antrum and stays mildly tonically contracted. Water and fluids pass easily; food particles are held until almost fluid.
Dominant regulatorBoth stomach and duodenum send signals, but duodenal feedback is far more potent and limits delivery to the small intestine's processing capacity.

Factors Promoting vs. Inhibiting Emptying

Promotes EmptyingInhibits Emptying
Increased gastric volume stretches the wall → local myenteric reflexes increase pyloric-pump activity and inhibit the pylorusDuodenal distention or irritation, acidic chyme, abnormal osmolality, and protein or fat breakdown products trigger enterogastric inhibition
Gastrin released by antral G cells in response to stretch and especially meat-digestion products mildly/moderately stimulates body motility and enhances the pyloric pumpEnterogastric pathways inhibit pyloric-pump contractions and increase pyloric-sphincter tone
—Fat releases inhibitory hormones. CCK is most potent; secretin and GIP may also contribute

Three Routes of the Enterogastric Inhibitory Reflex

  • Directly through the enteric nervous system from duodenum to stomach
  • Through prevertebral sympathetic ganglia and inhibitory sympathetic fibers back to stomach
  • Probably to a slight extent through vagal pathways to brain stem, where normal vagal excitatory output is inhibited

Acid & Osmolality Traps

When duodenal pH falls below about 3.5–4, reflexes may block further acid delivery until neutralization. Both hypotonic and especially hypertonic fluids inhibit emptying, limiting rapid shifts in extracellular electrolyte balance.

AspectDetail
CausesOften chronic bacterial infection; gastric-barrier injury from irritants, especially excess alcohol or aspirin; autoimmunity may contribute to later gastric atrophy
SeverityMay be superficial; may penetrate deeply and cause mucosal atrophy; acute severe disease may produce ulcerative excoriation by the stomach's own peptic secretions

  • Achlorhydria: failure to secrete HCl; diagnosed when gastric pH does not fall below 6.5 after maximal stimulation
  • Hypochlorhydria: diminished acid secretion

5. Peptic Ulcer: Physiological Basis

A peptic ulcer is an excoriated area of stomach or intestinal mucosa caused mainly by digestion by gastric juice or upper-small-intestinal secretions. The commonest site is within a few centimeters of the pylorus; ulcers also occur on the lesser curvature of the antral end, the lower esophagus with reflux, and at a gastrojejunostomy (marginal ulcer).

Pathogenic SideProtective Side
Excess acid and pepsin secretionMucus from lower esophagus, stomach surface, mucous neck cells, pyloric glands, and Brunner glands
H. pylori penetrates the barrier and releases ammonium, which liquefies it and stimulates HCl secretionPancreatic bicarbonate neutralizes HCl and inactivates pepsin; Brunner-gland bicarbonate and bile bicarbonate
Smoking increases nervous stimulation; alcohol, aspirin, and other NSAIDs break the barrierDuodenal acid triggers enterogastric inhibition and secretin-mediated pancreatic bicarbonate secretion
Irritation and poor blood supply—

Two Basic Routes to Ulceration

  1. Excess acid-pepsin secretion OR
  2. Reduced ability of the gastroduodenal barrier to resist acid-pepsin digestion

At least 75% of persons with peptic ulcer have chronic H. pylori infection.

Treatment

Includes antibiotics to eradicate infectious bacteria plus acid suppression. Ranitidine blocks stimulatory histamine H2 receptors and reduces gastric acid secretion by 70–80%. Historical approaches include major gastrectomy and vagotomy.

High-Yield Additions

  • Zollinger-Ellison syndrome: Gastrinoma (often pancreatic) secretes excessive gastrin → massive acid hypersecretion → severe refractory peptic ulcers
  • Gastric acid secretion phases: Cephalic (30%, vagal), Gastric (60%, distention/gastrin), Intestinal (10%, chyme in duodenum)
  • Parietal cell stimulators: ACh (M3), gastrin (CCK-B), histamine (H2); inhibitors: somatostatin, prostaglandins, secretin, GIP

After a meal, duodenal stretch and a gastroenteric reflex increase peristalsis. Gastrin, CCK, insulin, motilin, and serotonin enhance motility; secretin and glucagon inhibit it. The individual importance of these hormones in normal control remains uncertain.

Peristaltic Rush

Severe mucosal irritation, as in some infectious diarrhea, produces powerful rapid peristalsis through autonomic/brain-stem reflexes plus enhanced intrinsic myenteric reflexes. Long-distance waves sweep irritative chyme into the colon within minutes, relieving irritation and emptying the small intestine.

1. Functions & Movements of the Colon

The proximal half of the colon mainly absorbs water and electrolytes to form solid feces; the distal half mainly stores feces. Movements are sluggish but retain mixing and propulsive capability.

MovementMechanismTiming / Purpose
Haustration (mixing)About 2.5 cm of circular muscle constricts, sometimes nearly occluding the lumen, while three teniae coli contract; unstimulated wall bulges into haustraPeaks in about 30 sec and fades over next 60 sec. Slowly digs and rolls fecal material against mucosa for absorption; only 80–200 mL feces are expelled daily
Mass movement (propulsive)A constrictive ring forms at a distended/irritated point, often transverse colon; 20+ cm distally loses haustra and contracts as a unit, moving feces en masseUsually 1–3/day, often for about 15 min in first hour after breakfast. Force builds for about 30 sec, relaxes over 2–3 min; series lasts 10–30 min
Slow haustral propulsionPersistent haustral contractions in cecum and ascending colonMay require 8–15 hr to move chyme onward while it becomes semisolid

Gastrocolic & Duodenocolic Reflexes

Distention of the stomach and duodenum after a meal facilitates colonic mass movements. These reflexes are greatly reduced or absent after removal of extrinsic autonomic nerves, so they are transmitted through the autonomic nervous system.

2. Defecation: Sphincters & Reflexes

  • The rectum is usually empty because of a weak functional sphincter about 20 cm from the anus at the sigmoid-rectal junction and a sharp angulation
  • The internal anal sphincter is thickened circular smooth muscle and is tonically contracted
  • The external anal sphincter is striated voluntary muscle, supplied by the pudendal nerve, and is usually kept tonically contracted until consciously relaxed
ReflexArc & ResponseStrength
Intrinsic myenteric defecation reflexRectal distention → afferent signals through rectal-wall myenteric plexus → peristaltic waves in descending colon, sigmoid, rectum → myenteric inhibition relaxes internal sphincter. Defecation follows if external sphincter is voluntarily relaxedRelatively weak alone
Parasympathetic defecation reflexRectal receptors → sacral spinal cord → pelvic parasympathetic nerves → descending colon, sigmoid, rectum and anus. Intensifies peristalsis and relaxes internal sphincterPowerful reinforcement; may empty bowel from splenic flexure to anus

Voluntary Assist

Defecation signals promote a deep breath, glottic closure, abdominal-wall contraction, and pelvic-floor relaxation. Voluntary relaxation of the external sphincter completes the act. Repeatedly suppressing natural reflexes makes later defecation less efficient.

4. Constipation & Hirschsprung Disease

Constipation is slow movement of feces through the large intestine. Dry hard feces in the descending colon result from excessive fluid absorption or inadequate fluid intake. Mechanical obstruction may be caused by tumors, constricting adhesions, or fecal impaction.

MechanismPhysiological Result
Habitual suppression of defecation or overuse of laxativesNatural reflexes weaken over months or years and the colon becomes atonic
Spasm of a short sigmoid segmentEven mild spasm can cause serious constipation because normal colonic motility is weak. Accumulation above the spasm may later provoke excess secretion and a day of diarrhea, producing alternating constipation and diarrhea
Hirschsprung / megacolonDeficient or absent ganglion cells in the myenteric plexus of a sigmoid segment → no strong peristalsis or defecation reflex in that segment → small spastic sigmoid → proximal fecal accumulation → megacolon of ascending, transverse, and descending colon

Megacolon Numbers

  • Severe constipation may allow bowel movement only every several days or once weekly
  • The colon may reach a diameter of about 3–4 inches

High-Yield Addition: Water Handling in the Colon

  • About 1.5 L of fluid enters the cecum daily; only ~150 mL is lost in feces
  • The colon can absorb up to 5–6 L/day if needed, but its capacity is overwhelmed in severe diarrhea
  • Na+ absorption is coupled to short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) absorption; SCFAs are produced by bacterial fermentation of fiber

1. Functions of the Liver

  • Receives portal blood from gut, spleen, and pancreas before it enters the general circulation
  • Hepatic cells temporarily take up and store roughly ½ to ¾ of water-soluble absorbed nutrients and perform intermediary metabolism
  • Secretes about 600–1000 mL of bile daily
  • Through bile acids, supports fat emulsification and absorption; bile itself contains no fat-digesting enzymes
  • Excretes bilirubin (from hemoglobin destruction) and excess cholesterol
  • Produces about 6 g bile salts/day from cholesterol

Bile-Salt Functions

  • Detergent action lowers fat-particle surface tension and emulsifies fat

Pancreas

Learning Objectives

  • Explain pancreatic function and regulation
  • List causes and pathophysiology of acute and chronic pancreatitis

1. Exocrine Pancreatic Function

Pancreatic acini secrete digestive enzymes; ductules and larger ducts secrete large volumes of sodium-bicarbonate solution. Their combined secretion enters the pancreatic duct, which usually joins the hepatic duct before opening at the papilla of Vater within the sphincter of Oddi. About 1 L/day is secreted. Insulin is secreted into blood by the islets of Langerhans.

Food TypePancreatic EnzymesAction / Activation
ProteinTrypsin, chymotrypsin, carboxypolypeptidaseTrypsin and chymotrypsin form peptides; carboxypolypeptidase releases some amino acids. Secreted as trypsinogen, chymotrypsinogen, and procarboxypolypeptidase. Enterokinase activates trypsinogen; trypsin activates itself and the other zymogens.
CarbohydratePancreatic amylaseHydrolyzes starch, glycogen, and most carbohydrates except cellulose, forming mainly disaccharides and some trisaccharides
FatPancreatic lipase, cholesterol esterase, phospholipaseLipase forms fatty acids and monoglycerides; cholesterol esterase hydrolyzes cholesterol esters; phospholipase removes fatty acids from phospholipids
Acid neutralizationHCO3− plus waterDuctal epithelium can produce up to 145 mEq/L bicarbonate, about five times plasma; creates an alkaline / neutral pH suited to pancreatic enzymes

Anti-Autodigestion Defense

Acinar cells also produce trypsin inhibitor, which prevents trypsin activation inside cells, acini, and ducts. Because trypsin activates the other proteases, blocking trypsin blocks the entire cascade.

2. Regulation & Phases of Pancreatic Secretion

StimulusOrigin / TriggerMain Secretion
AcetylcholineVagus and other cholinergic enteric nervesAcinar digestive enzymes; relatively little water/electrolytes

SecretinS cells in duodenum and jejunum; acid chyme below pH 4.5–5.0, especially HClCopious ductal water and bicarbonate with low chloride; release rises greatly as pH falls to 3.0

Stimuli potentiate one another, so combined secretion exceeds the simple sum. The cephalic phase provides about 20% of post-meal enzymes, the gastric phase another 5–10%, and the intestinal phase is dominant.

3. Pancreatitis: Causes & Mechanism

Pancreatitis may be acute or chronic. Excess alcohol is the most common cause and a gallstone blocking the papilla of Vater is second; together they account for more than 90% of cases.

Autodigestion Cascade

  • Papillary obstruction blocks the pancreatic and common bile ducts
  • Enzymes accumulate in ducts and acini
  • Trypsinogen overwhelms trypsin inhibitor
  • A little trypsin forms → trypsin activates more trypsinogen, chymotrypsinogen, and procarboxypolypeptidase
  • Vicious cycle → activated proteases digest pancreatic tissue
Feature / ConsequencePhysiological Basis
Rapid pancreatic destructionActivated proteolytic enzymes digest large portions of the gland within hours
Circulatory shock and possible deathSevere acute pancreatitis may be accompanied by circulatory shock and can be fatal
Permanent pancreatic insufficiencySevere autodigestion may permanently destroy digestive-enzyme secretion
Maldigestion / fatty fecesPancreatic failure removes proteases, amylase, lipase, and other enzymes. Up to 60% of fat and ⅓ to ½ of protein and carbohydrate may be unabsorbed.

Enterokinase (brush border) → Trypsinogen → Trypsin → activates Chymotrypsinogen, Procarboxypeptidase, Proelastase, Prophospholipase A2. This cascade is the basis of autodigestion in pancreatitis.

Vomiting Reflex

Learning Objectives

  • Describe stages of the vomiting act
  • Trace the vomiting reflex arc
  • Explain the chemoreceptor trigger zone in drug-induced vomiting and motion sickness

1. Definition, Stimuli, & Reflex Arc

Vomiting is the means by which the upper gastrointestinal tract empties itself when the upper tract is excessively irritated, overdistended, or overexcitable. Duodenal irritation or distention is especially powerful.

Vomiting Reflex Arc

Receptors mainly in pharynx, esophagus, stomach, and upper small intestine → vagal and sympathetic afferents → distributed brain-stem nuclei, especially area postrema, collectively called the vomiting center → CN V, VII, IX, X and XII to upper GIT; vagal and sympathetic efferents to lower tract; spinal nerves to diaphragm and abdominal muscles.

2. Stages of Vomiting

StageEvents
1. Antiperistaltic preludeMay begin minutes before vomiting, as far down as ileum. Reverse waves move at 2–3 cm/sec and can return much of lower-small-intestinal content to duodenum/stomach in 3–5 min.
2. Upper-GIT preparationDuodenal and gastric distention triggers strong intrinsic contractions and partial relaxation of the esophagogastric sphincter, allowing contents to enter esophagus
3. Protective opening / closureDeep breath; hyoid and larynx rise to open upper esophageal sphincter; glottis closes; soft palate rises to close posterior nares
4. PressurizationDiaphragm contracts downward while abdominal-wall muscles contract, squeezing the stomach and sharply raising intragastric pressure
5. ExpulsionLES relaxes completely. Abdominal squeeze plus stomach-wall contraction and open esophageal sphincters expel gastric contents

3. Chemoreceptor Trigger Zone (CTZ)

FeatureDetail
LocationArea postrema on lateral walls of the fourth ventricle
Drug pathwayApomorphine, morphine, and some digitalis derivatives directly stimulate CTZ → vomiting center → vomiting
Lesion effectDestruction blocks drug-triggered vomiting through this route but does not block vomiting from direct gastrointestinal irritation
Motion sickness pathwayVestibular labyrinth receptors → vestibular nuclei → cerebellum → CTZ → vomiting center

Nausea is the conscious recognition of subconscious excitation near or within the vomiting center. It may arise from gastrointestinal irritation, motion-related lower-brain impulses, or cerebral-cortical signals; vomiting can occasionally occur without nausea.

High-Yield Addition: Vomiting Consequences

  • Metabolic alkalosis from loss of HCl (hypochloremic, hypokalemic)
  • Mallory-Weiss tear from forceful vomiting (longitudinal gastric mucosal laceration)
  • Boerhaave syndrome: esophageal rupture from violent vomiting → mediastinitis
  • Anti-emetics: Ondansetron (5-HT3 antagonist), prochlorperazine (D2 antagonist), metoclopramide (D2 antagonist + prokinetic)

3. Gastrointestinal Obstruction

The GI tract can become obstructed at almost any point. Common causes include cancer, fibrotic constriction (from ulceration or peritoneal adhesions), spasm of a segment, or paralysis (paralytic ileus).

Location of ObstructionPhysiological Consequence
High (at the Pylorus)Often due to fibrotic constriction from peptic ulcers. Results in persistent vomiting of stomach contents. The excessive loss of gastric acid (H+) leads to whole-body metabolic alkalosis.
Mid (Beyond Stomach)Antiperistaltic reflux pushes small intestinal juices backward. The patient vomits neutral or basic fluid. Severe dehydration occurs, but the loss of acid and base is relatively balanced, so acid-base shifts are minimal.
Low (Distal Large Intestine)Feces accumulate in the colon, causing an intense feeling of extreme constipation. Vomiting is not severe initially but becomes severe once the colon is completely filled. Prolonged obstruction can cause rupture of the intestine or severe shock.

4. Gases in the Gastrointestinal Tract (Flatus)

Gases can enter or form in the gastrointestinal tract from three main sources: (1) swallowed air, (2) gases formed by bacterial action, or (3) gases that diffuse from blood into the gut.

  • Stomach Gas: Mostly nitrogen and oxygen from swallowed air. Usually expelled by belching.
  • Small Intestine Gas: Contains small amounts of gas, much of it passed down from the stomach.
  • Large Intestine Gas: Bacterial action generates most of the gases here, especially carbon dioxide, methane, and hydrogen.

Explosion Risk & Odor

  • When methane and hydrogen mix with oxygen, they can form an explosive mixture. Use of electric cautery during a sigmoidoscopy has been known to cause mild explosions.
  • Certain foods (beans, cabbage, cauliflower, corn) promote gas because they contain indigestible carbohydrates that serve as excellent fuel for colonic fermentative bacteria.
  • The average amount of gas formed in the colon daily is about 7 to 10 liters, but only about 0.6 liters is expelled as flatus. The remainder is absorbed into the blood and expelled through the lungs.
Fecal Odor: The actual odor of feces and flatus is caused by products of bacterial action, specifically indole, skatole, mercaptans, and hydrogen sulfide.

Digestion of Carbohydrates, Proteins, & Fats

  • Detail the specific enzymes, their origins, and sites of action for carbohydrate, protein, and fat digestion

1. Digestion by Hydrolysis

The basic process of digesting carbohydrates, fats, and proteins is hydrolysis. Digestive enzymes return hydrogen (H+) and hydroxyl (OH−) ions from water molecules to the food polymers, splitting them into monosaccharides, free fatty acids/monoglycerides, and amino acids, respectively.

2. Digestion of Carbohydrates

The diet contains mainly starches, sucrose, and lactose. Cellulose is indigestible by humans.

Location / SourceEnzymesAction & Final Products
Mouth & StomachPtyalin (salivary α-amylase)Hydrolyzes starch to maltose and small glucose polymers. Accounts for 20–40% of digestion before being blocked by stomach acid (pH < 4.0).
Small IntestinePancreatic amylasePowerful enzyme that digests the remaining 50–80% of starches into maltose and polymers within 15–30 minutes of entering the duodenum.
Brush Border (Enterocytes)Lactase, Sucrase, Maltase, α-dextrinaseLactase → galactose + glucose. Sucrase → fructose + glucose. Maltase/dextrinase → glucose. Result: 100% monosaccharides (mainly glucose).

3. Digestion of Proteins

Location / SourceEnzymesAction & Final Products
StomachPepsinActive only at highly acidic pH (2.0–3.0). Crucial for digesting collagen (connective tissue in meats). Provides 10–20% of protein digestion (yielding proteoses and peptones).
Pancreatic SecretionTrypsin, Chymotrypsin, Carboxypolypeptidase, ElastaseTrypsin/Chymotrypsin split proteins into small polypeptides. Carboxypolypeptidase cleaves individual amino acids from the ends. Elastase digests elastin fibers.
Brush Border & CytosolPeptidases (Aminopolypeptidase, dipeptidases)Microvilli peptidases split large polypeptides into di/tripeptides. Cytosolic peptidases digest these into single amino acids (>99% of absorbed proteins).

Cholesterol esters and phospholipids are digested by cholesterol ester hydrolase and phospholipase A2, respectively, and are also ferried by micelles.

Absorption: Anatomy, Water, & Electrolytes

Learning Objectives

  • Describe the anatomical basis of absorption in the small intestine
  • Explain the mechanisms of water and electrolyte absorption (Na+, Cl-, HCO3-, Ca2+)
  • Discuss the role of aldosterone in the colon

1. Anatomical Basis of Absorption

The intestines must absorb about 8 to 9 liters of fluid daily (1.5 L ingested + 7 L GI secretions). All but about 1.5 liters are absorbed in the small intestine.

The absorptive surface area is massively amplified by three structures:

  • Folds of Kerckring (Valvulae conniventes): Circular folds increasing surface area by ~3-fold.
  • Villi: Millimeter-long projections increasing surface area by ~10-fold. Contains a vascular capillary network and a central lacteal (lymphatic vessel).
  • Microvilli (Brush Border): 1000 microvilli per enterocyte increasing surface area by ~20-fold.
Total surface area amplification = 3 × 10 × 20 = ~1000-fold increase, resulting in about 250 square meters of absorptive surface (the size of a tennis court).

2. Isosmotic Absorption of Water

Water is transported entirely by diffusion/osmosis. As ions and nutrients are absorbed into the paracellular spaces and blood, an osmotic gradient is created, causing water to follow via transcellular and paracellular pathways, keeping the chyme isosmotic with plasma.

3. Absorption of Electrolytes

ElectrolyteMechanism of Absorption
Sodium (Na+)Powered by the Na+/K+ ATPase pump on the basolateral membrane. This drives intracellular Na+ down, creating a steep gradient that pulls Na+ from the lumen across the apical membrane, often co-transporting glucose and amino acids.
Aldosterone EffectIn dehydration, aldosterone highly upregulates Na+ absorption (and consequently water), especially in the colon, preventing salt loss in feces.
Chloride (Cl−)Follows Na+ passively down the electrical gradient. In the ileum and colon, it is also absorbed in direct exchange for secreted bicarbonate.
Bicarbonate (HCO3−)In the upper SI, secreted H+ combines with HCO3− (from pancreatic/biliary juices) to form H2CO3 → H2O + CO2. The CO2 is absorbed into blood and exhaled. In the colon, HCO3− is actively secreted to neutralize bacterial acids.
Calcium & IronCa2+ is actively absorbed in the duodenum, precisely regulated by Parathyroid Hormone (PTH) and Vitamin D. Iron is absorbed based on body needs for hemoglobin.

Extreme Secretion in Cholera

Cholera toxin stimulates massive cAMP formation, which opens vast numbers of apical chloride channels. Cl− floods the lumen, pulling Na+ and massive amounts of water by osmosis, producing lethal secretory diarrhea.

1. Absorption of Carbohydrates & Proteins

NutrientTransport Mechanism
Glucose & GalactoseAbsorbed via secondary active transport (co-transport) with Sodium. The Na+ gradient literally "drags" glucose into the cell.
FructoseAbsorbed via facilitated diffusion (independent of Na+). The rate is only about half that of glucose. Once inside, much of it is converted to glucose.
Amino Acids / PeptidesAlmost all are absorbed via Na+ co-transport. A few are absorbed via facilitated diffusion.

2. Absorption of Fats

  • Micelles carry monoglycerides and free fatty acids into the microvilli clefts.
  • Because lipids are soluble in the cell membrane, they diffuse directly into the enterocyte. The empty micelles remain in the lumen to ferry more fat.
  • Inside the cell's smooth endoplasmic reticulum, they are reconstituted into new triglycerides.
  • These triglycerides are packaged into chylomicrons and exocytosed into the interstitial fluid, entering the central lacteal (lymphatic system), eventually draining into the blood via the thoracic duct.
  • Short- and medium-chain fatty acids: Being more water-soluble, they bypass the ER and absorb directly into the portal blood (e.g., butterfat).

3. Absorption in the Large Intestine

About 1500 mL of chyme enters the large intestine daily. The colon typically absorbs most of this, leaving less than 100 mL of fluid to be excreted.

  • Absorbing Colon: Proximal half of the colon. Highly efficient at active Na+ absorption against a steep gradient (enhanced by aldosterone).
  • Storage Colon: Distal half, primarily stores feces until defecation.
  • Maximum Capacity: The colon can absorb a maximum of 5 to 8 liters of fluid daily. Excess manifests as diarrhea.

4. Composition and Characteristics of Feces

FeatureDescription
Composition~¾ water and ¼ solid matter. Solid matter = 30% dead bacteria, 10–20% fat, 10–20% inorganic matter, 2–3% protein, 30% roughage/slough.
ColorBrown color is caused by stercobilin and urobilin, derivatives of bilirubin formed by colon bacteria.
OdorCaused by bacterial products: indole, skatole, mercaptans, and hydrogen sulfide (H2S).
